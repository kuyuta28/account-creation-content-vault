### CHUNK audio-03-01

On the morning of the eighth of November, the fifth day of the survey, you meet the proto da marangon at the entrance to the first covered galley shed, as arranged. The proto da marangon — the master ship carpenter — is a man named Nicolò Falier, who has held the position since fourteen eighty-four, when his predecessor retired and the appointment was confirmed by the Collegio on the fourteenth of September of that year. He carries, on a cord at his belt, a wooden mallet and a small blade of the kind used to probe for rot in the planking of hulls, and he carries under his arm a working ledger of his own that is separate from the Arsenal's central register and that he maintains as a running record of his assessments of each hull, updated whenever he inspects them, which he does on an irregular schedule as time and the demands of active construction work permit. Your appointed task, on days five through eight, is to survey all thirty-eight hulls stored in the covered sheds — the squeri — and to record for each: its name, its type, its original construction year, its most recent refit date, its current condition grade, and, for those graded da raddobbo or da disarmare, the proto's current estimate of either the required repair cost in ducati or, for condemned hulls, the estimated salvage value of the material to be recovered. You will work alongside Nicolò Falier, who provides the technical assessments, and you will record his judgements, cross-referenced against entries in the spring register, in the winter register's hull inventory section. The thirty-eight hulls are distributed across nine covered sheds, with between two and five hulls per shed depending on the size class of the vessels.

Shed one contains three hulls. The first is the Serenissima, a galea sottile, built in fourteen seventy-nine, most recently refitted in fourteen eighty-seven, graded navigabile in the spring register. Nicolò Falier walks the keel line, probing the strake planking at intervals with his blade, testing the knee joints at the bow, pressing with his thumb on the seating of the forward rowing bench. He produces no audible expression of concern. The hull is graded navigabile, confirming the spring assessment. No repair work required at present, though Falier notes that the forward caulking between the second and third strakes on the port side has dried since the refit and may need attention before the next active deployment; this is noted in the register as a minor maintenance observation, not a condition change. The second hull in shed one is the Santa Chiara, a galea sottile of the same class, built in fourteen eighty-one, last refitted in fourteen eighty-eight. The spring register grades it as da raddobbo, citing a longitudinal crack in the keel, visible under the central rowing bench structure, that was discovered during the spring survey. Falier examines the keel crack: it has progressed. The progression is described as approximately three inches further along the keel line from the forward point of the crack as measured in spring, and the crack has widened slightly at its mid-point. His estimate for repair — which in the spring was recorded as fifty-two ducati for the keel repair plus caulking — is now revised upward to seventy-eight ducati, pending a more detailed assessment of whether the outer planking on either side of the keel section also requires replacement. You record the revised estimate and note the condition change: the crack has progressed since the spring survey, confirming da raddobbo grade, repair estimate increased. The third hull in shed one is the Madonna della Salute, a fusta, shorter than the galleys, built in fourteen eighty-five, listed in the spring register as navigabile. Falier spends less than four minutes on this hull. He grades it navigabile.

The method of working through a shed is established by the second shed and remains consistent for the following seven: you proceed from the hull nearest the water gate at the shed's far end to the hull nearest the landward entrance, taking each in sequence, Falier conducting his inspection while you read the spring register entry aloud for the relevant hull, so that the spring grading is known to him before he finishes and commits to a current grade. This procedure was not explicitly specified in your instructions, but Falier proposed it at the beginning of the second shed, and it has the advantage of efficiency. Shed two contains four hulls. The first is the San Marco, a large galley of the transport class — a galea grossa — built in fourteen seventy-one, which is to say nineteen years ago. The San Marco has been listed in the Arsenal register as da raddobbo since the winter survey of fourteen eighty-three. That is seven years of continuous da raddobbo grading. In those seven years, no repair work has been commissioned on this hull. The repair estimate for the San Marco, as it appears in the winter register of fourteen eighty-three, was two hundred and ninety-five ducati, for a keel replacement, the replacement of three strakes on the starboard quarter, and recaulking throughout. By the spring survey of fourteen ninety, the estimate had risen to six hundred and forty ducati, the intervening years of unmaintained storage having allowed the strake damage to spread and the deck framing to develop its own problems. Falier examines the San Marco for approximately twenty minutes — longer than any other hull in the first two sheds. He probes the keel at five points. He removes a section of the inner ceiling planking with his mallet to look at the frame beneath. He stands in the bow and looks aft. When he returns to where you are standing, he gives an estimate of seven hundred and forty ducati, which he qualifies as a minimum figure, because the frame condition he has just examined in the bow suggests the possibility of further frame damage through the midship section that would not be accessible without partial dismantling. You record seven hundred and forty ducati, minimum, with Falier's qualification noted.

The remaining three hulls in shed two — the Grazia di Dio, a galea sottile of fourteen eighty-seven, graded navigabile; the Santa Maria Formosa, a galea sottile of fourteen eighty-two, graded da raddobbo for planking damage on the port quarter; and the Fortuna, a fusta of fourteen eighty-nine, graded navigabile — are assessed and recorded without significant deviation from the spring register. Shed three occupies the remainder of the fifth day. It contains five hulls: three galee sottili and two fuste. The Corpus Domini, one of the galee sottili, is in this shed and is graded da disarmare for the first time in this inventory — having been listed as da raddobbo in both the spring survey of fourteen ninety and the winter survey of fourteen eighty-nine. Falier's assessment: the keel is failed, the damage is beyond economic repair, the hull has no material future as a vessel. The Corpus Domini is therefore the first of the thirteen hulls that will be graded da disarmare in this winter survey, a number that will become clear only when the full survey is complete, but that begins here in shed three. The grading da disarmare carries, by the Arsenal regulations, an administrative implication that requires a separate action: the hull must be scheduled for dismantling, and its recoverable material — timber in reusable lengths, bronze and iron fittings, lead sheathing where present — must be assessed for salvage value, because the question of whether condemned hulls remain on the asset register at salvage value or are struck from it entirely is currently under review. You record the da disarmare grading and note the ongoing status of the accounting question, as it appears in the instructions: see Provveditori inquiry ref. fourteen eighty-eight, outcome pending.

Days six and seven, the ninth and tenth of November, are devoted entirely to the galley sheds, working through sheds four through eight. The pattern of the survey continues as established: Falier inspects, you record. The findings for these three days follow a pattern that has become familiar. Most of the hulls surveyed hold their spring gradings without change — seven are confirmed navigabile, their condition stable. Among the hulls graded da raddobbo, the trend is toward higher repair costs: four show estimates that have risen since spring, the increases ranging from twelve to one hundred and fourteen ducati, the wood continuing to deteriorate in storage as wood does. Two others in the da raddobbo category hold steady or have slightly improved, one of them having received partial repair work since spring at a recorded cost of thirty-one ducati, enough to stabilise a condition that had previously been worsening. Four more hulls are graded da disarmare, joining the Corpus Domini already condemned on day five. One of the four new da disarmare hulls, the Pietà, a galea sottile of fourteen seventy-three, raises a further question about the written record: the Pietà's name, as it appears on the painted identification on the hull's bow, is in a form that has been altered — the original painted letters have been half-covered by a second application of paint, and the currently visible name, Pietà, appears in different letterforms from the first application. The name visible under the new application, partially legible where the newer paint has crept away from the edge, appears to read Beatrice. The spring register lists no hull named Beatrice. The spring register lists a hull named Pietà, in this shed, in this position, graded da raddobbo. Whether the hull named Pietà in the current spring register is the same physical hull that bore the name Beatrice at some earlier date, and whether the renaming was carried out under any formal procedure that generated a record, cannot be determined here. You note the observation: hull name appears to have been altered, no renaming record found in the documentation. Falier says he is not aware of any renaming but does not claim certainty.

Two hulls in shed seven warrant individual attention beyond the standard recording. The first is the Buona Fortuna, a galea grossa completed in fourteen sixty-eight, which makes it the oldest hull currently in storage in the Arsenal at twenty-two years of age. The Buona Fortuna is graded da disarmare, and has carried that grade, according to back-checking the relevant registers, since at least the spring survey of fourteen eighty-one — nearly a full decade without being dismantled or removed from the register. The spring register of fourteen ninety records a salvage value estimate of ninety-one ducati for the reusable timber and fittings of the Buona Fortuna. Falier's current assessment is considerably lower, at sixty-four ducati, a reduction of roughly a third, because in the years since the da disarmare grade was first consistently applied, some of the structural timber has degraded past the point of reuse and is now suitable only for burning. The reduction in estimated salvage value, from ninety-one to sixty-four ducati, is noted in the register along with the extended period of unexecuted disarmament, which is itself caused, according to Falier, by the same administrative uncertainty about the accounting of condemned hulls that affects all thirteen da disarmare hulls in this survey: while the question of how they are to be classed in the asset register is unresolved, the decision to proceed with the dismantling, which would fix the salvage value as actual recovered material and remove the hull from the register entirely, has not been made. The instruction from the Provveditori's inquiry of fourteen eighty-eight, which is the document creating the ongoing review, does not authorize the continuation of the existing practice, nor does it prohibit it, but rather suspends the question pending a ruling that, as of the fourth of November fourteen ninety, has not been issued.

The second hull requiring additional attention in shed seven is the Pellegrino, a galea sottile completed in fourteen eighty-three, graded da raddobbo in the current spring register for a reason that is described in that register as "damage sustained during removal from the water, fourteenth of March, fourteen ninety." The damage described is a fractured knee at the bow, caused when the hull, being hauled on the boat-hauling equipment from the water into the shed, slipped on the hauling ramp and struck the ramp's edge. The spring register records an estimate of thirty-four ducati for the repair of the fractured knee. Falier examines the hull and confirms the fracture. He also identifies, on the port side amidships, a section of planking that was not noted in the spring register and that shows signs of earlier damage — a repair patch, applied with bolts and lead sheeting, of the kind made when a plank has been stove in or cracked in service and repaired at sea or at an outstation without formal registry in the Arsenal's records. The patch is old — Falier judges it to be at least four years old, possibly more — and the repair is described by him as competent, the patch having held without subsequent opening. He grades it as a legacy repair that poses no current structural risk but should be noted as a deviation from the build record. You record the patch, its approximate location, Falier's assessment of its age and condition, and the absence of any corresponding entry in the hull's historical record as held in the Arsenal's files. The total estimate for the Pellegrino is now thirty-four ducati for the knee repair, with the legacy patch noted as a finding.

Shed nine, the final shed, is surveyed on the eighth day of the overall survey, the eleventh of November. Shed nine contains four hulls: two navigabile, one da raddobbo, and one da disarmare. The da disarmare hull in shed nine is the thirteenth and final condemned hull in the winter survey, which brings the total for the da disarmare category to thirteen, as follows: seven hulls carrying the grade from previous surveys, now confirmed; six hulls newly graded in this survey, having been da raddobbo in the spring. Of the seven previously condemned hulls, the Buona Fortuna has been da disarmare since at least fourteen eighty-one; the Corpus Domini was graded da raddobbo as recently as last spring. When the galley shed assessment is complete — late morning, eleventh of November, the cold now persistent through the day so that Falier wears his winter cloak throughout the shed inspections — the full count is recorded in the register. Of the thirty-eight hulls surveyed, seven are graded navigabile, ready for deployment; eighteen are graded da raddobbo, requiring repair; and thirteen are graded da disarmare, condemned. The repair costs for the eighteen da raddobbo hulls, summed across all entries, come to six thousand seven hundred and forty-three ducati, a figure dominated by the San Marco in shed two, whose estimate of seven hundred and forty ducati alone accounts for more than a tenth of the total. The thirteen condemned hulls carry a combined estimated salvage value of three hundred and nineteen ducati, a figure that will decrease over time as the unexecuted disarmament continues. You close the galley shed section of the register. The outstanding administrative matter — the accounting treatment of the thirteen condemned hulls, pending the Provveditori inquiry of fourteen eighty-eight — is noted again at the foot of the section, as it was noted in the section header, and as it will be noted again in the final certification. Tomorrow is the oar inventory.

### CHUNK audio-03-09

The grading distinctions applied to the thirty-eight surveyed hulls are not always as clean as the three categories suggest, and there are cases during the shed assessment where the practical boundary between da raddobbo and da disarmare is a matter of some discussion between yourself and Nicolò Falier. The rule, as Falier explains it, referring to a provision that appears in the proto's manual posted on the wall of his workroom — a document he has had occasion to consult — is that a hull is da raddobbo if the total estimated repair cost does not exceed one-third of the estimated replacement cost of a new hull of the same class, and da disarmare if it does. For a standard galea sottile, Falier estimates that a complete new hull would cost approximately one thousand and eight hundred ducati, which means the repair cost would need to stay below roughly six hundred ducati to remain in the da raddobbo category. The San Marco's repair estimate of seven hundred and forty ducati exceeds that threshold, which by strict application of the rule would make it da disarmare. Falier's position, which he states without particular force, is that the San Marco is a galea grossa, not a galea sottile, and that a new hull of the galea grossa class would cost between three thousand and three thousand five hundred ducati — nearly double the smaller class — making the relevant threshold far higher and placing the seven hundred and forty ducati estimate comfortably within the da raddobbo range. His reasoning appears sound. The grading question is noted in the register with the San Marco entry, with a reference to the applicable provision and a record of Falier's reasoning. You do not alter the grade he has assigned; your role in this section is to record the assessments, not to override the proto. The note ensures that if the Provveditori wish to re-examine the grading, the basis for the current assignment is visible in the record.

The condition of the condemned hulls — the thirteen graded da disarmare — presents a separate administrative difficulty that is distinct from the accounting question already raised. The accounting question concerns the treatment of their value in the treasury's records. A separate matter concerns the physical occupation of shed space. The thirteen condemned hulls occupy sheds six, seven, and portions of eight and nine. Shed six is entirely occupied by condemned material — five hulls in various states of deterioration, the most advanced being the Buona Fortuna of fourteen sixty-eight, which has been da disarmare since fourteen eighty-one and which Falier declines to enter at all, the floor planking of that particular hull having rotted through at the bow to such a degree that his weight on the hull's interior deck boards might, in his assessment, cause a plank failure. He examines it from the exterior only. The five hulls in shed six together carry a combined estimated salvage value of sixty-eight ducati. The greater part of that value, thirty-eight ducati, lies in the iron fittings — the anchors, chain, rings, and fastenings that retain their worth regardless of how far the timber surrounding them has rotted. The recoverable timber accounts for the remaining thirty ducati. Across sheds seven through nine, the other eight condemned hulls vary widely in what they might yield: the smallest, a fusta too narrow to produce useful timber lengths, is valued at nine ducati, while a retired galea sottile built with Dalmatian oak frames and relatively complete iron fittings could return as much as forty-one. The aggregated total across all thirteen condemned hulls comes to three hundred and nineteen ducati, stated in the section as an estimate rather than a fixed valuation; the instructions note that actual salvage values realized in any eventual condemnation and sale may vary from the estimates, and that the register's figures represent the proto's best current assessment given the hulls' condition at the time of the survey.
