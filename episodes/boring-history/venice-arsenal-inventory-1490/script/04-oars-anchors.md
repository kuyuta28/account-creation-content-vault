On the twelfth of November, the ninth day of the survey, you are assigned to the oar stores. The oar stores are located in the Arsenale Nuovo, in two large shed structures running parallel to the central canal of that section. The sheds are designated simply as the Nuovo oar shed and the Vecchio oar shed, names established by administrative convention and not by the physical location of the structures, since both are in the Nuovo section, the Vecchio shed having been relocated from the original section sometime in the mid-fourteenth century, according to a note in the Arsenal's physical survey of fourteen seventy-three which you read on the morning of the ninth day while preparing your cahier. The combined oar inventory of both sheds, as recorded in the spring register, is twenty-two thousand four hundred and eleven oars, a figure which, when you first read it from the spring register, requires a moment to place in proportion. Twenty-two thousand four hundred and eleven is the figure, distributed across racks numbered one through twenty-three in the Nuovo shed and racks twenty-four through thirty-one in the Vecchio shed. Each rack holds oars of a single length, the length being the primary sorting variable and the secondary variable being wood species — fir and beech are the two species in use for standard oars, with fir being the preference for active galleys and beech held as the deeper reserve stock, beech being heavier but more durable in storage. The oar count is conducted rack by rack using a tally method: each oar as counted is marked in a column in working notes and the column total transferred to the register at the end of each rack. You have one assistant for this day, a younger scrivan named Marco, whose last name you do not note in the register, assigned from the scribes' room for the counting task on the recommendation of the head of that room, on the basis that the physical count of oars benefits from two people.

Racks one through eight are surveyed in the first half of the ninth day, without significant deviation from the spring register. The oars in these racks are the shortest length class — ten Venetian feet, used on the fuste and the smaller galleys — and are all fir. The spring register records three thousand eight hundred and forty-two oars across these eight racks. Your two-person count, conducted by you and Marco moving along opposite sides of each rack and comparing figures at the end of each section, yields three thousand eight hundred and thirty-nine — three fewer than the spring register. The discrepancy of three could be accounted for by oars removed for use on active vessels since the spring, withdrawn without a corresponding update to the rack register, which is the working document maintained by the shed foreman, a man named Zuan Battista who follows the count at a distance and who, when the discrepancy is noted to him, says that he believes three short-length oars were issued to the maintenance crew attached to the Nuovissimo gate in September for the repair of a dock structure that required the use of oar-length timbers as bracing material. He does not produce a written record of this issue, because, he says, the issue was informal and was approved verbally by the Arsenal's building supervisor, and the oars were expected to be returned once the bracing was no longer needed, but they have not been returned. The status of the three missing short oars is therefore: either still in the Nuovissimo gate structure, or consumed, or elsewhere without record. You note this as you have noted the missing rigging: circumstances not determined, three units absent, no written issue record found. Racks nine through sixteen are surveyed in the afternoon. All eight racks of the medium-length class — twelve Venetian feet, the standard galea sottile working oar — match the spring register to within one or two units per rack, the minor differences attributable to counting imprecision rather than to any systematic removal.

Rack seventeen is reached in the late afternoon of the ninth day. Rack seventeen holds oars of the fourteen-foot length, which is a longer class used on the heavier galleys and the galeazze when the standard twelve-foot oar does not provide sufficient leverage for the extended outrigger arrangement. The spring register records that rack seventeen contains one thousand two hundred oars of the fourteen-foot length, all fir, and that these oars bear a stamp on the blade — a small mark burned into the wood with an iron of a design specific to the Arsenal at Famagusta, the Cypriot northern port — which distinguishes them from the Arsenal's own stock and which is the reason they are held in a separate rack rather than integrated with the non-stamped fourteen-foot oars in rack sixteen. When you count the oars in rack seventeen, you count zero. The rack is empty. The pegs on which the bundles would be stacked are present, the pegs in the wall are present, the identification board at the end of the rack is present and reads, in the hand of Zuan Battista, "Venice — Famagusta stamp — 14 foot — fir — 1200 units." The oars recorded as in that rack are not there. Zuan Battista is asked. He says he is aware that the rack is empty. He says the oars were not removed during his tenure, or were not removed by anyone he authorized. He does not know when they left the rack. You ask whether there is any documentation covering the movement of oars out of rack seventeen. Zuan Battista identifies a file kept in the shed foreman's office, in a wooden box, containing documents relating to deliveries and withdrawals since approximately fourteen eighty-three. He produces the box. You search the documents. You find, in the section labeled fourteen eighty-seven, a copy of a Council of Ten authorization, dated the fourth of June fourteen eighty-seven, for the temporary transfer of one thousand two hundred fourteen-foot fir oars from the Arsenal's rack seventeen to the garrison at Famagusta, in Cyprus, to be delivered via the Arsenal's supply vessel and to be returned within eighteen months or compensated at the rate of three soldi per oar, approximately one hundred and eighty ducati in total.

The Council of Ten authorization, being a copy, not the original, reads as follows in the relevant operative clause: one thousand two hundred oars, fourteen Venetian feet, fir, marked with the Arsenal da mar di Famagosta stamp, to be dispatched from Venice to the garrison of the Republic at Famagusta, in the island of Cyprus, on a temporary basis, for a period not to exceed eighteen months from the date of delivery. At the conclusion of that period the oars are to be returned to the Arsenal in Venice or the recipient is to pay compensation to the Arsenal at the rate of three soldi per oar. The authorization is signed and bears the Counter-signature of two members of the Council; the copy appears to have been made by the Arsenal's own scribes' room at the time of the dispatch. The delivery is recorded in the shed foreman's box by a second document: a receipt from the supply vessel captain, dated the nineteenth of June fourteen eighty-seven, confirming that one thousand two hundred oars were loaded and dispatched. The eighteen-month period specified in the authorization would have elapsed on approximately the nineteenth of December fourteen eighty-eight, which is now approximately two years ago. No subsequent documentation in Zuan Battista's box covers the return of the oars or the receipt of compensation from the Famagusta garrison. You are aware, from general knowledge, that the garrison at Famagusta no longer exists in the form that would have enabled it to act on the return clause: Cyprus was ceded following negotiations concluded in fourteen eighty-nine, and the Venetian administration of the island has been fully wound down. This knowledge is not documented in the shed's files. You note what can be noted from the documents: the oars were dispatched under proper authorization in June fourteen eighty-seven, the return deadline passed in December fourteen eighty-eight, no return or compensation received as documented in the files available, current status of the recipient garrison unknown from these files.

The complication introduced by rack seventeen is an accounting one of a specific type. The spring register records one thousand two hundred oars in rack seventeen, under the heading "on loan — Famagusta — Council of Ten authorization ref. June fourteen eighty-seven." That entry means the spring survey treated the oars as assets of the Arsenal, currently held off-site on a temporary basis, to be returned or compensated — which is the correct accounting treatment when an outgoing loan is within its term or has a realistic expectation of return. The loan's return deadline has, as noted, elapsed nineteen months ago. The spring scribe chose to retain the "on loan" accounting treatment, noting the elapsed deadline in a marginal annotation and adding that a written query had been submitted to the Collegio regarding the status of the authorization, but that no reply had been received as of the date of the spring survey. You look for the spring register's marginal annotation. It is present. You look in the box of documents for the written query to the Collegio. There is a copy of a query, dated the eighteenth of March fourteen ninety, signed by the scribe Bartolomeo Sanudo, transmitted via the Arsenal's administrative office to the Collegio, requesting a ruling on the accounting treatment of the Famagusta oars. The box does not contain a reply. You ask whether a reply was received elsewhere, one not kept in this box. Zuan Battista does not know. You search your own documentation for any Collegio ruling on this matter. There is none. The oars remain, for the purpose of this inventory, in the same ambiguous state in which the spring survey left them: listed as assets, currently unlocated, loan overdue, query submitted and unanswered. You record them in the winter register as the spring register recorded them, with the additional notation that the query of March fourteen ninety remained unanswered as of the date of this survey.

The oar survey continues through racks eighteen to twenty-three in the Nuovo shed, and then through racks twenty-four to thirty-one in the Vecchio oar shed, on the tenth day, the thirteenth of November, concluding by midday. The total physical count across all racks, excluding rack seventeen, is twenty-one thousand two hundred and eleven oars, which, added to the one thousand two hundred recorded as on loan from rack seventeen, gives a register total of twenty-two thousand four hundred and eleven, matching the spring register total precisely. The count has surface consistency. The physical discrepancies are minor: four racks with counts one or two units below the registered figures, two racks with counts one unit above, all within the range attributable to counting imprecision. The only substantive finding is rack seventeen, which has been recorded and noted extensively. After the oar survey, on the afternoon of the tenth day, you move to the anchor inventory, which occupies the northeast section of the Nuovo and is the final outdoor inventory before you return to covered spaces for the armoury. The anchors are stored on cradles of wooden beams, arranged in rows on a stone platform that is partially roofed by a simple lean-to structure, partially open to the sky. The anchors are classified in the inventory register by type and by weight class. The lightest are the kedge anchors, used for setting temporary holding positions, which range from one hundred up to three hundred libbre veneziane. The bower anchors, the main working anchors, are heavier, weighing from three hundred up to nine hundred libbre veneziane. The heaviest class is the sheet anchor, reserved for the largest vessels, each weighing above nine hundred libbre. The spring register records forty-three kedge anchors, seventeen bower anchors, and four sheet anchors in the main stores, plus the separate entry for the Bucintoro.

The anchor count is completed on the afternoon of the tenth day. The kedge anchors number forty-three, matching the spring register. The bower anchors number seventeen, also matching. The four sheet anchors are present in the main stores, and you examine each one, recording their catalogue numbers, which are stamped into the anchoring ring of each piece; all four numbers appear in the spring register and the numbers match. There is a fifth anchor on the cradle system — partly concealed behind the largest of the four sheet anchors, without a tag — that was not recorded in the spring register. Zuan Battista, who has accompanied you to the anchor stores, identifies this piece, after examination, as an old bower anchor that has been out of active classification since at least fourteen eighty and that has remained in this location because no instruction to remove it has been issued. The unregistered bower anchor is an iron piece in poor condition: the ring is cracked, the palm of one fluke has separated from the arm at the weld. It has no usable value as an anchor. Its scrap value in iron is minimal. Zuan Battista estimates one ducat, perhaps less. You record it as found: one unregistered bower anchor, old, condition failed, no current catalogue number, no registration record found, estimated scrap value one ducat. The Bucintoro's anchors are recorded in a separate sub-register that the arsenal maintains for the state ceremonial galley, which is housed in its own dedicated shed at the western end of the Vecchio section. The Bucintoro sub-register records two anchors: a small bower of two hundred and forty libbre veneziane and a sheet anchor of one thousand and twelve libbre veneziane, the largest single anchor in current Arsenal stock. Both are present. Both are in the condition recorded in the sub-register. The anchor inventory is complete.

### CHUNK audio-04-08
<!-- img: IMG-04-08 -->

The anchor inventory raises a question of classification that is worth recording. The distinction between a kedge anchor and a bower anchor — the two main operative classes — is not purely dimensional; it also reflects use, a kedge being deployed for temporary mooring or to help lever a vessel off a shoal in a manoeuvre called kedging, and a bower being the standard working anchor that rides in the bow hawse of a deployed galley. A single anchor's physical form can serve either function depending on the vessel to which it is attached, which means that the class recorded in the register reflects the anchor's assignment at the time of its cataloguing, not any permanent intrinsic quality of the piece. Zuan Battista notes that three of the registered kedge anchors are, by weight, in the range that would qualify them as light bowers and were reclassified from bow to kedge status in fourteen eighty-three when they were removed from a set of heavy galleys that were subsequently refitted with larger bower anchors. The reassignment is confirmed by a note in Zuan Battista's own records from fourteen eighty-three. You record this as additional information in the anchor section of the register, because it bears on the accurate interpretation of the category totals: formally, forty-three kedge anchors; informally, forty of the forty-three are original kedge classifications, and three were formerly light bowers reclassified in fourteen eighty-three. The total anchor count in the main stores, including the unregistered damaged bower already recorded, is sixty-five pieces. Of these, sixty-four are registered in the central inventory and one is not. The aggregate weight of all registered anchor stock is not calculated in the present survey, the instructions not requiring it; Zuan Battista says the weight calculation was last performed formally in the spring survey of fourteen eighty-two and is contained in that register, which you do not have access to at the shed. The total weight figure from fourteen eighty-two, if still considered applicable, was expressed as approximately fourteen thousand five hundred libbre veneziane for all registered bow and kedge anchors, not including sheet anchors. The four sheet anchors contribute an additional estimated four thousand three hundred libbre veneziane, bringing the total approximate weight of the stored anchor stock to eighteen thousand eight hundred libbre veneziane — a figure that is noted in the register as carried forward from the fourteen eighty-two survey and not independently verified today.

The practical logistics of counting twenty-two thousand four hundred and eleven oars across thirty-one racks over two days require a system, and the system you and Marco use, on the recommendation of Zuan Battista who has participated in previous inventory counts, is as follows. Each rack is approached from the same end — the western end for all racks, without exception — and the oars are counted from the closest row to the furthest, moving along the rack in numerical order from left to right. One counter, you, moves along the left side; the second counter, Marco, moves along the right side. Each counter calls numbers aloud in groups of ten, and the other counter acknowledges each group of ten with a mark in the working notes. At the end of each group of one hundred, both counters compare their intermediate totals. If the totals agree, the count proceeds; if they disagree by more than one, the section is recounted from the start of the last group of ten. This method was not specified in the instructions — the instructions merely say that oars are to be counted by rack and totals recorded — but Zuan Battista says it was established by the head scribe of a previous inventory, approximately eight years ago, and has been used since, with the result that the final count has in recent surveys been more reliable than in earlier ones, where discrepancies of twenty or thirty oars across the total were not unusual and were attributed to counting fatigue over many hours of repetitive enumeration. Over the two days, the method proves its worth. On the ninth day, the count across racks one through fifteen in the Nuovo shed falls short of the spring register by two oars — a discrepancy well within the expected range for this volume of counting. On the tenth day, racks sixteen through thirty-one produce a count that runs two oars over the register, as if the difference has migrated from one half of the inventory to the other. Whether two oars have been physically moved between rack sections or whether the two discrepancies are independent counting errors that happen to cancel, the evidence does not determine. What matters for the register is the net result: across all thirty racks that contain oars, the total physical count matches the spring register precisely. Rack seventeen remains empty, as previously recorded. You close the oar inventory section of the register and report both the rack-level discrepancies and the net total to the head of the scribes' room in the working summary delivered at the end of each day.
