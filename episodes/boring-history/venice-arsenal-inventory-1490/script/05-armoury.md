### CHUNK audio-05-01

The eleventh and final day of the survey, the fourteenth of November, is allocated to the armoury, which is the last and largest of the covered inventory sections and which the instructions place at the end of the survey schedule on the grounds that it requires more time per linear foot of storage than the rope and ordnance sections because the items are smaller, more numerous, and require individual counting rather than estimation. The armoury occupies a stone building in the Vecchio section, adjacent to the wall of the first internal courtyard, entered through an iron-bound wooden door for which the key is held by the Arsenal's armoury keeper, a man named Pietro Zane, who has held the position since fourteen seventy-nine and who appears at the armoury door at the appointed hour with his own ledger — the third internal ledger, after the Tana's and the timber yard's, that you will need to cross-reference against the central inventory register during this survey. The armoury is a single large room, approximately forty passi in length and eighteen in width, with stone walls thick enough to provide a notable reduction in sound from the courtyard outside. The ceiling is barrel-vaulted, like the cannon hall, but lower. The inventory here is of personal arms and defensive equipment: crossbows, bolts, shields, plate armour, mail coats, helmets, and miscellaneous personal weapons. The spring register divides the inventory into eight categories, each with its own running sequence of catalogue numbers, and the armoury's own ledger maintained by Pietro Zane mirrors this division. You begin with the crossbows, which are racked along the west wall.

The crossbow inventory is divided by draw weight into three classes: light, for use by personnel of lesser physical strength or for the smaller vessels where the heavier bows create problems in the confined rowing benches; medium, the standard military crossbow for the galley infantry; and heavy, the large siege and tower crossbow intended for use from fixed positions on larger vessels or fortifications. The spring register records the crossbow inventory across three draw-weight classes: two hundred and nine of the light specification, four hundred and seventeen of the medium, and eighty-three of the heavy, bringing the combined total to seven hundred and nine. Pietro Zane's armoury ledger records the same numbers. Your count of the light crossbows, taken from the rack in numbered order, yields two hundred and eight — one fewer than the register. You search the vicinity of the rack for an unracked bow; there is none visible. Pietro Zane says he is aware of a light crossbow that was taken from the rack for inspection by an Arsenal official approximately six weeks ago and that has not been returned to the rack. He has a note in his ledger to this effect. The note gives the name of the official and a date; the official is identified as a member of the Provveditori's inspection staff and the date is the second of October. The bow is presumably still with that individual, or in the Provveditori's office, or returned to the rack and miscounted. You note the one-unit shortfall with the explanation from Zane's ledger. The medium crossbows: four hundred and seventeen present, matching. The heavy crossbows: eighty-three present, matching. The crossbow total is seven hundred and eight physical pieces against seven hundred and nine registered, the difference accounted for by the noted removal. The bolt inventory follows: bolts are stored in bundles of one hundred, in wooden bins under the crossbow racks, sorted by calibre to match the three bow classes. The spring register records two hundred and thirty-one bundles total. You count two hundred and thirty bundles: one bundle short. Pietro Zane's ledger does not contain a note about any bolt withdrawal. The missing bundle is noted as unexplained.

The pavises — the large rectangular shields, approximately four feet by two feet, used by crossbowmen to create a protected firing position on the exposed deck of a galley — stand in two rows along the north wall, stacked four deep. They are typically made of poplar faced with canvas, painted, and the standard Arsenal pavise has the lion of Saint Mark on the obverse, though several of the examples you examine have been repainted and the original decoration is obscured under a ground coat of white. The spring register records one hundred and forty-six pavises. You count the stacks: thirty-seven groups of four gives one hundred and forty-eight, but two of the groups are only three deep, reducing the total by two, which yields one hundred and forty-six. This matches the spring register. Of the one hundred and forty-six, Pietro Zane's ledger notes seventeen as requiring repair — canvas separation from the wood, splintering, damage to the carrying straps — but none of them is graded for removal from service, all repairable at modest cost. You examine five at random. The canvas separation on two of the five is visible; the spine structure is intact. The condition description in the ledger is broadly confirmed by the sample. The full suite of plate armour for officers occupies the south wall: complete harnesses — breast and back, visor-less sallet helmets or barbutes, arm guards, and leg guards — stored on wooden stands. The spring register lists twenty-three complete harnesses for senior officers and forty-two partial harnesses, the partial ones lacking one or more components. Zane's ledger records the same. You count twenty-three complete stands. You count forty-two partial stands. This matches. The material condition of the plate ranges from polished and oiled to showing surface rust in the joints; Zane's ledger grades each harness individually, and you spot-check six of the grades against the condition you can see. In five of the six cases, the ledger grade is consistent with the visible condition. In the sixth, a partial harness graded as buono in the ledger, the gorget is warped and the wrist guard of the right arm has a crack that suggests it would not serve its function adequately in use. You note the discrepancy and indicate that the piece should be re-evaluated.

The mail coats and the helmets occupy the eastern section of the armoury, along with the miscellaneous personal weapons — short swords, boarding axes, and spears — and represent the largest single item count of the day. The spring register records four hundred and eighty-six mail coats and seven hundred and sixty-three helmets. The mail coats are hung on iron rods screwed into the east wall, in groups of ten, each group tagged with a card bearing the group number and the count within the group. The tags were placed by Pietro Zane at the time of the last full rearrangement of the armoury, which he says occurred in fourteen eighty-five. You count the tags: there are forty-eight groups, which at ten coats per group would yield four hundred and eighty. One group has a card reading eleven rather than ten — this is group twenty-three, and a physical count of group twenty-three confirms eleven coats. This gives a total of four hundred and eighty-one. The spring register records four hundred and eighty-six — five more than the count. The discrepancy cannot be immediately explained. Pietro Zane searches his ledger for any note of mail coats issued or removed since the spring. He finds a notation from the seventeenth of September recording that four mail coats from groups fourteen and fifteen were identified as having broken rings and removed to the Arsenal's metalwork repair section for mending, and that one additional coat was sent with them for size comparison as a reference item. These five coats — four for repair and one reference — would account for the discrepancy if they have not been returned. Zane contacts the repair section through a messenger; the messenger returns, in the time it takes to complete the helmet count, with the information that the four repair coats are still in the repair section, and the reference coat is with them. Five coats, as noted, absent and in repair. Discrepancy resolved. The helmets: you count the helmet racks and arrive at seven hundred and sixty-one. The spring register records seven hundred and sixty-three. Two helmets unaccounted for by the rack count. Zane's ledger does not explain the difference.

### CHUNK audio-05-05

The numbering system in the armoury presents a further observation, recorded in the register as a finding rather than a discrepancy. When you examine the catalogue numbers on the individual items — each piece in the armoury should, by current practice, bear a stamped or burned catalogue number that corresponds to its entry in Pietro Zane's ledger — you find that a proportion of the items carry numbers in a format that does not match the current system. The current system, established in the armoury re-cataloguing of fourteen sixty-five, uses a three-part number: a letter prefix indicating the item category (C for crossbow, B for bolt bundle, P for pavise, F for plate armour, M for mail coat, E for helmet, A for miscellaneous arms), followed by a two-digit sequential number within the category, followed by a single-digit quality grade. Items catalogued before fourteen sixty-five use a simpler system: a two-part number with a different letter prefix and no quality grade. Of the one hundred and twelve pieces you examine across all categories for the spot-check of condition gradings, thirty-four bear the pre-fourteen-sixty-five format. These thirty-four pieces have entries in Zane's ledger, mapped to their old numbers by a translation table that was created at the time of the fourteen sixty-five re-cataloguing, but the physical items themselves were never re-stamped. The spring register notes the existence of items carrying the old format but also does not require their re-stamping, treating the translation table as sufficient. You record the observation as you find it: approximately thirty per cent of items in the spot-check sample carry catalogue numbers in the pre-fourteen-sixty-five format, translation table held in the armoury ledger, no physical re-stamping conducted since fourteen sixty-five. The armoury survey is complete at the sixth hour of the afternoon. Total serviceable items across all categories: three thousand two hundred and fourteen. The stated wartime mobilization requirement for the full Arsenal galley fleet, as listed in an annex to the instructions you were given on the fourth of November, is six thousand. You note both figures in the closing line of the armoury section, without analysis of the difference, which is not within the scope of the survey. You close the armoury section. You close the register. Your cahier, begun on the fourth of November with blank pages, is now full. The eleventh day is complete. Tomorrow you compile the certification page.

The miscellaneous personal weapons — the category that Pietro Zane's ledger labels armi varie — occupy the last storage area in the armoury, a section of open rack shelving along the southern portion of the east wall, lower than the helmet racks and partially behind a wooden divider that Zane installed in fourteen eighty-seven to separate what he describes as the cutting and impact weapons from the ranged and protective equipment on practical grounds — the crossbow strings, he explains, were becoming entangled with the protruding axe handles. The armi varie category covers three sub-classes in the spring register: scimitarre corte, which the register uses for short swords of varying blade type; asce da abbordaggio, boarding axes; and lancie, spears and boarding pikes. The spring register for the short swords records forty-seven pieces across a range of blade lengths from fourteen to twenty-two Venetian inches, subdivided by blade type as straight, slightly curved, or fully curved, the last being taken from various sources including decommissioned vessels and captured material. You count the short swords individually, the blades being bound in pairs by leather straps for storage: twenty-three pairs and one single, which gives forty-seven, matching the spring register. The boarding axes present a different picture. The spring register records sixty-one in this sub-class, of which fifty-seven are the standard Arsenal issue with a single-bit blade and spike, and four are described as captured German-style axes of different dimension. Your count yields sixty, one fewer than the register, and Zane's ledger contains no note of any issue or removal. The spears and boarding pikes are similarly one short: the register records one hundred and twelve, a combination of full-length boarding pikes and shorter spears, but you count one hundred and eleven. Again, no note in Zane's ledger explains the absence. In total, the miscellaneous weapons section shows two hundred and eighteen pieces present against two hundred and twenty registered, with two pieces absent and no documentation to account for either.

The total count of serviceable items across all eight categories of the armoury — crossbows, bolts, pavises, complete plate harnesses, partial plate harnesses, mail coats, helmets, and miscellaneous weapons — is three thousand two hundred and fourteen, as previously noted. The instructions you carry, in an annex prepared by the Arsenal's administrative office, compare this figure to the mobilization requirement: six thousand items of personal arms and armour to fully equip the complement of a deployed galley fleet of the primary war establishment. The ratio is, therefore, approximately one item available for every one point nine required. This comparison is not elaborated upon in the annex, and you do not elaborate upon it in the register; the annex simply presents the two figures side by side and notes that the deficiency will be the subject of a separate report to the Senate by the Provveditori at the end of the fiscal year. You record the mobilization requirement figure and the inventory figure in the closing summary of the armoury section, as required by the instructions, without analysis. Pietro Zane, who has accompanied the entire day's inventory, does not comment on the comparison. He assists with the closing count of the register pages and signs his name at the foot of the armoury section, as the instructions require him to do as the custodian of the items surveyed, accepting responsibility for any items found present and graded serviceable in the register thus certified. His signature is the third of four that will appear on the certification page; the fourth will be that of Nicolò Falier, who has already been told via a note sent through the scribes' room that he is required at the certification ceremony scheduled for the fifteenth of November. You close the armoury section and make a final review of the day's entries, checking the column totals against the individual entries, confirming that the summation in the closing paragraph of each sub-category matches the sum of the individual count rows above it. The review takes approximately two hours. You find one arithmetic error in the bolt sub-section — a column total written as two hundred and thirty where the correct sum of the rows is two hundred and thirty — which is to say the total is in fact correct and the apparent error was your own momentary misread. You make no correction. The eleventh day ends at the eighth hour of the afternoon.
