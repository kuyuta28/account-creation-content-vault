### CHUNK audio-00-01
<!-- img: IMG-00-01 -->

On the fourth of November, in the year fourteen hundred and ninety, you arrive at the water gate of the Arsenale Vecchio, on the north-western wall of the Arsenal complex, at approximately the sixth hour of the morning, which is to say shortly after dawn, the sky above the lagoon still grey and the surface of the water pale and without reflection, the wind from the northeast carrying a smell of cold air and rope tar and the faint salt of the sea. You carry with you a leather satchel, in which are placed the following items: a letter of appointment signed by the senior Provveditore all'Arsenal, Ser Marco Grimani, dated the first of November and countersigned by his two colleagues on the magistracy; and the inventory register from the previous spring, which is a bound volume of one hundred and four pages, the entries made in the hand of the scribe who conducted that survey, a man named Bartolomeo Sanudo, who is not present today as he is engaged on a separate assignment at the Nuovissimo. The satchel also contains a fresh cahier of blank pages, eighty leaves, prepared by the Arsenal's own scribes' room and stamped on the inside cover with the Arsenal's seal, and two reed quills freshly cut, with a small folded paper of dried ink cakes and a glass vial of water, the latter stoppered with wax. You also carry, rolled and tied, a second letter containing the specific instructions for the winter inventory, issued under the authority of the Collegio on the twenty-eighth of October, being the same instructions as those issued for the spring inventory with amendments noted in the margin in a different hand, the annotations being concerned primarily with the procedure for condemned hulls, about which more will be said in the appropriate section. The water gate is attended by two Arsenal guards in the livery of the Republic. You present your letter of appointment. One guard reads it, or appears to read it, for a period of time sufficient to have read a document of that length, and then stands aside. The second guard does not read it. The gate opens.

### CHUNK audio-00-02
<!-- img: IMG-00-02 -->

You are escorted through the Arsenale Vecchio by the guard who read your letter, or appeared to read it, along a paved path between two storage buildings whose doors are closed, toward the scribes' room, which is a low building set against the south interior wall of the Vecchio section, built of brick with a tiled roof from which rain has recently fallen, leaving a dark stain along the eaves. The scribes' room contains three long tables, each with benches, and on the table nearest the window, which faces south and receives the morning light when there is morning light to receive, you find the following: the previous year's winter register, in a binding similar to your spring register but with a broken clasp, repaired with a strip of leather tied with thread; and a list of the sections of the Arsenal to be surveyed and the days assigned to each section, written in a clear administrative hand. Beside these is a note from the head of the scribes' room, signed but without a date, advising that the proto da marangon, the master ship carpenter, will meet you at the door of the covered sheds on the morning of the fifth day, and that on days one and two you are to conduct the ordnance survey of the cannon hall without a carpenter's escort, that function being unnecessary for ordnance. The Arsenal itself, which you have entered before on two previous occasions, is divided into three sections: the Vecchio, established in the year eleven hundred and four and enlarged several times thereafter; the Nuovo, built approximately between the years thirteen hundred and three and thirteen hundred and twenty-five; and the Nuovissimo, the most recent extension, constructed in the early years of the previous century, used primarily for timber seasoning and the storage of the largest class of galley. The total area enclosed within the Arsenal walls is approximately thirty-two campi, by the measurement recorded in the most recent survey of the physical plant, which was conducted in fourteen hundred and seventy-three and which is cited in the instructions you carry. The instructions do not describe the Arsenal further, on the assumption that the scribe assigned to the inventory will have made himself familiar with the layout by prior visit or by consultation with the spring register. You open the spring register. You read the first entry. You prepare your fresh cahier. On the fourth of November, fourteen hundred and ninety, you begin.

### CHUNK audio-00-03
<!-- img: IMG-00-03 -->

The Arsenal that you are about to survey employs, in rough terms, a permanent workforce of approximately one thousand to fifteen hundred men called the arsenalotti, whose labour is organized under the authority of the three Provveditori all'Arsenal and administered through a hierarchy of proto positions, each responsible for a different technical domain and each maintaining separate working records. The proto da marangon oversees carpentry. The proto da calafao handles caulking. Further positions cover the mast and spar works and the ordnance. Any of these officials may be called upon during the inventory to provide technical assessments that fall within their expertise. This organizational structure, while effective for the daily management of the Arsenal's production activities, creates a specific administrative challenge for the winter inventory: the central register that you are responsible for producing is a single document that draws on information from at least eight separate sources. The four proto offices each maintain their own internal register. Beyond those, there is the Tana's ledger, the timber yard's working records, the sub-register maintained for the Bucintoro, and the armoury keeper's ledger. These internal registers do not share a common numbering format, a common date standard, or a common system for recording discrepancies. The reconciliation of these different systems into a single coherent document is, in effect, the primary task of the winter inventory, of which the physical counting is only one component. You note all of this not in the register — which is not the place for this kind of observation — but in your working notes, written in the back pages of the cahier on the third of November when you were preparing for the survey, and which you will destroy after the register is complete, as the instructions indicate that working notes are not to be preserved with the register.
