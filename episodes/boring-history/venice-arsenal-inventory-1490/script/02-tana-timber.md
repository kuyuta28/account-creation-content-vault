On the morning of the sixth of November, the third day of the survey, you are directed to the Tana, the rope factory, which occupies a building on the north side of the Arsenale Nuovo, set parallel to the north wall of the complex, its eastern end connecting to the Nuovissimo by a covered passage, its western end terminating at a large wooden door that opens onto the main courtyard of the Nuovo. The building's length, as recorded in the physical survey of fourteen hundred and seventy-three and reiterated in the instructions you carry, is approximately one hundred and eighty Venetian passi — a measurement that, if converted, corresponds to somewhere close to three hundred and sixteen metres by the standards of the period, though the instructions do not make this conversion and you do not perform it, having no reason to. You have been in the Tana once before, at the beginning of the year, accompanying a senior scribe on a brief visit for a different purpose, but you have not surveyed it, and the building's interior, when the main door is opened by the key held by the Tana's own foreman, a man named Zorzi Contarini, presents itself as longer than you had remembered. The vault extends ahead of you, slightly graded from west to east to manage rainwater drainage through a series of small stone channels in the floor, the ceiling rising to a height of approximately six Venetian passi at the crown of the arch at its highest point, and above six passi at the entry arch, where the vault is wider. Along both side walls, at the height of a man's shoulder and above, great coils of rope are suspended from iron hooks screwed into the masonry, each coil tied with a cord from which hangs a wooden tag, painted or burned with a number that corresponds to the Tana's own internal stock register, a separate ledger maintained by Zorzi Contarini and his two assistants, which you will need to reconcile with the Arsenal's central inventory register over the course of the day.

The rope inventory is divided by the Tana's internal register into six categories, and you proceed through them in the order established by that register, which runs from the heaviest to the lightest, left wall to right wall, west end to east. The first category is the anchor cable: tarred hemp rope of the heaviest specification, twisted in three strands of nine yarns each, used for the bower and kedge anchors of the largest class of galley. The Tana register records eleven coils of this specification in stock, each coil of a nominal weight of one hundred and twenty libbre grosse, the heavy pound used for material of this density, making a total recorded weight of one thousand three hundred and twenty libbre grosse. You count the anchor cable coils on their hooks along the left wall: there are eleven. The coil tags are checked against the Tana ledger: nine tags match their ledger entries by number and approximate weight. One tag — number forty-three in the ledger — is missing from its coil, replaced by a blank cord with a knot at the end, suggesting the tag was removed and not replaced. Zorzi Contarini offers no explanation for this, saying only that the missing tag was noted in an entry in his own ledger from the fourteenth of September and that a replacement tag was requested from the scribes' room, but that the scribes' room returned the request as falling outside their remit, such tags being the responsibility of the Tana's own staff. The eleventh coil has a tag that corresponds to a ledger entry, but the ledger entry is crossed out and re-entered in a different hand on the following line, the difference being a weight notation that was changed from one hundred and twenty libbre grosse to one hundred and eleven libbre grosse, an amendment that, according to a marginal note beside the crossing out, was made on the third of October following a reweighing. You record the anchor cable count as eleven coils, total registered weight one thousand three hundred and eleven libbre grosse following amendment, one tag absent.

The second category is the shroud rope: a slightly lighter specification than the anchor cable, in three thicknesses designated in the Tana register as heavy shroud, medium shroud, and light shroud, each thickness intended for a different class of vessel. The heavy shroud rope is reserved for galleys of the first class, the galeazze and the large transport galleys; the medium shroud for the standard galea sottile; the light shroud for the smaller vessels including the fuste and the peote. The Tana register records, for heavy shroud rope: seven coils, total registered weight six hundred and thirty-eight libbre veneziane, noting that the libbra veneziana rather than the libbre grosse is used for this category. This distinction, Zorzi Contarini explains, arises from a regulation change in fourteen eighty-one that affected the Tana's weighting system without apparently affecting the anchor cable category, and that has resulted, since that year, in the two categories being measured in different pounds. The central inventory register accommodates this situation by carrying both figures side by side, though the column widths in the register's printed form were not designed for this dual entry and the figures consequently appear crowded in the relevant section. You count the heavy shroud rope coils and find seven, matching the register. For the medium shroud rope, the Tana register records nineteen coils distributed across four separate hanging sections, some on the left wall and the first two hooks of the right wall near the west entry. You count twelve on the left wall and seven on the right, which together account for all nineteen recorded in the register. The light shroud rope is stored on the right wall between the fourth and ninth hooks counting from the west, where the register records thirty-one coils; your count confirms thirty-one. You record the shroud rope inventory in full: all three specifications together account for fifty-seven coils, every one of them matching the Tana register, with a noted discrepancy in the pound system used which predates this inventory and is a matter of administrative record rather than a physical finding.

The running rigging — the untarred ropes used for the manipulation of the sails and yards during operation, as distinct from the fixed structural ropes of the standing rigging — occupies the central section of both walls, between the shroud rope to the west and the sail-rigging reserve to the east. The Tana register divides the running rigging inventory into seven sub-categories corresponding to specific applications aboard a standard galley: halyards, sheets, braces, lifts, bowlines, clew lines, and a miscellaneous category that the register labels simply as corde di manovra generale, general manoeuvre rope. Each sub-category occupies its own section on the wall, with its own set of tags. The total count across all seven, as recorded in the Tana register, is one hundred and four coils. You work through each sub-category individually, counting coils and cross-checking tags against the ledger entries. The first categories present no surprises. The halyards are complete. Then you reach the sheets, where the register lists twenty-two coils and you count twenty-one — one hook is empty, with neither a tag nor a blank cord present, suggesting the absence has not been noted. Zorzi Contarini, when asked, says that a coil was taken out for testing on one of the hulls in the third shed approximately three weeks ago, on or about the fourteenth of October, and was not returned; he believes it to have been returned but is not certain; he has not checked since the fourteenth. The coil's status — whether it is somewhere in the sheds, considered on loan, or consumed in use — cannot be determined from the present location. You note the absence. Working through the remaining categories — braces, lifts, bowlines, and clew lines — you find sixty-seven coils where the register lists sixty-eight. Whether this missing coil is the same one absent from the sheets or a separate loss, the evidence does not allow a determination. The general manoeuvre rope, the final category, is complete at fourteen coils. In total, one hundred and two coils are present against one hundred and four registered, two absent for reasons that remain uncertain.

At the eastern end of the Tana, in the final quarter of the building, the inventory transitions from ropes in use and ready stock to ropes awaiting assessment. This section is less ordered than the western portions of the hall: the coils here are stacked on the floor in groups rather than hung on hooks, because the hooks in the eastern section were damaged in a collapse of a section of the wall's plaster cladding in fourteen eighty-six and have not been replaced, the repair having been assessed and scheduled twice and cancelled on both occasions for reasons unrelated to the rope inventory. On the floor, your count proceeds by group rather than by individual hook, which introduces a higher margin of estimation error. The Tana register records in this section: sixteen coils of reserve anchor cable, four coils of heavy shroud held as reserve, and forty coils of mixed rope listed as assortita — assorted, which is a category used for rope that has been inspected, found serviceable but not of a standard sufficient for the first-line categories, and retained in the second-line stock that may be issued for training exercises, minor vessel maintenance, or civilian work carried out within the Arsenal's walls. You count in the eastern section: sixteen reserve anchor cable coils, four heavy shroud reserve coils, and approximately forty-two coils of assorted rope, the imprecision arising from the floor-stacked arrangement, which makes individual counting difficult without moving all the coils, which you and Zorzi Contarini do for two stacks but not the third. The estimate for the third stack is thirty-eight to forty-two, for which you record forty as the working figure, consistent with the register, noting the estimation method. In addition to these registered coils, in the eastern corner nearest the door to the Nuovissimo passage, there is a further quantity of rope — approximately forty coils, tightly stacked against the wall — which does not appear in the Tana register and which Zorzi Contarini identifies as a consignment received from Corfu on the twenty-second of September, not yet inspected, not yet accepted into stock, therefore not recorded in any inventory classification.

The Corfu consignment presents a category problem. The rope exists within the Arsenal on its territory, within the building under your survey. It is not listed in the Tana register. It is therefore not listed in the central inventory. To include it in the winter register as a counted and assessed item would require that it first be formally received and classed by Zorzi Contarini, which would in turn require an inspection by a designated rope inspector, a position currently held by a man named Alvise Tagliapietra, who, according to Zorzi Contarini, is on assignment at the Arsenal's outstation at Pula until the fifth of December. To exclude it from the register entirely would leave a physical quantity of material within the Arsenal's walls unaccounted for in any document. The instructions you carry do not address this scenario. The spring register does not contain a precedent for it. You consult the instructions a second time. The closest relevant provision, in the sixth clause of the instructions, states that any material arriving in the Arsenal between the spring and winter surveys that has not yet been formally received is to be noted as follows: present within the Arsenal, unregistered, arrival date if known, origin if known, quantity estimated, pending formal receipt. You record the Corfu rope consignment accordingly: approximately forty coils of rope of undetermined specification, present within the Tana unregistered, arrived by the records of the water gate on the twenty-second of September from Corfu, not yet inspected or accepted into stock, pending formal receipt by the designated rope inspector on or after the fifth of December. The total Tana inventory for the winter register, therefore, lists one hundred and ninety-three registered coils in the active categories plus reserve, plus approximately forty coils unregistered and pending. The Tana section of the survey is completed at the close of the third day. You seal the section. Tomorrow is the timber yard.

The timber yard is situated in the Nuovissimo section, the most recently built extension of the Arsenal complex, and it is reached on the fourth day by crossing the main yard of the Nuovo and entering the Nuovissimo through the wooden gate in the dividing wall, a gate that is, unlike the cannon hall door, not kept open but is unlocked each morning by the yard foreman, a man named Piero Scarpa, who has held the position since fourteen eighty-one and who meets you at the gate with a ledger of similar format to the Tana's internal register. The timber yard is partly covered — four large shed structures with tile roofs, each capable of sheltering two or three rows of planked timber — and partly open to the sky, the open sections being used for the initial seasoning of freshly cut timber before it is moved under cover when the moisture content, assessed by a method that Piero Scarpa describes briefly and that involves probing the end grain with a pointed rod, has reached a level that the covering sheds will not cause further harm. The covered sheds are designated by letter: shed A through shed D. The open sections are numbered one through seven. The timber yard survey proceeds through each section in this sequence. In covered shed A: fourteen rows of oak planking in lengths of approximately twelve Venetian feet, stacked on timber supports at a height that allows air circulation between the rows, tagged with the date of initial stacking, which ranges across these rows from fourteen eighty-one to fourteen eighty-six. The September fourteen eighty-six stack, which occupies the two southern rows of shed A, is assessed by Piero Scarpa as fully seasoned, consistent with the four-to-five-year standard for oak of this thickness. The earlier stacks, from fourteen eighty-one through eighty-four, are assessed as over-seasoned — that is, beyond the optimal window — and are noted in the yard ledger as priority for use before further degradation occurs.

Covered shed B contains larch and elm planking, materials used for specific structural components — larch for decking and internal partitions where weight reduction is preferred, elm for keels and keel supports where the wood's resistance to water immersion is the relevant property. The larch stack in shed B dates from fourteen eighty-seven, assessed as adequately seasoned for immediate use. The elm stack is more recent, arriving in two consignments: the first in fourteen eighty-eight, now adequately seasoned; the second in late fourteen eighty-nine, assessed as requiring a further twelve to eighteen months before use and currently occupying the northern half of shed B. Covered sheds C and D contain the Dalmatian oak consignment, which arrived in two shipments in April and June of fourteen eighty-nine. The Dalmatian consignment — four hundred and seventeen planks in the first shipment, three hundred and sixty-two planks in the second, a combined total of seven hundred and seventy-nine planks — is assessed by Piero Scarpa as requiring a further twenty-four months of covered seasoning, which would bring it to readiness no earlier than late fourteen ninety-one. The problem with the Dalmatian oak is not its quality — it is assessed as high-quality material — but its location. Covered sheds C and D are, by the yard ledger, designated as temporary storage for the Dalmatian consignment pending the construction of two additional covered shed sections, to be called shed E and shed F, which were planned in fourteen eighty-eight and for which the foundation work has not been started, the relevant allocation of funds from the Provveditori's budget having been deferred in each of the two years since the plan was written. The Dalmatian oak in sheds C and D is therefore occupying the two sheds that were, before the consignment arrived, used for the storage of fully seasoned material awaiting use, and that function has been displaced to the open sections one through four, which are considered suitable for short-term outdoor storage of seasoned timber but not for material requiring long-term protection. Piero Scarpa notes this in the yard ledger. You note it in the inventory register, as a condition finding: covered storage capacity insufficient for current stock, two sheds occupied by material requiring twenty-four additional months of covered seasoning, sheds E and F not constructed. This is noted without comment on the cause or the remedy, both of which fall outside the scope of the survey.

The open sections of the timber yard — numbered one through seven — are surveyed in the afternoon of the fourth day, after the covered sheds have been recorded. The open sections occupy the northern portion of the Nuovissimo yard, separated from the covered sheds by a wide passage of packed earth and stone that serves as the working thoroughfare for the timber handlers and their carts. Open sections one through four, as previously noted, currently hold seasoned material displaced from the covered sheds by the Dalmatian oak consignment. In section one, forty-eight oak beams of the heavy-construction class, length approximately twenty Venetian feet, width approximately one and one-half feet at the broadest section, destined for use as keel sections and stem posts. These are wrapped in tarpaulin — a treatment applied when seasoned material must remain in the open for an extended period — but Piero Scarpa observes that two of the tarpaulin covers have split along the fold seam and are no longer providing full protection from rain. The beams beneath the split tarpaulins are assessed as still serviceable, though the split occurred recently enough — within the past three weeks, based on visible water staining on the top faces of the beams — that no material damage has resulted. He intends to address the split covers this week; you record the condition as: forty-eight beams, serviceable, two tarpaulins requiring replacement. Open sections two and three contain fir planking in two specification classes, the lighter used for deck construction and the heavier for hull planking of the smaller vessel classes. Section two: eighty-three planks, fir, deck specification. Section three: one hundred and twelve planks, fir, hull specification, stacked in four rows of approximately twenty-eight planks each. Both section two and section three are covered with tarpaulin, and the tarpaulins are intact, and the material is assessed as serviceable. Open section four contains mixed offcuts and short-section timber designated as workshop stock, material used within the Arsenal's carpentry shops for fittings, repairs to internal structures, and miscellaneous wooden components. The yard ledger lists this material collectively as assorted timber, with a quantity expressed in weight rather than in individual pieces, recorded as approximately nine hundred and fifty libbre grosse. No count of individual pieces is required for the workshop stock; you record the quantity as nine hundred and fifty libbre grosse workshop timber, consistent with the yard ledger, condition satisfactory.

Open sections five, six, and seven hold timber that has been delivered to the yard but has not yet been formally accepted into the Arsenal's registered stock, occupying a status similar to the unregistered Corfu rope in the Tana. Section five: a quantity of timber described in the delivery note — a folded paper document that Piero Scarpa locates from a wooden box near the section — as sixty planks of Istrian larch, standard specification, delivered on the seventh of October from the Arsenal's supplier Giacomo di Bortolomeo of San Polo, awaiting inspection and formal acceptance. The material appears sound to Piero Scarpa's inspection, though formal acceptance requires the signature of the proto da marangon, Nicolò Falier, and Falier has not yet attended this section, having been occupied with other construction work since the delivery. Section six is empty. The yard ledger entry for section six reads: reserved for incoming fir consignment expected September, not arrived; and a second note below, in newer ink: still expected, no delivery confirmation received, consignment may have been diverted to Chioggia Arsenal based on September routing instructions, confirmation pending. You record section six as empty, incoming consignment not arrived, situation noted. Section seven, the easternmost open section, contains the timber designated as condemned: material assessed as having deteriorated beyond serviceable limits, retained in the yard pending formal condemnation proceedings that would authorize its disposal. There are nineteen pieces by Piero Scarpa's count — twelve planks and seven beams in various sizes — all tagged with a yellow-painted strip on one end, the standard visual designation for condemned material in the timber yard. Most are early fourteen eighties in origin, having been in the yard long enough that the tarpaulin formerly covering them has rotted away without replacement. You record nineteen condemned pieces, formal disposal authorization pending. The timber yard section of the register is completed at the close of the fourth day. Two items remain open: the formal acceptance of the Istrian larch in section five, pending Nicolò Falier's inspection, and the confirmed status of the missing fir consignment last directed toward Chioggia. Both are noted as outstanding at the foot of the timber yard section.

The sail inventory, which by the instructions should follow the rope inventory in the Tana section before turning to the timber yard, occupies the final part of the afternoon on the third day, after the section on unregistered material has been closed. The sails are not stored in the Tana itself — the Tana being a rope factory and the sail stores being maintained in a separate lockable room in the Nuovo section — but the inventory of sails is grouped with the Tana section in the central register by administrative convention, on the grounds that both the rope and the sail stores are managed under the same port authority. The sail room is in a building adjacent to the Tana's eastern wall, accessed by a separate key held by Zorzi Contarini, who accompanies you to the room and locates the key on a hook inside the Tana before leading you around the outside of the building to the sail room's own door. The sail inventory categories in the spring register are four: main sails for galea grossa, main sails for galea sottile, lateen sails for fuste and smaller vessels, and spare triangular sails described as vela di fortuna — storm or emergency sails. The spring register totals are: eight galea grossa main sails, twenty-three galea sottile main sails, nineteen lateen sails for the smaller vessel classes, and six vela di fortuna. The sails are folded and sealed in oilcloth packages, stacked on wooden shelves, each package marked with the sail type and dimensions in painted letters on the oilcloth face. You count the packages shelf by shelf. The galea grossa main sails and the vela di fortuna are complete — eight and six respectively, matching the spring totals. The galea sottile section, however, shows twenty-two packages where the spring register lists twenty-three, and the lateen section shows eighteen against nineteen. Zorzi Contarini's records for the sail room account for both absences: one galea sottile main sail was issued to an active vessel in August and not returned, and one lateen sail was issued in September to the same vessel, replacing a sail damaged during a training exercise. The absences are explained. You record them as issued and outstanding. In total, fifty-four packages are present of the fifty-six registered.
