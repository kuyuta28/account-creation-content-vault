The cannon hall is a stone building attached to the south wall of the Arsenale Vecchio, entered through a door wide enough to admit a wheeled cart, the door currently standing open, propped with a wedge of timber. You enter on the morning of the fourth of November, which is the first day of the survey, in the company of the Arsenal guard assigned to the ordnance section, whose name is recorded in the guard roster as Zuanne di Maffio, a man of approximately forty years, who carries a torch in one hand and a ring of keys in the other, though the principal door is already open. The hall is long and narrow — approximately sixty-two Venetian passi end to end, by the measurement recorded in the spring register, which you have not yet verified yourself — and the ceiling is low, supported by stone arches at intervals of seven passi, the arches darkened at the crown with the smoke of years. Along each wall, on wooden trestles of uniform construction, the cannon are arranged. The arrangement follows the classification established by the ordnance regulations of fourteen hundred and sixty-one, revised in fourteen hundred and seventy-eight: pieces are grouped first by type, then within each type by calibre in ascending order from the door inward, with iron pieces and bronze pieces of the same type kept in separate rows, bronze to the left as you enter, iron to the right. The spring register lists seventeen distinct types of ordnance present in the hall. Your task on the first two days is to verify that each piece is present, in the position recorded, in the condition recorded, and to note any discrepancies between the current state and the spring register entry. You begin at the door with the smallest pieces, the falconetti, on the left-hand side of the first arch.

The falconetti are the smallest cannon in the hall, intended for mounting along the gunwale of a galea sottile for use against personnel and small craft at close distance. The spring register records twenty-three falconetti of the first calibre — that is, pieces intended to fire a ball of approximately one and one-half libbre veneziane — and seventeen of the second calibre, intended for a two-libbra ball, the distinction being relevant to the fitting arrangements on different classes of galley. You begin counting the bronze falconetti of the first calibre. There are twenty-two on the trestle, where the register lists twenty-three. You note the discrepancy and move on. The iron first-calibre pieces, the second-calibre bronze, and the second-calibre iron all prove consistent with the register — six, seventeen, and four respectively, each where expected. The one missing piece from the first calibre bronze falconetti — piece number fourteen in the spring register's running sequence, a piece described as cast in fourteen hundred and seventy-one at the Arsenal's own foundry and bearing the Arsenal arms on the left trunnion — cannot be located within the hall by visible inspection. Zuanne di Maffio, the guard, is asked whether any piece has been removed from the hall since the spring. He is not aware of any removal. He suggests the piece may have been moved to the Nuovo section for a fitting trial. No record of such a transfer is present in the documentation you have been given. You note the absence of piece number fourteen and the absence of a transfer record, and you proceed to the next type.

The falconetto is followed in the ordnance sequence by the passavolante, the long-barrelled piece intended for flat-trajectory fire at longer distance, a type whose barrels are notably lengthier in proportion to their bore than the pieces on either side of them in the classification. The spring register records eleven passavolanti in the hall: seven bronze and four iron. The calibre ranges from three libbre veneziane to seven libbre in the pieces recorded here, the eight-libbra and above being classified separately as colubrine. You count seven bronze passavolanti. You count four iron passavolanti. The total matches the spring register. You examine each piece for the condition notation — the spring register assigns to each piece one of three condition grades: buono, da riparare, or da disarmare, the last meaning the piece is judged no longer serviceable and suitable only for the melting down of its material. All eleven passavolanti in the hall are currently graded buono. You verify this by visual inspection of the cascabel, the touchhole, and the bore of each piece, using the calibre gauge hanging from a cord at the end of the second arch, a bronze disc with nine graduated holes. Seven of the eleven pieces are bronze; of these, six bear founder's marks cast into the barrel — names or symbols, most of them illegible either from wear or from the darkening of the metal over time. The seventh bronze passavolante, a piece of six-libbra calibre, bears no founder's mark. You record this as a notation: no founder's mark present, calibre confirmed six libbre veneziane, condition buono, no further information available from the physical inspection. Across the next several days of the survey, eleven further pieces in various categories will be found to lack a founder's mark. The significance of this, if any, is not discussed in the instructions.

The colubrine occupy the largest section of the hall, the middle portion between the third and seventh arches on each side. The colubrina, as a type, is the dominant tactical cannon of the period, a medium-length barrel of moderate calibre suited to both naval and land use, and the Arsenal's stocks of this type are accordingly the deepest in the hall. The spring register records forty-seven colubrine in total: thirty-one bronze and sixteen iron, ranging in calibre from eight libbre veneziane to sixteen libbre. You work through the bronze colubrine first, in calibre order. The bronze pieces number thirty-one in total, spread across five calibre classes from eight to sixteen libbre, the lighter calibres better represented than the heavier, the count tapering from nine pieces in the smallest class down to four in the largest. Each calibre matches the spring register. You move to the iron colubrine. There are sixteen iron pieces across the same five classes, the distribution running from four in the smallest calibres down to two in the largest, and these too match the register in every class. Of the forty-seven colubrine, two are graded da riparare in the spring register: one bronze ten-libbra piece with a noted crack in the cascabel, and one iron twelve-libbra piece with corrosion reported at the touchhole. The bronze piece with the cracked cascabel: the crack is visible. The crack has not widened, or does not appear to have widened, since the spring; the spring register included an annotation requesting that a repair estimate be obtained from the foundry, and notes in the margin of that annotation indicate that a request was submitted on the fourteenth of May, but no estimate is present in the documentation you carry. This is noted. The iron piece with the touchhole corrosion is examined. The corrosion is present. It has, by visual comparison with the spring register's brief description, progressed. You grade the piece da riparare, as before, and note that re-boring may now be required where a surface treatment alone may have been sufficient in the spring.

The bombardelle are stored, according to the classification scheme, at the far end of the hall, inward from the seventh arch, adjacent to a section of wall that shows a faint stain of moisture, which is noted in the spring register as a long-standing condition that has been reported to the Arsenal's building supervisor on three separate occasions, the most recent of which was fourteen hundred and eighty-eight, with no response received. The bombardella is an older type of piece, short-barrelled and heavy-walled, designed to fire stone balls at short range with a high arc; the type had been largely superseded for naval use by lighter bronze pieces, but the Arsenal retains them in stock against the possibility that they may be required for coastal or fortress use. The spring register records eight bombardelle in the hall: all iron, all of the fifteen-libbra stone calibre, all graded buono. You count eight. You examine each. They are of uniform appearance, being almost certainly cast in the same period, though only three bear what might be a foundry mark — a series of parallel lines on the left breech face that could be a tally mark of the caster or could be a defect in the casting. The spring register notes the three marks. You verify their presence. The bombardelle are otherwise unremarkable. None has a touchhole obstruction. None shows cascabel damage. They sit on their trestles at the end of the hall, in a section that, because of its distance from the door, is darker than the rest, and the torch carried by Zuanne di Maffio is the only illumination. At this distance from the entrance, the sound from outside — men moving in the yard, an occasional hammer blow, water against the quay wall — is reduced to a low and even texture that does not resolve into any specific source. You record the eight bombardelle. You return toward the door.

### CHUNK audio-01-06

The petriere occupy the right-hand side of the hall, closer to the door than the bombardelle, on the iron-side rows: they are essentially the stone-throwing variant of the bombard principle, thinner-walled than the bombardelle but firing larger stone balls at still lower velocity. The Arsenal distinguishes between two types: the petriera ordinaria, for lighter stone loads, and the petriera grossa, for the heaviest calibres. The spring register records fourteen petriere in the hall: nine ordinarie and five grosse, all iron, all graded buono or da riparare. You begin with the ordinarie. There are nine. Each is examined with the calibre gauge. The bore diameter of each piece is compared against the gauge holes and, where a gauge confirms the calibre, compared against the calibre entry in the spring register. For seven of the nine ordinarie, the bore measurements confirm the register entries. For the eighth, the spring register records a fourteen-libbra stone calibre, and the calibre gauge confirms fourteen libbre. For the ninth, the spring register records a fourteen-libbra stone calibre, and the calibre gauge returns a reading of twelve and one-half libbre. The difference is one and one-half libbre veneziane in bore diameter, which is a meaningful difference — a ball sized for fourteen libbre will not seat correctly in a bore of twelve and one-half, and vice versa. You note the discrepancy. You move to the petriere grosse. There are five. For three of them, the bore measurements match the spring register precisely. For the fourth, you note minor surface corrosion at the muzzle face, not recorded in the spring register; you grade this as a new observation and record it as condition change since spring inventory, to be assessed by the foundry. The fifth petriera grossa is the second piece whose measurements present a discrepancy.

The second discrepancy — the fifth petriera grossa — presents a situation that requires somewhat more attention in the record than the first, because the spring register's entry for this piece includes a cross-reference to the winter register of fourteen hundred and eighty-seven, three years prior, in which the same piece was listed with a bore measurement of sixteen libbre veneziane stone calibre. The spring register entry notes that in the spring survey of fourteen hundred and ninety, the piece measured sixteen libbre, consistent with the fourteen eighty-seven record. When you apply the calibre gauge today, the bore of this piece measures fourteen and one-half libbre. The difference between sixteen libbre and fourteen and one-half libbre in bore diameter is a difference that would, in a piece of this class, amount to approximately thirty-eight to forty millimetres of bore diameter — a significant variation, not attributable to measurement error from a correctly applied gauge. You apply the gauge a second time, rotating it to confirm the reading. The reading is fourteen and one-half libbre, or near enough that the difference between fourteen libbre and fourteen and one-half libbre is within the tolerance of the gauge, but the difference between either of those measurements and sixteen libbre is not within any tolerance you are aware of. There are three possible explanations. The first: the fourteen eighty-seven measurement and the spring measurement were both incorrect and today's reading is correct. The second: today's measurement is incorrect. The third: the piece currently sitting on the trestle is not the same piece recorded in fourteen eighty-seven and in the spring register, and the piece that was recorded on those two occasions is elsewhere or destroyed. None of these possibilities can be determined from an inspection of the physical piece alone. You note the discrepancy, the cross-reference to fourteen eighty-seven, and the three possible explanations, in summary form, on the page. You also note that the scribe responsible for the winter register of fourteen eighty-seven, whose name appears on the certification page of that register as Giacomo di Pietro, is not currently employed at the Arsenal. His last recorded date of employment is the twenty-second of September, fourteen eighty-eight, with no reason given. You note that a written query regarding the discrepancy will need to be submitted to the Provveditori for adjudication, and that the query cannot be submitted until after the inventory is complete and the full register is compiled.

The second day of the survey, the fifth of November, is spent completing the cannon hall and beginning the supporting documentation. The remaining tasks from the first day — a final check of the iron colubrine condition gradings and a full count of the shot inventory stored in the wooden bins along the north wall — are completed by the third hour after dawn. The shot inventory, which is recorded in a separate sub-register attached to the back of the cannon hall section, lists shot by material (stone, iron, lead), by calibre in libbre veneziane, and by count. Stone shot: nine hundred and fourteen pieces in twenty-two calibres, stored in labelled wooden bins. The bins are closed with fitted lids, and the count is conducted by lifting each lid and counting, or, in the case of bins with large quantities of small shot, by estimating from the volume and cross-referencing against the spring register's count. In three cases, the estimated count differs from the spring register by more than five pieces. The first is bin eleven, which holds iron shot of the eight-libbre calibre and should contain forty-three pieces according to the spring register; you count forty-one, two short. The second is bin seventeen, a stone shot bin of the twelve-libbre calibre, where the spring register lists sixty-two pieces but you count sixty-four — two over the registered amount, suggesting either an addition not recorded since the spring or a counting error in one survey or the other. The third is bin twenty-two, also stone shot, fourteen-libbre calibre, where you count thirty-six against a register entry of thirty-seven. None of the three discrepancies is large enough to suggest systematic removal of material, and each is noted in the margin alongside its possible explanation. None of the discrepancies is large enough to suggest systematic removal of material. You close the bins and return the lids to their positions. You record the shot inventory figures. You note the three discrepancies in the margin.

The afternoon of the first day is spent in a more detailed examination of the condition gradings for the pieces identified as da riparare in the spring register. There are fourteen such pieces in total across all types, and for each of them you are required by the instructions to note whether the condition has improved, remained the same, or worsened, and whether the repair estimate noted in the spring register appears still applicable or requires revision. For pieces graded da riparare where no spring repair estimate was recorded — which is the case for six of the fourteen, the spring scribe having noted the condition without attaching an estimate — you are to prepare a preliminary cost notation based on a standard rate schedule maintained by the Arsenal's foundry records, a document that you find folded into the back of the spring register. The rate schedule is dated fourteen eighty-six and is described as applicable to the repair of ordnance of the types and materials represented in the Arsenal, with costs expressed in lire di piccoli per unit of work. You work through the six unpriced pieces and apply the schedule to each. The simplest repairs — touchhole cleaning and re-boring — are costed at three lire di piccoli per piece. Cascabel re-casting, which requires removal and foundry work, runs higher, from twenty-two lire di piccoli for the smaller colubrina class to thirty-eight for the petriere. Surface crack patching on non-load-bearing barrel sections falls between these at eight lire di piccoli. For the two pieces whose conditions have worsened since spring — the iron colubrina with the eroded touchhole, and a bronze passavolante identified earlier as showing new surface cracking along the chase — you note the condition change and revise the cost notation upward from the spring estimate. In both cases, the revision is modest: two lire di piccoli more for the colubrina, given the deeper erosion now requiring re-boring rather than surface treatment, and five lire di piccoli more for the passavolante, given the crack's length and the question of whether the barrel metal at the location of the crack retains structural integrity. You record the final cost notations for all fourteen da riparare pieces in a supplementary column in the register.

The cannon hall survey also encompasses a section of secondary ordnance materials stored in a locked side room off the main hall, accessible by a small door set into the north wall near the sixth arch, which Zuanne di Maffio opens with a separate key — smaller than the main hall key — and which requires, once opened, a moment for the eyes to adjust, the room having no windows and deriving all illumination from the torch. This side room is not listed as a separate section in the spring register; instead, its contents are folded into the cannon hall section under a sub-heading that reads: accessori, meaning accessories or ancillary equipment. The accessori fill the room in groups. Powder flasks of cowhide and wood hang from a rail near the door, seventeen in number, used for carrying priming powder to the pieces during action. Against the far wall stand the loading implements — four iron ladles on long wooden poles for charging powder, and nine rammers of similar construction for seating the charge and ball. Eight linstock holders of iron, shaped to hold the slow match that ignites the charge, occupy a rack opposite. The final items are three calibre-gauge sets, each consisting of twelve bronze discs of graduated bore diameter strung on a cord. Two of the three sets match the spring register's description. The third differs: its eleven-libbra disc is absent, replaced by what appears to be a spare of the ten-libbra calibre, giving it two discs of the same size and none for the eleven-libbra class, which renders it unreliable for measuring any piece of that calibre. You note the error. Under the sub-heading accessori, the spring register also lists a quantity of priming powder in two sealed leather containers. You find two sealed leather containers. The spring register notes that the containers were sealed by the foundry master on the third of March and are not to be opened for inventory purposes. You record them as present and sealed.

On the morning of the second day, before turning to the shot inventory, you return to the petriera grossa whose bore measurement did not match the register, and you conduct a second measurement using a different calibre-gauge set, the one from the main hall's end-of-second-arch hook, which is the original Arsenal gauge and is the one that Zuanne di Maffio regards as the authoritative instrument. The result of the second measurement is consistent with the first: the bore is approximately fourteen and one-half libbre veneziane, not sixteen. The two gauge sets agree. This confirms that the discrepancy is not a gauge error. You return to the register page for this piece and re-read the entire entry from the winter register of fourteen eighty-seven, which you have with you as a reference copy. The fourteen eighty-seven entry, written in the hand of Giacomo di Pietro and signed at the foot of the page in confirmation, records the following data for this piece: catalogue number forty-one in the petriera class, iron, grossa, bore measurement sixteen libbre veneziane stone, length of barrel from muzzle face to base of cascabel forty-eight Venetian inches, weight estimated at nine hundred and forty libbre veneziane, founder's mark absent, condition buono. Today, the piece before you carries the same catalogue number, forty-one, and the material is iron, and the classification grossa, and the barrel length from muzzle face to base of cascabel measures forty-eight Venetian inches, all of which are consistent with the fourteen eighty-seven entry. The founder's mark is absent, as recorded. The weight has not been re-measured today. The condition is buono, with surface corrosion not noted in the earlier record. The bore, however, does not agree: where the fourteen eighty-seven entry records sixteen libbre veneziane, the gauge applied this morning reads approximately fourteen and one-half. The barrel length match is notable. If the piece had been entirely swapped for a different piece, you would expect the length to differ as well, since the bore and the length of a piece are both fixed at the time of casting and would not independently vary. The fact that the length matches while the bore does not is consistent either with a measurement error in fourteen eighty-seven or with a re-lining of the bore — a repair technique available to the foundry that would reduce the bore diameter — applied at some point between fourteen eighty-seven and today, with no corresponding notation in the register. You add this observation to the marginal note already made, and cross-reference it to the foundry repair records, which will need to be searched as part of the Provveditori's eventual inquiry.

At the end of the second day, you compile the cannon hall section of the register: the full ordnance count by type and calibre, the condition gradings, the founder's mark status, the shot inventory, and the notations for each discrepancy. The total ordnance count for the cannon hall, as recorded in the completed section, is one hundred and twelve pieces, against the spring register's count of one hundred and thirteen — the missing falconetto, piece number fourteen, accounting for the single-piece difference. The condition breakdown is as follows: ninety-four pieces graded buono, fourteen pieces graded da riparare, four pieces graded da disarmare. The four pieces graded da disarmare are all iron, all of a calibre no longer matched by shot in the current inventory, and are noted as candidates for smelting in the next scheduled foundry run, which, according to a separate document in your satchel, is planned for the following March. The cannon hall section is sealed with a notation at the foot of the page: inventory of ordnance complete, day two of eleven, discrepancies as noted, queries outstanding as noted, to be transmitted to the Provveditori at the conclusion of the survey. The outstanding queries are two in number. The first: the transfer of falconetto number fourteen, if a transfer has occurred, requires documentation. The second: the bore measurement discrepancy in the petriera grossa, cross-referenced to the winter register of fourteen eighty-seven, requires a ruling from the Provveditori as to whether an amended record is appropriate or whether a formal discrepancy report — a separate document with its own submission process — is the correct procedure. Both queries are listed in your working notes. As of the fifth of November, fourteen hundred and ninety, neither has been submitted, both having to await the completion of the full register. You close the cannon hall section. The hall itself is locked behind you by Zuanne di Maffio, who returns the keys to the key register at the door of the scribes' room. Tomorrow the survey moves to the Tana.
