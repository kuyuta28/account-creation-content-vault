# Han Dynasty Household Registry Correction, 2 AD

## Angle / Hook
> In the second year of the Yuan Shi era, a county magistrate's clerk sat down to correct the household registry for Yangling county — seven thousand nine hundred and twelve names, each one cross-referenced against the previous count, and each discrepancy recorded in red ink on a separate list that no superior official ever asked to see.

## Target Audience
- Người xem muốn: ancient census methodology, the feel of endless name-lists, the bureaucracy of population counting
- Mood target: rhythmic, meditative, the sound of brush on bamboo slips

## Recurring Motifs (unresolved throughout)

1. **The woman listed as male (ward seven, entry 211)** — first noticed during the initial cross-reference in Part 1, marked with a dot in the margin. Reappears in Part 3 when the village head's report arrives. Confirmed as an error in Part 4 — corrected. Why it happened is not discussed. The error has persisted through three consecutive censuses without correction.
2. **The missing guanfu lower seal** — a transfer certificate in Part 2 missing its lower seal. Noted. No replacement arrives during the correction period. Listed again in Part 6 as "seal absent, verification pending." Never resolved.
3. **The eighteen "pending confirmation" entries** — out-migration entries in Part 3 with no matching receiving-county record. Noted. Still pending at submission. Listed in Part 6. No responses arrive.
4. **The double-count removal (three households)** — struck in the Year 1 correction and again in the supplementary adjustment. Noted as potential double-count in Part 3. Flagged for Provveditori review in Part 6. Not resolved.
5. **The household of nine** — nine persons registered under one name in Part 4. Village head's report appended. No resolution within the register. Still flagged in the correction list.

## Script Notes

- **POV:** Second-person (`you`). You are a hushucao — a low-level clerk responsible for household registers. You sit at a desk in the county office, working through the register slip by slip.
- **Tone:** Archival monotone. The pleasure is in the rhythm of repeated entries, not in any story. No commentary on the Han state's power or the lives behind the names. Names are data, not characters.
- **Pacing:** Slow. Each entry follows the template. Short fact → long contextual sentence about Han registration law → digression into a specific regulation or procedure → return to next entry. Repetitive by design. Callbacks: "As previously noted" to refer back to earlier entries without resolving them.
- **Tránh:** No stories about individual people — names are data, not characters. No drama about tax evasion — discrepancies are noted, not investigated. No comparison to modern China or the hukou system. No commentary on population control. No famine, war, or suffering (topic blacklist). The "double-count" discrepancy is noted, not resolved.
- **Language:** English
- **Từ đặc trưng / style:**
  - hushu (household register), hushucao (registry clerk), xiang (ward), li (village), lizheng (village head), guanfu (transfer certificate), gaibuzhuang (correction list), zhushu (red-ink correction), qing (unit of land ≈ 6.7 mu), zu (tax category), nong/gong/shang (farmer/artisan/merchant), sui (age-counting year), tianbu (land records), nan/nü (male/female).
  - Numbers always written out: "seven thousand nine hundred twelve" not "7,912."
  - Han dynasty place names kept in original: Yangling, Jingzhao, Mian — no modern equivalents needed.

## Notes

- **Scale**: Han dynasty census recorded ~59.6 million registered individuals in 2 AD. This episode focuses on one county's ~7,912 — a microcosm of the imperial system.
- **Bamboo slips (jian)**: Each slip held one column of text, 20-40 characters. A register of 7,912 entries would fill hundreds of slips, bound with cord in groups by ward.
- **Red ink (zhushu)**: Specifically for corrections and official annotations — black for original entries. Convention dates to Qin dynasty. The Qin standardised zhushu across all commanderies; the convention persists over 200 years later without significant change.
- **Han age counting**: Two systems — chronological age (born = 0) and sui (born = 1, +1 at each lunar new year). The 21 entries off by exactly one year are almost certainly sui-conversion errors, not real age discrepancies.
- **Tax category digression**: The nong (farmer) category requires a minimum landholding. A household classified as nong but holding no registered qing of land is a discrepancy that triggers a review — but the review is a separate process, not part of the census correction. The census correction notes it; it does not resolve it.
- **The nan/nü stroke difference**: The characters 男 (nan, male) and 女 (nü, female) differ significantly in form, but when written cursively on bamboo slips by a village head working from verbal report, misreading is possible — particularly if the head was told the sex of the household member but wrote the wrong character. A single stroke error is a known Han registration error type.
- **Key term**: hushu (household register) — the ancestor of the modern hukou system. This connection is NOT mentioned in the script per the "no comparison to modern China" rule.
- **County archive**: 67 existing bundles + 1 new = 68 bundles by the end. The final sentence is the shortest in the script: "Sixty-eight. That is all." — flat, definitive ending per the outro formula.
- **The shelf sagging**: A small physical detail in the outro — the shelf sags under the weight of 68 bundles. This is the last sensory detail before the final sentence. It grounds the ending in physical reality.
