On the eighth day, the birth notification slips arrive. They come in a leather satchel carried by the courier from the magistrate's office — the same courier who brought the commandery's copy on the first day, a man with a slight limp who does not speak. He hands you the satchel without ceremony and leaves. You open the satchel. Thirty-seven slips inside. Each one is a single bamboo strip, narrower than the register slips, cut from bamboo that is still slightly green — cut this year, not last, air-dried for a few weeks rather than dried over a fire. The surface is smoother and less absorbent than the register slips. The ink is visibly fresher, darker, slightly more fluid. You lay the slips on the desk. Each bears the village head's seal — a small circular stamp in red ink at the bottom, just below the last character. The seal is not the magistrate's seal, which is larger and rectangular. This is the village head's personal seal, specific to each li, supposed to be kept by the lizheng at all times, though the reliability of this arrangement varies from li to li. You arrange the slips by village. Eight villages in Yangling county, one for each ward. The slips from each village are tied together with thin hemp cord. The cord is a different colour for each village — green for Ward One, brown for Ward Two, and so on. This system was introduced by the magistrate's deputy two corrections ago. Whether it has improved the sorting process is a matter of some debate. The brown and the red used for Wards Two and Four are particularly easy to confuse in low light. You sort the slips. The ink on the newest ones is still slightly tacky to the touch.

The birth notification slips are, in theory, straightforward. Each slip records a single birth: the name of the child, the date of birth, the name of the head of household to whom the child belongs, the relationship of the child to the head, and the village head's seal. You are required to verify each birth against the county's birth register — a separate document kept in the archive room, a thin bamboo register of its own, bound with cord and stamped with the magistrate's seal, containing all births reported since the last correction. You retrieve the birth register from the archive. The cord is stiff with age. The seal is intact. You open it. The first entry corresponds to the first notification slip: a child named Chen Wei, born on the eighth day of the first month, son of Chen Shi, head of household in Ward One, entry one. You compare the notification slip against the birth register entry. Name, date, and head of household all match. The village head's seal is present. You set this slip aside in the pile of verified additions. The second notification: Li Fang, born on the fifteenth day of the first month, daughter of Li Wen, Ward Two. The birth register confirms it. The seal is present. The third, the fourth, the fifth — each matches, each is verified, each is set aside. The process acquires a rhythm. Comparison of two sets of characters on two different surfaces. One older, one newer. One on yellowed bamboo, one on green. The brush strokes of the village head are broader and less controlled than those of the county scribe. The seal is the same red circle at the bottom of every slip. The rhythm is not unpleasant.

Of the thirty-seven birth notification slips, thirty-four are straightforward. Names, dates, and household heads match the birth register. The village head's seal is present and legible. These slips can be added to the register without further inquiry. The remaining three require attention. The first irregularity is a slip from the village of Ward Five. It records the birth of a child named Wang Jiao, born on the third day of the seventh month, daughter of Wang Cheng, head of household. The birth register contains a matching entry. The name and date are correct. But the village head's seal on the notification slip is not the seal you recognize. The seal of the lizheng of Ward Five is a small circle with the character for five — wu — inside a border of dots. The seal on this slip is a different design entirely: a square, with no character inside, only a border of dots. It is possible that the village head of Ward Five has changed his seal since the last correction. This is not common, but it is not prohibited, and such a change should have been reported to the county office. No such report has been received. It is also possible that the slip was sealed by someone other than the village head — a deputy, perhaps, a practice that is not authorized but is known to occur in villages where the lizheng is elderly or infirm. You note the irregular seal on a working slip. You set the notification aside. It will be included in the addition pile with a notation. The correction list will note the irregularity for the magistrate's review.

The second irregular birth notification is from the village of Ward Three. It records the birth of a child named Zhao Min, born on the twentieth day of the fourth month, son of Zhao Gu, head of household. The birth register contains a matching entry. The name and date are correct. But the notification slip lists the head of household as Zhao Hai — Zhao Gu's brother — while the birth register lists Zhao Gu. The head of household cannot be both Zhao Gu and Zhao Hai unless the headship has changed between the time the birth was reported and the time the notification slip was prepared. This is possible. The fourth month is four months before the current correction period. A change of headship could have occurred in that time, particularly if the original head had died or moved away. The birth register does not record changes of headship. The notification slip does not explain the discrepancy. You note this on a working slip. The birth itself is confirmed by the birth register. The head of household field is inconsistent. It will require a verification slip from the village head. The third irregular notification is simpler. A slip from the village of Ward Seven — the same ward that contains entry two hundred and eleven, the entry you marked with a dot on the first day. The village head's seal is present and correct. The name and date match the birth register. The head of household is consistent between the two documents. But the notification slip is written on the reverse side of the bamboo — the smooth side rather than the prepared side. The ink bleeds slightly. Two of the characters are difficult to read with certainty. You note this as a minor defect. The birth is confirmed. The slip will be included in the additions, with a notation about the ink bleed.

### CHUNK audio-02-05
<!-- img: IMG-02-05 -->

The in-migration entries are twelve in number. They require a different process from the birth notifications. Each in-migration entry represents a household that has moved into Yangling county from elsewhere, and each must be accompanied by a transfer certificate — a guanfu. You lift the first guanfu from the pile. The document is heavier than a birth slip. It consists of two parts: an upper half and a lower half. The upper half is retained by the sending county. The lower half is carried by the migrating household to the receiving county, where it is presented as proof of previous registration and good standing. The guanfu system was established in the years following the founding of the Han dynasty, when the movement of population between counties was considerable and the need to track households as they moved was recognized as essential to the maintenance of the tax and conscription registers. The system is simple in concept. A household cannot register in a new county without presenting the lower half of a guanfu. A county cannot remove a household from its register without issuing the upper half. The movement of households is tracked at both ends. In practice, the system relies on the timely and accurate processing of the certificates by the scribes of both counties, on the reliability of the courier system for transmitting the upper halves to the commandery, and on the willingness of the migrating household to present the lower half within the reporting window. That window is three months. If the household fails to present the guanfu within three months of arrival, the receiving county is required to send a query to the sending county. If no response is received within a further three months, the household is registered as unattached, which carries a higher tax rate and restricted access to land allocation. You set the first guanfu down. You pick up the next.

Of the twelve in-migration entries, eight are from Mian county, the neighbouring county to the east. The remaining four are from more distant counties. Two from Duyang county to the south. One from Huayin county to the north. One from Chang'an county, which contains the imperial capital. You examine each guanfu in turn. The process: read the upper half, which contains the sending county's record of the household's registration — head of household, number of members, land held, tax category. Compare this against the lower half, which should contain the same information plus the sending county's seal. Compare both against the notification submitted by the household to the Yangling county office. The eight guanfu from Mian county are in good order. Seals present on both halves. Information matches. The bamboo is of a similar quality to the Yangling slips. The writing is in a hand you recognize from the previous correction — the Mian county scribe, who writes with a pronounced leftward lean to the vertical strokes. You set these eight aside. The two guanfu from Duyang are also in order, though the bamboo is thicker and lighter in colour. The guanfu from Huayin is in order. The guanfu from Chang'an is in order, with one difference: it is written on paper rather than bamboo. This is not unusual for Chang'an, where paper is more readily available. It does not affect the validity of the document, though it requires you to handle it more carefully. The ink on this particular document has smudged slightly at the top of the lower half, where the paper was apparently folded during transport and the still-damp ink transferred to the opposite side.

And then there is the matter of the missing lower seal. You pick up the Chang'an guanfu again. The upper half is complete. It bears the seal of the Chang'an county office — a rectangular stamp in red ink, notably more elaborate than the village head's seals on the birth notification slips, containing the name of the county, the name of the registering official, and the date of the transfer. The lower half contains all the required information. The writing is in the same hand as the upper half. The smudged ink at the top is consistent with the smudge on the upper half, suggesting both halves were written at the same time and folded together. But where the lower half should bear a seal, in the position corresponding to the seal on the upper half, there is only a faint impression in the paper. A depression. No visible ink mark. You examine this impression under the lamplight. It is circular, approximately the size of the village head's seal on the birth notification slips. It is in the correct position on the document. But there is no red ink. The seal was either applied without ink, or the ink has faded or been rubbed off, or the seal was never properly applied at all. You cannot determine which from the document itself. You note this on a working slip. Chang'an county guanfu. Lower seal absent. Impression present, no ink visible. Requires follow-up query. You set the guanfu aside in a separate pile, distinct from the verified additions and the irregular birth notifications. This is the second irregular document you have set aside. You will return to both during the compilation of the correction list. For now, the guanfu with the missing seal is set aside. You continue.

### CHUNK audio-02-08
<!-- img: IMG-02-08 -->

The guanfu in your hand reminds you of the previous correction, when a household moving from a county two hundred li away reported that its guanfu had been destroyed when the household's cart overturned in a stream crossing. The event was recorded in the correction list as "guanfu lost in transit, household self-reported, verification pending." You were told by the previous hushucao that the notation had still been pending when he transferred out. You did not follow up on it, because it was not within the scope of your assignment. The guanfu system, like the household registration system it supports, dates to the reign of the Emperor Gaozu, the founder of the Han dynasty. The previous Qin dynasty had imposed strict controls on household registration. Those controls were disrupted by the wars of the transition period. The guanfu was the solution. Each county was required to issue a two-part certificate to any household declaring its intention to move. The system, in theory, is foolproof. A household cannot register in a new county without the lower half. A county cannot remove a household from its register without issuing the upper half. Movement is tracked at both ends. But the system relies on the scribes of both counties processing the certificates promptly and accurately. It relies on the courier system transmitting the upper halves to the commandery on schedule. It relies on the migrating household presenting the lower half within the three-month window. Failures at any of these points result in the kind of irregularity you are now holding in your hand. A guanfu with a missing lower seal. A document that is technically incomplete. It cannot, by the strict letter of the regulation, be accepted as proof of the household's previous registration. The three-month window for this household has not yet expired. The household arrived in Yangling on the twenty-eighth day of the eighth month. That was eight days ago. The window expires on the twenty-eighth day of the eleventh month. There is time for a follow-up query to be sent to Chang'an, and for a response to be received, before the correction is due. You note this on the working slip. You set the guanfu aside.

It now contains ninety-one slips from the cross-referencing plus three new slips from the birth and migration review, for a total of ninety-four. The desk is clear. The lamp is lit. The eighth day is over. Tomorrow, you begin the removals.
