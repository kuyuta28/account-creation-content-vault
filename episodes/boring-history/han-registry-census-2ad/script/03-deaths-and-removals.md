### CHUNK audio-03-01

On the morning of the ninth day, you begin the removals. The removals are, in principle, the inverse of the additions. Where the additions require you to create new entries from notification slips and guanfu, the removals require you to strike existing entries from the register based on death reports and out-migration records. The process is different in one essential respect. The striking of an entry is performed in red ink. The red ink — zhushu, the same ink used for official corrections and annotations — is not the same as the black ink used for the original entries. It is made from cinnabar rather than pine soot, ground with the same animal glue binder but producing a different consistency. Thicker. Slightly sticky. The brush requires a different pressure to achieve a clean line. You prepare the red ink on a second ink stone, which you have brought from the archive room for this purpose. The stone is smaller than the black, and it is stained with years of cinnabar use — the surface a faint orange-pink that never fully cleans. You grind the ink stick. The colour is vivid. The smell is different from the black. Mineral rather than vegetable. Sharp rather than earthy. You test a line on a blank slip. The line is wide and even. The red is bright against the pale bamboo. You begin.

The death reports arrive in the same manner as the birth notifications — in a leather satchel, from the village heads, via the magistrate's courier. One hundred and sixty-seven death reports in total. The distribution by ward is uneven, as deaths tend to be. Ward One: twenty-one. Ward Two: eighteen. Ward Three: thirty-two — the highest count, which may reflect the larger population. Ward Four: nineteen. Ward Five: twenty-four. Ward Six: fifteen. Ward Seven: twenty-two. Ward Eight: sixteen. You begin with Ward One. The first death report: Chen Bo, age nineteen sui, son of Chen Shi, Ward One, entry two. The same Chen Bo who appeared in the second entry of the register, the son of the first head of household. The death register confirms: Chen Bo, died on the fourth day of the third month. Cause not recorded. The death register does not record causes — only names, dates, and the reporting village head's seal. You take up the register. You turn to Ward One, entry two. The slip is in good condition. The ink is clear. The characters are small and precise. You draw a single red line through the entry, from top to bottom, crossing all six fields with a single stroke. The red line is wider than any of the characters it crosses. It is unmistakable. Chen Bo is struck. The entry remains on the slip. It is not erased, not removed, not replaced. But the red line declares it void. You write the corrected count in the margin of the working slip. Ward One, total living entries, eight hundred and forty-two. The brush feels different in your hand when you use the red ink. The strokes are broader. You must hold the brush at a slightly different angle to achieve a clean line. The adjustment takes several entries to become natural.

You work through the death reports for all eight wards over the course of the ninth and tenth days. The process is the same for each entry. Read the death report. Verify against the death register. Locate the entry in the register. Strike it with a red line. Write the corrected count in the working notes. The rhythm is different from the cross-referencing of the first seven days. Where the cross-referencing required you to compare two documents line by line, the striking requires you to find a specific entry and mark it. A simpler action, but one that carries a different weight. The red line on the bamboo is a permanent and visible alteration to a document that has been, until this moment, unchanged since the last correction. The one hundred and sixty-seven deaths are distributed across the eight wards in the numbers previously noted. Ward One, after twenty-one entries struck, has eight hundred and twenty-two living entries. Ward Two, after eighteen, has seven hundred and twenty-one. Ward Three, after thirty-two, has one thousand and seventy-two. The total living count, after all death entries have been struck, is seven thousand seven hundred and forty-five — one hundred and sixty-seven fewer than the original seven thousand nine hundred and twelve. This figure is temporary. The in-migration additions will increase it. The out-migration removals will decrease it further. The final count will be calculated at the end. You clean the red ink stone. The water runs pink. You dry the stone with a cloth. The stain on your fingers has dried to a faint pink that will not wash off with water alone. It will remain for several days, marking your hands as the hands of a scribe who has been striking entries from a register. The other scribes in the county office will recognise the mark without comment. They have all performed this duty at one time or another.

### CHUNK audio-03-04
<!-- img: IMG-03-04 -->

The out-migration entries are fifty-two in number. They present a different challenge from the deaths. A death is a death. The death register confirms it, the village head reports it, and the entry is struck. An out-migration is a departure, and a departure is not always final or confirmed. The fifty-two entries represent households that have left Yangling county since the last correction. Each departure is documented by a guanfu — the same transfer certificate used for in-migration, but in reverse. Yangling county issues the upper half. The departing household carries the lower half to the receiving county. You retrieve the upper halves from the archive room. They are bound in a single bundle tied with white cord. White for departure records. Green for birth notifications. The colour-coding system, like the colour-coding of the village head's seals, was introduced by the magistrate's deputy two corrections ago. You check each upper half against the register entry for the departing household. All fifty-two are consistent — the name, number of members, land held, and tax category all match between the guanfu and the register. The next step is to check whether the receiving county has processed the corresponding lower half. This check is performed by consulting the commandery's copy of the receiving county's register, which should contain a note indicating that the household has been added, with a cross-reference to the guanfu number. You check each of the fifty-two entries against the commandery's records. Of the fifty-two, thirty-four have corresponding entries in the receiving counties' records. These are confirmed departures. The entries can be struck without reservation. The remaining eighteen have no corresponding entry. Eighteen out of fifty-two. You note this.

The eighteen unconfirmed departures are flagged as "pending confirmation" on the working slips. The reason for the absence of a corresponding entry may be one of several. The household may not have arrived at its destination yet. The receiving county may have processed the guanfu but not yet transmitted the record to the commandery. The household may have presented itself to the wrong county office. Or the household may not have presented itself at all, in which case its members are technically unattached — outside the registration system and subject to the penalties that apply to unattached persons. None of these possibilities can be determined from the documents currently available to you. The commandery's records are updated on the same ten-day courier schedule as the county's. Some of the eighteen entries may appear in the next update, which is due in four days. You note on each of the eighteen working slips: pending confirmation, check next commandery update. If the entries are confirmed in the next update, they will be struck. If they are not confirmed, they will remain in the register with a notation on the correction list. The matter will be referred to the magistrate's office for a follow-up query to the receiving county. The striking of the thirty-four confirmed departures proceeds as with the deaths. You draw a red line through each entry. You write the corrected count. You set the guanfu upper halves aside in a bundle tied with white cord. The total number of removals from the register is now one hundred and sixty-seven deaths plus thirty-four confirmed departures, for a total of two hundred and one entries struck. The eighteen pending departures are not struck. They remain in the register, with a notation in the margin of the working slip indicating their status. The red ink on the confirmed removals is a slightly different shade from the red ink on the death entries. The death entries were struck over two days, during which the ink stick was ground twice. The second grinding produced a slightly darker shade. The confirmed departures are struck on the third day, with a fresh grinding that is closer to the first day's colour. The difference is small but visible to any future reader who examines the ink closely.

### CHUNK audio-03-06
<!-- img: IMG-03-06 -->

When a household is removed from the register, the land held by that household reverts to the county for reallocation. This is a fundamental principle of the Han land system, and it applies to all categories of land except privately owned land, which is a small but growing category in this period and which passes by inheritance rather than reverting to the county. The majority of land recorded in the register is allocated land — land granted by the county to the household for cultivation, in exchange for tax and labour. This land, when the household that held it is removed, becomes available for reallocation. The reallocation process is not automatic. It requires a separate petition from the county magistrate to the commandery, requesting authorisation to reallocate the land to one or more existing households or to a new household that has recently arrived. The petition must specify the qing amount, the location of the land, the identity of the proposed recipient, and the reason for the reallocation. The commandery reviews the petition. If approved, it issues an authorisation that is returned to the county, recorded in the tianbu, and the household register is updated accordingly. The process takes, on average, between three and six months. During that time, the land lies fallow unless the county permits a temporary cultivation arrangement, which is a matter of local discretion. The land held by the two hundred and one households that have been struck during this correction amounts to approximately four hundred and thirty-seven qing. A significant amount — enough to support roughly sixty-five new households at the standard allocation. The preparation of the reallocation petitions is not your responsibility. Your responsibility ends with the striking of the entries and the recording of the corrected count. You turn to the next working slip.

On the afternoon of the tenth day, while you are reviewing the out-migration entries for the second time — checking the eighteen pending entries against the commandery's copy one more time, in case any have been added since your first review — you notice a discrepancy of a different kind. Three of the fifty-two out-migration entries appear to have been struck from the register twice. Once in the Yuan Shi Year 1 correction, which is the correction you are currently performing. And once in a supplementary adjustment made in the fourth month of the previous year, which is an adjustment you were not told about and that is not documented in the instructions you received from the magistrate's deputy on the first day. The supplementary adjustment was a mid-year correction made to account for a group of households that departed Yangling in the spring. The entries for these households were struck in the supplementary adjustment. Then, when the current correction cycle began, the same entries were struck again — or, more precisely, a second red line was drawn through entries that already bore a first red line from the supplementary adjustment. The result is three entries in the register that now bear two red lines each. The first line, drawn in the fourth month, used a different batch of cinnabar, which produces a colour that is more orange than the current batch. The second line, drawn by you in the past two days, is more red. The difference is small but visible. Three households have been counted as removed twice. This will reduce the total living count by three more than it should, unless the double-strike is identified and corrected. You note this on a working slip. Three entries double-struck. First strike supplementary adjustment fourth month. Second strike current correction. Potential double-count. Flag for review. This is the fourth irregularity you have found. The irregular seal on the birth notification. The Chang'an guanfu with the missing lower seal. The eighteen pending confirmation entries. And now the three double-count entries.

### CHUNK audio-03-08

Also on the tenth day, a second satchel arrives from the magistrate's office. This one contains the village heads' reports. These are not the same as the birth and death notification slips — those are event-specific, reporting a single birth or death. The village heads' reports are periodic assessments of the households within each li, submitted at the request of the county office in preparation for the census correction. Each report contains a list of all households in the village, with notes on any changes since the last report — changes of headship, changes of occupation, changes of land holding, and, in some cases, notes on irregularities that the village head wishes to bring to the county's attention. You receive the satchel from the courier. You open it. The reports are written on bamboo slips, bound by ward, each bundle stamped with the village head's seal. You set the reports aside for now. You will consult them during the age and sex discrepancy review, which is the next phase, and during the tax category review, which follows that. But before you set the reports aside entirely, you check one thing. You turn to the report for Ward Seven. You open the bundle. You scan the entries for entry two hundred and eleven. The report for this household is present. It reads: Zhang Nü, head of household, age thirty-one sui, female, occupation nong, land held two qing, no changes since last report, no irregularities noted by village head. You read this entry again. The village head's report lists the sex as female. Nü. The register lists the sex as male. Nan. The village head, who is responsible for the original registration of households in his li, has reported the sex as female. The register, which was compiled from the village head's original report three censuses ago, lists the sex as male. The error, whatever its cause, was introduced during the original registration. It has been carried forward through three consecutive censuses without correction. You set the report aside. You will return to this entry in the age and sex discrepancy review. The dot you placed in the margin on the first day has not been forgotten.
