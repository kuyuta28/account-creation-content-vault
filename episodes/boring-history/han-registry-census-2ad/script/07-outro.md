### CHUNK audio-07-01

You take up your brush. The ink is ready. You write the summary report on a single wide bamboo slip, longer than the standard slip, in the fixed format prescribed by the commandery. The dates of the correction: from the third day of the ninth month to the seventeenth day of the ninth month, in the second year of the Yuan Shi era. The name of the scribe conducting the correction — your own name, written in the formal hand used for signed documents. The name of the magistrate's deputy, who reviewed the sample. The total number of entries in the register at the beginning of the correction: seven thousand nine hundred and twelve. The total number of new entries added: forty-nine. Thirty-seven births. Twelve in-migration households. The total number of entries removed: two hundred and one. One hundred and sixty-seven deaths. Thirty-four confirmed departures. The total number of corrections made: three hundred and nine, as recorded on the gaibuzhuang. The total number of pending items: nineteen. Eighteen out-migration entries pending confirmation. One guanfu with a missing lower seal. The total number of entries in the corrected register: seven thousand nine hundred and twelve minus two hundred and one plus forty-nine, equal to seven thousand seven hundred and sixty. This figure does not include the eighteen pending entries, which, if confirmed, would reduce the total to seven thousand seven hundred and forty-two — a figure that is noted as provisional. You write each of these figures in black ink on the summary slip. The characters are small and even. The columns are aligned with the edge of the slip. The brush strokes are precise and controlled, the way you were trained to write official documents, the way the previous hushucao wrote them, the way every scribe in every county office in the empire writes them. The format is prescribed. The figures must be legible and unambiguous. A scribe who writes the wrong figure on a summary slip will be held accountable for the error, as the previous hushucao was held accountable for an error in the previous correction — a single incorrect figure in the total count, discovered by the commandery scribe during the review, which required a supplementary correction to be submitted, a process that delayed the final submission by three days, and which the previous hushucao mentioned to you as a cautionary example when he handed over the correction duties. He said, in the only personal remark he made during the entire handover, that a scribe's worst enemy is not the ink or the bamboo but his own carelessness with figures. You check each figure on the summary slip a second time.

### CHUNK audio-07-02

The binding of the corrected register takes the remainder of the fourteenth day and the morning of the fifteenth. For each entry that has been struck from the register, you replace the original slip with a new slip on which the corrected text is written in black ink, with the original text struck through in red ink above it. The original slips — the ones that have been struck — are not discarded. They are retained in a separate bundle, marked "superseded" in black ink on the binding cord, and placed alongside the gaibuzhuang and the summary report as part of the complete submission. The new slips, written on the blank slips from the cahier that you received on the first day, are inserted into the register in place of the original slips, bound with the same cord, and sealed with the magistrate's seal. The household of nine in Ward Eight, entry five hundred and ninety-three, is corrected by adding eight new slips immediately after the head's entry. Each of the eight slips contains the information from the village head's report — name, age, relationship, occupation — and the note "household of nine" on the head's slip is struck through with a red line. The eight new slips are bound into the register in the same manner as the other slips, with new cord and a new seal on the Ward Eight bundle. Entry two hundred and eleven in Ward Seven — the woman listed as male — is corrected by striking through the character nan in the sex field with a red line and writing the character nü above it in red ink. The red line and the red character are visible and unambiguous. They declare the correction to any future reader. The loose cord on the Ward Seven bundle, which you noticed on the first day, is re-tied with a new cord, tighter than the old one, and sealed with the magistrate's seal, which is applied by the deputy in the afternoon of the fourteenth day. The binding is complete. The corrected register is now a document of seven bundles, one for each ward, each bound with cord and sealed, with the gaibuzhuang as a separate bundle, the summary report as a single slip, and the bundle of superseded slips tied with white cord and marked "superseded" in black ink. The total number of documents in the submission is ten. Seven ward bundles. One gaibuzhuang. One summary report. One bundle of superseded slips.

### CHUNK audio-07-03

On the fifteenth day, the courier arrives from Jingzhao. The courier is not the same man who brought the commandery's copy on the first day — that courier had a limp and did not speak. This one is younger, with no limp, and he does speak, though only to confirm the receipt of the documents and to ask whether the submission includes all ten bundles, which you confirm that it does. The courier loads the ten bundles into a leather satchel. He seals the satchel with the magistrate's seal — a different seal from the one on the register bundles, this one being the county's official seal rather than the magistrate's personal seal — and departs. You receive a receipt from the courier, stamped with the county seal, confirming that the ten bundles were received by the courier on the fifteenth day of the ninth month, in the second year of the Yuan Shi era, and will be delivered to the commandery office in Jingzhao within one day. The ink on the receipt is still wet. You place the receipt on the desk. The ink dries as you watch it. The desk is now empty, except for the receipt, the ink stone, the water vial, and the two reed quills, which are worn down to the point where they will need to be recut before they can be used again. You gather the working slips — the two hundred and six slips that record every discrepancy, every note, every observation made during the fourteen days of the correction — and you place them in a separate bundle, which you do not seal, because the instructions specify that working notes are not to be preserved with the register. You will destroy them after the submission is complete. The old register — the previous year's corrected register, which has been on the desk for fourteen days and which you have used as the basis for the current correction — is placed in the county archive room, on the shelf next to the sixty-seven other bundles that represent the previous corrections. Each one a register like the one you have just completed. Each one containing the household entries of Yangling county for a single year, corrected and submitted and filed and, in most cases, never opened again. You place the old register on the shelf. The shelf sags slightly under the weight of the sixty-eighth bundle. The next census correction is due in three years, in the fifth year of the Yuan Shi era. The next hushucao may be you, or it may be someone else. The ink on the receipt is dry. The scribes' room is empty. The oil lamp is unlit. The door sticks when you push it, as it did on the first day. The courtyard is quiet. Sixty-eight. That is all.
