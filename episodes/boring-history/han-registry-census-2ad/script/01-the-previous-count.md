You read the first entry. Ward One, entry one: head of household, Chen Shi, age forty-two sui, relationship self, occupation nong, land held three qing, tax category nong. The commandery's copy reads the same. You place a small mark in the margin of your working notes. You will not write a marginal mark for every consistent entry — only for the discrepancies. This is the practice established by the previous hushucao and confirmed by the magistrate's deputy at the beginning of the previous correction. The second entry: Chen Bo, age nineteen sui, relationship son, occupation none, land held none, tax category dependent. The commandery's copy reads the same. The third entry: Chen Nü, age sixteen sui, relationship daughter, occupation none, land held none, tax category dependent. The same. You continue through the first ten entries without finding a discrepancy. The bamboo slips of the county register are a slightly different colour from those of the commandery's copy. The county slips are more yellow. The commandery slips are more pale. The county's slips are cut from bamboo grown in the Yangling area. The commandery's slips come from the imperial store in Chang'an. The difference in soil and climate produces a difference in colour that has no effect on the writing surface but allows you to distinguish the two registers at a glance. The eleventh entry of Ward One presents the first discrepancy. The county register records the age as thirty-seven sui. The commandery's copy records thirty-six sui. The difference is one year. You note this. It is the first of what will be many such discrepancies. You set it aside in your mind as a category to be examined later.

### CHUNK audio-01-02
<!-- img: IMG-01-02 -->

You work through the remaining entries of Ward One at roughly forty entries per hour. This pace you have found sustainable over the previous correction. It allows sufficient time to examine each entry carefully without losing concentration, though by the third hour the concentration does begin to slip. The characters blur slightly at the edges. The brush strokes of the original scribe become harder to distinguish from the grain of the bamboo. You find that you must look away from the slips for a moment every hundred entries or so. You look at the wall of the scribes' room instead. The wall is bare except for a small wooden shelf holding two ink stones that are not yours, and a crack in the rammed earth that runs from the base to approximately the height of your head. The crack has been there since before your first assignment. It shows no sign of widening or being repaired. By the end of the first day, you have completed Ward One and Ward Two. A total of one thousand five hundred and seventy-one entries. The discrepancies: eleven age differences of one year, three age differences of two years, two land-holding figures that differ by one qing, one occupation field that reads gong in the county register but nong in the commandery copy. And one entry — entry nine hundred and three, in Ward Two — where the relationship field reads wife in the county register but mother in the commandery copy. This discrepancy cannot be resolved from the register alone. It will require a verification slip from the village head. You note each discrepancy on a separate working slip. The pile contains eighteen slips by the end of the day. The light in the scribes' room has shifted from the east window to the south. The afternoon sun reaches the desk only as a narrow band across the bamboo. The characters in that band of light are briefly illuminated. Then the sun moves further. The room returns to the even grey of ambient daylight.

The second day begins with Ward Three. This is the largest ward, at one thousand one hundred and four entries. The slips for Ward Three are bound in two bundles — entries one through six hundred, and entries six hundred and one through one thousand one hundred and four. A single bundle of more than six hundred slips becomes difficult to handle. The cord stretches under the weight. The slips shift out of alignment. The corrected register must be usable as a reference document between census cycles. That is why the larger wards are split. The cord on the first bundle of Ward Three is tighter than the cord on Ward One. You work the knot with the tip of your knife for several moments before it loosens. The slips are in good condition. The ink is even and consistent throughout, suggesting that the original entries for this ward were written by a single scribe in a single session, or in sessions close enough together that the ink composition did not vary. The first two hundred entries contain no discrepancies. This is unusual. In Wards One and Two, discrepancies appeared within the first fifty entries. You begin to wonder whether the scribe who wrote the commandery's copy for Ward Three was particularly careful, or whether the county scribe was, or whether the ward itself has simply had fewer changes since the last full census. You continue. The absence of discrepancies is not a guarantee of accuracy. Both copies may contain the same errors.

### CHUNK audio-01-04
<!-- img: IMG-01-04 -->

The bamboo slips arrive at this office through a process you know well, because you have performed this duty for three years and because the previous hushucao explained the process to you when he handed over the correction work. Bamboo stalks of approximately two fingers' width are cut in the autumn. They are split lengthwise into strips and dried over a slow fire for several days to remove the natural moisture and to straighten the curve that green bamboo retains after cutting. The drying produces the characteristic smell of the archive room — heated grass and mineral, faintly sweet, persistent. You grind the ink on the stone. The smell rises. Once dried, the slips are scraped smooth on the writing surface with a bone knife. Two holes are bored near the top for the binding cord. The slips are bundled in groups of twenty and sent to the county office, where they are stored until needed. When a register is to be compiled, the scribe selects the required number of slips from the store, unbinds them, and writes directly on the prepared surface. The ink — pine soot and animal glue, ground with water on the ink stone — adheres to the bamboo without soaking through. Once dry, it is permanent. It cannot be erased. It can only be struck through with a line. This is the method used for entries that are to be removed from the register. The transport of the completed register between the county office and the commandery in Jingzhao follows a fixed schedule. Once every ten days. The register is wrapped in oiled cloth and placed in a leather satchel sealed with the magistrate's clay seal. A courier carries it on foot to Jingzhao in approximately one day. The return of the commandery's copy follows the same route in reverse, on the same schedule. The seal on the commandery copy that you are using is intact. It has never been broken in transit, at least not in your experience, though the previous hushucao mentioned one instance in his time when a satchel arrived with a cracked seal. The crack was caused, it was concluded, by the impact of the satchel against the courier's pack frame on a rough section of the road near the Wei River crossing. The inquiry lasted several weeks. It produced no finding of tampering. The integrity of the seal is the only guarantee that the register has not been altered between the county and the commandery. Any crack introduces doubt that the correction itself cannot resolve. The seal on your copy is intact. You return to the slips.

By midday of the second day, you have reached entry four hundred and twelve of Ward Three. The first discrepancy in this ward appears here. The county register records the land held as four qing. The commandery's copy records three qing. The difference is one qing. This is not a trivial amount. One qing is approximately six point seven mu — enough land to support a household of four in most parts of the Guanzhong plain. The difference between three qing and four qing may affect the tax assessment. The nong rate for more than three qing is one rate. The rate for three qing or less is different and lower. You cannot determine from the register alone which figure is correct. The land records, kept separately in the tianbu, will be consulted later, during the tax category review. The tianbu is the authoritative source for land-holding data. You note the discrepancy and continue. The afternoon of the second day yields three further discrepancies in Ward Three. Another age difference of one year. A second land-holding difference. One entry where the occupation reads shang in the county register but nong in the commandery copy — the opposite of the discrepancy found in Ward Two. This will also require a verification slip. You finish the first bundle of Ward Three by the end of the second day. The working slip pile now contains twenty-five slips. The afternoon light has gone. The room is lit by a single oil lamp, which you have lit without remembering when. The lamp gives a warm yellow light that falls on the desk and does not reach the walls.

The third day. You open the second bundle of Ward Three — entries six hundred and one through one thousand one hundred and four. The cord on this bundle is also tight. You cut it with your knife. The slips are slightly thinner than those in the first bundle. The bamboo was cut from younger stalks. The walls of the culm are thinner, and the writing surface is slightly more absorbent. The ink tends to spread a fraction more at the edges of each stroke, giving the characters a softer appearance than the sharp, precise strokes of the first bundle. You begin at entry six hundred and one. The commandery's copy for this section is in the same hand as the first section — consistent, even. But the county register for entries six hundred and one through seven hundred and thirty appears to have been written by a different scribe. The brush strokes are heavier. The characters are slightly larger. There is a small but consistent habit of leaving more space between the columns. A change of scribe within a ward is not prohibited, but it is unusual. The reason for the change is not recorded in the register itself. You note the difference in handwriting as an observation. It does not affect the content. The first fifty entries of this section contain three discrepancies. Two age differences. One relationship field that reads brother in the county register but uncle in the commandery copy. The relationship-field discrepancy can only be resolved by asking the household, which is not within the scope of the census correction. The correction can only note that the two copies differ and flag the entry for further inquiry. You note all three. The morning passes. Someone outside is sweeping the courtyard again. The sound is regular and unhurried.

Ward Four, Ward Five, and Ward Six occupy the fourth and fifth days. The work is consistent and without incident. The discrepancies accumulate. Ward Four produces fourteen discrepancies in nine hundred and seven entries. The most common are age differences of one year, which appear eight times. Land-holding differences appear three times. Ward Five produces nineteen discrepancies in eight hundred and sixty-two entries. Two entries in Ward Five show a discrepancy in the entire household structure — the county register listing five members, the commandery copy listing three. This suggests either a transcription error on one side or an amendment made to one copy after the other was sent. It cannot be resolved without consulting the village head's report, which you have not yet received. You note both. Ward Six produces twelve discrepancies in nine hundred and seventy-one entries. The lowest count so far. The entries in this ward are notably well-written. The characters are small and precise. The columns are evenly spaced. The bamboo slips are of a uniform pale colour that matches the commandery's copy almost exactly, making it slightly more difficult to distinguish the two registers at a glance. This is a minor inconvenience. You always verify the entry number before comparing the fields. By the end of the fifth day, you have processed four thousand four hundred and twenty-two entries across six wards. You have noted seventy discrepancies on working slips. The pile is now thick enough to require a second pile, which you begin on the left side of the desk, arranged in order by ward and entry number. The lamp has been lit every evening this week. The oil has been refilled once by the deputy, who came in the morning and did not speak.

Ward Seven. Nine hundred and eighty-four entries. It is in this ward that the first of the recurring discrepancies appears — the one that will occupy your attention at several points during the correction and that will, in the end, be confirmed as an error and corrected, though the reason for the error will not be discussed in the register, because the reason is not within the scope of the census correction. You are working through the entries in the usual manner, comparing each entry in the county register against the corresponding entry in the commandery copy, when you reach entry two hundred and eleven. The entry reads: head of household, Zhang Nü, age thirty-one sui, relationship self, occupation nong, land held two qing, tax category nong. The commandery's copy reads the same. The two copies are identical. The discrepancy is not between the two copies. The discrepancy is within the entry itself. The name Zhang Nü is a woman's name. The character nü means female. It is a common element in women's names of this period. But the sex field — the third character of the second column — reads nan, meaning male. The name says female. The sex field says male. You look at the entry a second time. You look at the commandery's copy. The commandery's copy also reads nan in the sex field. Both copies agree that this person is male. The name says otherwise. You consider the possibility that Zhang Nü is a man's name that happens to contain the character for female. This is not impossible — there are men's names that use nü in a different context, as part of a compound or a reference. But the construction of this name, with nü standing alone as the personal name, is consistent with the naming conventions for women and inconsistent with the conventions for men in this region and period. You place a small dot in the margin of your working slip, next to entry two hundred and eleven. You do not correct it. You do not strike it. You note it. The dot is barely visible. You will find it again when you return to this entry later in the correction.

You continue through Ward Seven after entry two hundred and eleven. The remaining entries of this ward produce seven further discrepancies, all of the types you have seen before. Age differences. A land-holding variance. One entry where the occupation reads gong in the county register and nong in the commandery copy. As previously noted in the case of the Ward Two and Ward Three discrepancies of the same kind, this will require a verification slip from the village head. The rate of discrepancies in Ward Seven is slightly lower than the average across the first six wards. This may be a coincidence. It may reflect the quality of the original registration work in this ward. Without access to the scribe who wrote the original entries, you cannot determine the cause. The handwriting in Ward Seven is similar in style to the second half of Ward Three — heavier strokes, larger characters, more space between columns. The bamboo slips are of the same thinner variety, suggesting that both sections were cut from the same batch and written by the same scribe. This is an inference. It is not a certainty. The cord on the Ward Seven bundle, which you noticed on the first day was slightly loose, has not caused any displacement of the slips. The entries are in the correct order. But the looseness means that the slips shift slightly when you turn them, requiring you to hold the bundle with one hand while comparing the entries with the other. This slows your pace by a small but noticeable amount. You finish Ward Seven on the afternoon of the sixth day. The light through the south window is bright and direct for the first time since you began. The rain from the first night has not returned. The courtyard is dry. The sweeping sound has stopped.

### CHUNK audio-01-10

Ward Eight. The final ward. Seven hundred and thirty-six entries, the smallest of the eight. The entries are written in a hand that is different from all previous wards — smaller, more cramped, the characters packed tightly into the columns, as if the scribe was trying to fit more information onto each slip than the standard format allowed. Each slip contains only the standard six fields and no additional data. The reason for the cramped style is unclear. The commandery's copy for this ward is in the same neat, even hand as the rest of the commandery document. The scribe in Jingzhao apparently has a consistent style regardless of the source material. You work through Ward Eight on the morning of the seventh day. The discrepancies in this ward are eight in number. One of them is of a type you have not seen before. Entry five hundred and ninety-three records a household with nine members. But the entry lists only the head of household — name, age, relationship self, occupation nong, land held five qing, tax category nong — and provides no data for the remaining eight members. The slips for the other members should follow the head's entry in sequence. That is the standard format for multi-member households. Each member is recorded on a separate slip, with the relationship field indicating the connection to the head. In this case, the entry simply states, in a note written below the head's entry in a slightly different ink, "household of nine." Nothing further. The commandery's copy contains the same note. Both copies agree that there are nine members and that only the head is named. This is not a discrepancy between the two copies. It is a deficiency in the registration itself. You note it on a working slip with a more detailed annotation than usual. Ward Eight, entry five hundred and ninety-three. Household of nine. Only head recorded. No member data. Both copies consistent. You will return to this entry later, when the village head's report arrives. You finish Ward Eight by midday of the seventh day. The cross-referencing of the entire register — seven thousand nine hundred and twelve entries — is complete. The pile of working slips contains ninety-one slips. The lamp is unlit. The room is warm. The seventh day ends.
