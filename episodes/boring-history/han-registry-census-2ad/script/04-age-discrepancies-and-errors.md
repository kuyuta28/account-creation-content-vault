### CHUNK audio-04-01

The age and sex discrepancy review begins on the eleventh day. You lift the first working slip from the pile. This phase requires the most careful attention, because the discrepancies in these fields are not always obvious. An age that is off by one year may be a genuine error or may be the result of a difference in counting conventions. A sex field that is incorrect may be a transcription error or may reflect a deliberate misregistration. The distinction between these possibilities is not always clear from the register alone. You sort the forty-three age discrepancies by type. Twenty-one of them are off by exactly one year. This is a pattern you recognise from the previous correction, from the training you received from the previous hushucao, and from the notes in the margin of his working slips, which were passed to you along with the register. The explanation is straightforward. The Han dynasty recognises two methods of counting age. The first is the sui system, used in the county register. A person is considered one sui at birth and gains one sui at each lunar new year, regardless of the actual date of birth. The second is the chronological system, used in the commandery's copy. A person's age is counted from the date of birth and increases by one year on each subsequent date of birth. The difference between these two systems is exactly one year for most of the year, and either zero or one year during the brief period between the lunar new year and the individual's specific date of birth. The commandery's copy, transcribed by a scribe in Jingzhao who was trained in the chronological system, records ages in chronological years for most entries. But twenty-one of those entries were not converted at all. The ages in these entries are simply copied from the county register without adjustment. The commandery copy shows the sui age where it should show the chronological age. The discrepancy is exactly one year.

### CHUNK audio-04-02
<!-- img: IMG-04-02 -->

You note these twenty-one entries on a working slip. Age discrepancy, one year, sui conversion error. Both copies consistent with sui counting. No correction required. This notation means that the entries are not errors in the ordinary sense. The information in the register is correct, and the information in the commandery's copy is also correct, but expressed in a different counting system. The correction is not to change the age in either document, but to note the discrepancy on the correction list so that future scribes will be aware that the two documents use different counting conventions for these entries. You set these twenty-one entries aside in a pile marked "sui conversion, no change." The remaining age discrepancies — the ones that are not off by exactly one year — are more serious. Nine entries where the age difference is two years or more. Thirteen entries where the difference is in the sex field rather than the age field. You will deal with these separately. But first, the sui system. The previous hushucao's notes on this subject contain an observation worth recording, even though it is not directly relevant to the correction. The observation is this: the problem of sui conversion was recognized by the commandery office at least as early as the reign of the Emperor Cheng, when a directive was issued requiring all commandery copies of county registers to use the chronological system. This directive was never consistently enforced. As a result, some commandery copies use the chronological system and some use the sui system, depending on the preference of the scribe who performed the transcription. The copy you have in front of you uses the chronological system for most entries but the sui system for twenty-one of them. The scribe who transcribed it began by converting the ages and then, at some point, stopped converting them. Perhaps the conversion was tedious. Perhaps the scribe was not trained in the chronological system. Perhaps the scribe simply forgot. The reason is not recorded. The result is twenty-one entries that are off by one year.

The nine entries where the age difference is two years or more require a different process. For each of these entries, you must prepare a separate verification slip — a narrow bamboo strip on which you write the entry number, the name of the head of household, the age as recorded in the county register, the age as recorded in the commandery's copy, and a request for the village head to confirm the correct age. These verification slips will be sent to the village heads of the relevant li. The village heads will return them with the confirmed age, which you will then use to determine which document is correct and what correction, if any, is needed. The nine entries are distributed across five wards. The largest discrepancy is in Ward Four, entry six hundred and seventeen, where the county register records the age as forty-two sui and the commandery's copy records it as thirty-eight. A difference of four years. The smallest is in Ward One, entry one hundred and nine, where the difference is two years — forty-one sui in the county register, thirty-nine in the commandery copy. The remaining seven entries fall between these extremes, with differences of two, three, or four years. You prepare the nine verification slips, writing each one in black ink on a narrow bamboo strip with the village head's name, the entry number, and the age discrepancy. The slips are tied in a bundle and set aside for the courier, who will deliver them to the village heads tomorrow. You do not expect all nine to be returned before the end of the correction period. The village heads are not always prompt. Some of the villages are a day's walk from the county office. A round trip for a verification slip can take three days or more. You note on the working slips which entries are awaiting verification and which village heads have been contacted. The nine slips are placed in a bundle tied with yellow cord. Yellow for verification requests. Green for birth notifications. White for departure records. Red for corrections. This colour-coding system was introduced by the magistrate's deputy two corrections ago. Its usefulness, like that of the village head's seal colours, is a matter of some debate. The brown cord used for Ward Two and the red cord used for corrections are easily confused in low light — a problem that has not been addressed, because the deputy who introduced the system has since been transferred to another county and his successor has not seen fit to modify it.

The sex field discrepancies number thirteen. Of these, twelve are errors in which the sex field in one document or the other is incorrect — a male listed as female, or a female listed as male. The thirteenth is the entry you have been carrying in your mind since the first day: Ward Seven, entry two hundred and eleven, the entry with the dot in the margin. You begin with the twelve routine discrepancies. Ten of them are straightforward. The county register and the commandery's copy disagree on the sex of the head of household, and the village head's report — available since the tenth day — confirms one version or the other. In all ten cases, the village head's report agrees with the county register, and the commandery's copy is in error. This is consistent with the pattern you have observed throughout the correction. The commandery copy, transcribed by a scribe who had no way to verify the information beyond what was written on the slips, contains more transcription errors than the county register, which was compiled by scribes with access to the village heads' original reports. The corrections for these ten entries are simple. The commandery's copy will be noted as erroneous. The county register will stand as the authoritative version. You write the corrections on working slips and set them aside. The eleventh sex field discrepancy is more unusual. Ward Five, entry seven hundred and forty-two. The county register lists the head of household as female. The commandery's copy lists the head of household as male. The village head's report confirms that the head of household is female. The discrepancy, on the face of it, is the same as the other ten — a transcription error in the commandery's copy. But this entry lists a woman as head of household, which is permitted under Han registration law but is not common. The commandery scribe may have assumed that the head of household must be male and transcribed the sex field accordingly. This would be an error of assumption rather than an error of transcription. You note this on the working slip. The twelfth sex field discrepancy is the same type of error in reverse — a male head of household listed as female in the commandery copy. Corrected in the same way.

And now you return to entry two hundred and eleven in Ward Seven. You have been carrying this entry in your mind for ten days, ever since you placed the small dot in the margin of the working slip on the first day of the cross-referencing. Now, with the village head's report in front of you and the sex field discrepancy review underway, you have the information you need to resolve it. The entry reads: head of household, Zhang Nü, age thirty-one sui, relationship self, occupation nong, land held two qing, tax category nong. The sex field, in the third column, reads nan — male. The name, Zhang Nü, is a woman's name. The village head's report confirms: Zhang Nü, female. The head of household is a woman. The sex field should read nü, not nan. Both copies agree on the sex field, and both copies are wrong. The error has persisted through three consecutive censuses. The current register, the previous register, and the one before that — all list the sex as male. How the error was introduced in the first place, the register cannot answer. The original registration, performed by the village head at the time of the first census, would have been the source. The village head at that time may have written nan instead of nü. He may have been told the sex of the household head by a third party who reported it incorrectly. He may have written nü and had it misread by the scribe who transcribed the register. The characters for nan and nü differ by a single stroke when written cursively. In the cramped space of a bamboo slip column, with the ink still drying and the village head's brush moving quickly, the difference can be overlooked or misread. The original village head's report from three censuses ago is not available. It was returned to the village and has in all likelihood been destroyed or lost. The current village head's report, which confirms the sex as female, is the only document that can be used to correct the error. You write the correction on a working slip. Ward Seven, entry two hundred and eleven. Sex field discrepancy. Both copies list nan. Village head report confirms nü. Error persisted three censuses. Correction: change nan to nü. Source: village head report. You set the working slip aside. The dot in the margin has served its purpose.

### CHUNK audio-04-06
<!-- img: IMG-04-06 -->

The sex field occupies the first position in the third column of the slip, before the relationship field. A single character. Nan for male, nü for female. Written in the same ink as the rest of the entry, the character is, in theory, as reliable as any other field. In practice, errors in the sex field are not uncommon. The original registration is performed by the village head, who visits each household and records the information verbally provided by the head of household. The village head writes the entry on a slip, which is then collected by the county scribe and transcribed into the register. Two opportunities for error exist in this process. The first is at the point of recording. The village head may write nan when the household head says nü, or nü when the household head says nan — a misunderstanding, a mishearing, or a simple mistake in writing. The second is at the point of transcription. The county scribe, reading the village head's slip and writing the entry into the register, may misread the character, particularly if the slip has been handled, dampened, or worn, and particularly if the village head's handwriting is not clear. You pick up the register. You turn to entry two hundred and eleven. You look at the character in the sex field. The difference between nan and nü, when written cursively, is a single stroke. The character nan consists of a field radical on the left and a power radical on the right. The character nü consists of the female radical on the left and a single stroke on the right. In the cramped space of a bamboo slip column, with the ink still drying and the village head's brush moving quickly, a single stroke is all that separates one sex from the other. The error in this entry — nan written where nü was intended, or nü misread as nan during transcription — is an error of this kind. The fact that it has persisted through three censuses without correction suggests that no one has compared the sex field against the actual sex of the household head in those three censuses, or that the comparison was made and the error was not caught, because both copies were wrong in the same way.

The final entry in the sex field discrepancy review is not a discrepancy between the two copies but a deficiency in the registration itself. You encountered it during the cross-referencing phase on the seventh day. Ward Eight, entry five hundred and ninety-three. A household of nine persons, but only the head of household is listed. No data for the other eight members. The village head's report for this household, which arrived with the other reports on the tenth day, provides the missing information. The head of household is Wang Fu, age forty-five sui, occupation nong. The eight other members are listed by name, age, relationship, and occupation. Wang An, age twenty-two sui, son, nong. Wang Jing, age nineteen sui, daughter, none. Wang Liang, age seventeen sui, son, none. Wang Mei, age fifteen sui, daughter, none. Wang Zhu, age twelve sui, son, none. Wang Fang, age nine sui, daughter, none. Wang Gao, age six sui, son, none. Wang Xiao, age three sui, son, none. The village head's report lists all nine members, including the head, in the standard format for a multi-member household. The fact that the register contains only the head's entry and a note saying "household of nine" is a deficiency not explained by the village head's report. The information was available at the time of the original registration but was not transcribed into the register, or was transcribed and subsequently lost, or was deliberately omitted for a reason that is not recorded. The most likely explanation, based on your experience with similar cases in the previous correction, is that the scribe who wrote the original entry ran out of space on the slip containing the head's entry and intended to continue on the next slip, but did not — or wrote the continuation on a separate slip that was later separated from the head's slip and lost, or was never written at all, the note "household of nine" being a placeholder that was never replaced with the full data. Whatever the cause, the result is the same. The register contains one entry where there should be nine. The correction will be to add the eight missing entries from the village head's report, on new slips, immediately following the head's entry, and to remove the note "household of nine" from the head's slip by striking it through in red ink. You note this on a working slip. Ward Eight, entry five hundred ninety-three. Household of nine. Only head recorded. Eight members to be added from village head report. Note to be struck. The village head's report is appended to the working slip by tying the two slips together with a short length of hemp cord. The working slip pile now contains one hundred and fifteen slips. The desk is full. The oil in the lamp is low. The eleventh day is over.
