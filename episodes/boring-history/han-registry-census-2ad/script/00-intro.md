### CHUNK audio-00-01
<!-- img: IMG-00-01 -->

You push the door. It sticks. The wood has swollen from the rain overnight, and the frame grips the edge like a hand that does not want to let go. You push harder. The door gives. The air inside is still and carries the smell of old bamboo and dried ink — a smell you have worked with for three years, a smell you no longer notice unless you have been away from it, as you have, the summer having passed without archive duty. The county office is a compound of seven buildings around a courtyard paved in packed earth. The administrative hall faces south. The archive room faces west. The scribes' room, where you will work, is a small building set against the north interior wall, built of rammed earth with a tile roof. A dark stain runs along the eaves where the rain has recently fallen. The third day of the ninth month, in the second year of the Yuan Shi era. The air outside carries wood smoke from the kitchens behind the compound. The earth under your feet is damp. The magistrate's deputy meets you at the door of the scribes' room. He carries two items. The first is the previous register from the Yuan Shi Year 1 count — a collection of bamboo slips bound in groups by ward, each bundle tied with cord, each stamped with the magistrate's seal. You notice that the cord on the Ward Seven bundle is slightly loose. You will remember this later. The second item is the commandery's copy of the same register, sent from Jingzhao for cross-referencing. This copy is in better condition. It has been stored in the commandery archive, not transported between the county and the village heads' offices. The deputy tells you the magistrate has given you fourteen days. This is the standard period for a correction in a county of this size. Fourteen days from today is the seventeenth day of the ninth month. By that date the corrected register must be bound, signed, and ready for the courier to Jingzhao. You accept both documents. The deputy leaves. You are alone in the scribes' room. The smell of bamboo is everywhere.

### CHUNK audio-00-02
<!-- img: IMG-00-02 -->

You set the documents on the desk. The desk is elm, the surface worn smooth by use, darkened where previous scribes have rested their writing arm. You lay out your materials. The fresh cahier of blank bamboo slips goes to your left — eighty slips prepared by the scribes' room, stamped on the reverse with the county seal. Your ink stone goes to the upper right, beside the water vial. You unstopper the vial. You add water in small amounts, grinding the ink stick — pine soot and animal glue, the same type used in this office for three years at least, supplied by the commandery in batches of twenty — until the ink holds a line without running. You test it on the margin of a blank slip. The line is even. The brush is a reed quill cut fresh this morning. It holds the ink without dripping. You place the previous register to your right, the commandery's copy to your left, within reach but not obstructing the writing area. The register contains seven thousand nine hundred and twelve entries, organized by ward. Yangling county has eight wards. Each contains between seven hundred and one thousand one hundred entries. Ward Three is the largest, at one thousand one hundred and four. Ward Eight is the smallest, at seven hundred and thirty-six. Each entry occupies a single bamboo slip. Six fields per slip: the head of household's name in the first column, the age in the second, the relationship in the third, the occupation in the fourth, the land held in qing in the fifth, and the tax category in the sixth. The slips are bound top to bottom with cord threaded through two holes near the top. The cord is knotted at the back of the bundle and sealed with a small clay seal impressed with the magistrate's mark. You pick up your knife. You cut the cord on the first bundle. Ward One. The slips fall open like a fan. The bamboo is dry and slightly curved. The ink has not faded. You begin.

### CHUNK audio-00-03
<!-- img: IMG-00-03 -->

The system you serve has been in place for over two hundred years. Every three years, a full census is conducted across all commanderies of the Han empire. In the years between, a correction is performed at the county level — to add new births and in-migration entries, to strike deaths and departures, to correct any discrepancies between the county register and the commandery's copy. This correction is what you have been assigned to perform. The theory is straightforward. Two copies of the same register, maintained by two different offices, should be identical. In practice they are not. The county register is updated by the county scribes and the village heads. The commandery's copy is maintained in Jingzhao, updated only when the corrected register arrives at the end of the correction period. The commandery copy is transcribed by a different scribe, at a different time, from a different original. Transcription errors between the two are one of the primary sources of the discrepancies you will be correcting. The other source is the village heads' reports, which arrive at irregular intervals, are sometimes incomplete, are occasionally illegible, and in at least one case from the previous correction were written on the wrong side of the slip — the blank side rather than the prepared side — causing the ink to bleed and rendering several entries unreadable. You do not expect this correction to be different from the previous one. You completed the previous correction in nine days. It produced two hundred and forty-one corrections out of seven thousand eight hundred and seventy-three entries. The number of entries has increased by thirty-nine. The number of corrections, you suspect, will also increase. By how much you cannot predict. The village heads' reports for this cycle have not yet been reviewed. You open Ward One to the first entry. You take up your brush. The ink is ready. The room is quiet. Outside, you can hear someone sweeping the courtyard. You read the first name.
