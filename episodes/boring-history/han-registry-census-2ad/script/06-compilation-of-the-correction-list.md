On the thirteenth day, you begin the compilation of the correction list — the gaibuzhuang. You take up the red ink. The cinnabar flows smoothly on the fresh bamboo. You prepare the red-bordered slips from the blank cahier that you received on the first day. The slips in this cahier are wider than the standard slips, as you requested from the scribes' room at the beginning of the correction. The bamboo is of good quality — pale, smooth, and consistent, cut from a single batch, unlike the mixed batches in the register itself. You draw the red border on each slip before writing the columns. The bone ruler guides the line along the edge. The red ink, ground from the same cinnabar you used for the striking, is bright against the pale bamboo. Each slip takes approximately the same amount of time to prepare. The drawing of the border. The writing of the five columns. The filling of the entry. The gaibuzhuang is a separate document from the register itself. It is the document that will be submitted to the commandery along with the corrected register as the official record of all changes made during the correction. Each entry on the gaibuzhuang records five things: the entry number, in the first column; the original text, in the second; the corrected text, in the third; the source of the correction, in the fourth; and the verifying official, in the fifth. The red border is a strip of red ink drawn around the edge of each slip before the columns are written, creating a frame that distinguishes the correction list from the register and that serves as a visual indicator that this document contains corrections rather than original entries. The rhythm of the work is steady and regular, a rhythm that you find easier to maintain than the stop-and-start rhythm of the cross-referencing, where each discrepancy interrupted the flow and required a decision. Here, the discrepancies have already been identified and noted. You are transferring them from the working slips to the gaibuzhuang, one by one, in order.

The total number of entries on the gaibuzhuang is three hundred and nine. Twenty-one sui-conversion age discrepancies, noted but not requiring a change to the register. Nine age discrepancies of two years or more, requiring verification slips from the village heads, noted as "pending verification." Twelve sex field discrepancies, corrected on the register. One sex field discrepancy — entry two hundred and eleven in Ward Seven, the woman listed as male — corrected and noted separately as having persisted through three consecutive censuses. One household of nine in Ward Eight, requiring the addition of eight new entries from the village head's report. Ninety-one tax category discrepancies, noted for review by the magistrate's office. One birth notification with an irregular seal, noted as irregular but verified. One guanfu from Chang'an county with a missing lower seal, noted as "seal absent, verification pending." Eighteen out-migration entries pending confirmation, noted as "pending confirmation, no response received." Three double-count entries, noted as "potential double-count, flag for review." The remaining entries are the thirty-seven births and twelve in-migration entries, which are additions rather than corrections and are listed on the gaibuzhuang as "new entries" with their sources noted. The total, when added to the corrections, comes to three hundred and nine. You work through the list in order, writing each entry on a separate slip. The red-bordered slips accumulate in a pile on the desk. Each one identical in format, each one different in content. The ink flows. The brush moves. The room is quiet. The afternoon light is even and grey.

### CHUNK audio-06-03
<!-- img: IMG-06-03 -->

You draw another red border. The brush feels familiar now. The convention of using red ink for corrections and official annotations, and black ink for original entries, dates to the Qin dynasty, which standardised the use of zhushu across all commanderies as part of its broader programme of administrative standardisation. The Qin regulation, issued in the form of a directive to all county offices, specified that corrections to official documents — including household registers, tax records, and legal proceedings — were to be made in zhushu, with the original text retained and the correction written adjacent to or over the original, so that the reader could see both the original and the corrected version and could verify that the correction was made by an authorised official. The regulation also specified that official annotations — notes, comments, and instructions added to a document by a superior official — were to be written in zhushu, to distinguish them from the original text written in black. You set the completed slip aside. The convention has persisted in the Han administrative system for over two hundred years, without significant change, and it is observed in every county office and commandery archive in the empire — with the exception, you are told, of a few remote commanderies in the far south where the supply of cinnabar is limited and where the scribes have been known to use a red-brown ink made from a local plant as a substitute. This practice is technically non-compliant but has not been the subject of any disciplinary action, as the commandery office in those regions apparently considers the substitution acceptable in the absence of a reliable supply of the standard material. The practical effect of the convention is that any document containing zhushu corrections is immediately identifiable as a corrected document. The red ink serves as a visual signal that the document has been reviewed and amended. The gaibuzhuang, with its red-bordered slips and its zhushu entries, announces itself as a record of changes. You take up the next slip. The bone ruler guides the line.

On the morning of the fourteenth day, the magistrate's deputy arrives to review a sample of the gaibuzhuang entries. The review is a standard procedure. The deputy examines a selection of entries from the correction list, chosen at random, and verifies that the corrections are consistent with the source documents — the register, the commandery's copy, the village heads' reports, and the tianbu. The deputy selects forty entries. This is the same number that was selected during the previous correction, and, the deputy tells you, the standard sample size for a register of this magnitude. You watch as the deputy examines each entry, comparing it against the corresponding working slip and, where the working slip references a source document, checking the source document as well. The deputy works in silence. The only sound is the turn of the bamboo slips and the occasional creak of the desk as the deputy leans forward to read an entry more closely. Of the forty entries, thirty-eight are found to be consistent. The two that are not are both minor. One has the entry number written in a slightly different format from the standard — you wrote "W3-412" where the standard format is "Ward Three, entry four hundred and twelve." One has the source recorded as "village head's report" without specifying which village head's report. An omission that the deputy asks you to correct before the list is submitted. You correct both entries. The deputy signs the gaibuzhuang on the final slip, in the fifth column, where the verifying official's name belongs, writing the deputy's name and title in black ink. You sign the slip below the deputy's signature. The gaibuzhuang is complete. The red-bordered slips are bound with cord, in the same manner as the register, and the bundle is stamped with the magistrate's seal. The bundle contains three hundred and nine entries on fifty-two slips — some entries requiring more than one slip when the original and corrected texts are long. The bundle is approximately the same thickness as the register for Ward Three. You set the gaibuzhuang on the desk, next to the register. The two documents are now ready for the summary report and the final submission. The fourteenth day is over. The correction period has one day remaining.
