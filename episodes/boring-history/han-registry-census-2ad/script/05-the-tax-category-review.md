### CHUNK audio-05-01
<!-- img: IMG-05-01 -->

On the twelfth day, you open the land records. The tianbu is heavier than the household register. It is wider, bound in leather rather than cord, written on paper rather than bamboo. You set it on the desk. The leather binding creaks. The paper is made from mulberry bark — pounded, soaked, dried into sheets that are thick enough to resist tearing but flexible enough to be folded and bound. The surface has a faint texture that the brush catches on slightly, requiring a lighter touch than the bamboo demands. The smell is different. Not the dry mineral smell of the bamboo slips, but a faint, woody, slightly sweet smell that you associate with the county office's storage room, where the tianbu is kept on a separate shelf from the household register. The tianbu contains, for each household in the register, the amount of land held in qing, the type of land — irrigated, dry, or garden — the location by ward and li, and any changes to the land holding since the last correction. The tianbu is the authoritative source for land-holding data. Where the household register and the tianbu disagree, the tianbu takes precedence. Your task in this phase is to verify the tax category — the zu field, the sixth and last column on each slip — against the land-holding data in the tianbu, and to note any discrepancies. The tax categories are four in number. Nong, the farmer category — households that cultivate land and pay tax in grain. Gong, the artisan category — households that practice a craft and pay tax in goods. Shang, the merchant category — households that engage in trade and pay tax in cash. And a small number of exempt categories for officials, students, and the infirm, registered as mian, meaning exempt. The relationship between the tax category and the land holding is governed by a regulation dating, in its current form, to the early years of the Former Han dynasty, though the principle it embodies — that a farmer must hold land in order to be classified as a farmer — is older than the dynasty itself. The principle has its roots in the Qin dynasty's land allocation system, which the Han adopted with modifications. Those modifications include the introduction of the mian category, which did not exist under the Qin, and the specification of minimum land-holding requirements for each zu category. These requirements are the source of most of the discrepancies you are about to find.

### CHUNK audio-05-02

You begin by matching each entry in the household register against the corresponding entry in the tianbu. The process is more time-consuming than the cross-referencing of the two copies of the register, because the tianbu is organized differently — by land parcel rather than by household. A single household may have multiple entries in the tianbu, one for each parcel of land it holds. You must sum the parcels to obtain the total land holding before comparing it against the zu field in the register. The work is slow. The tianbu is heavy. The leather binding creaks each time you turn a page — a sound that is audible in the quiet room and that you notice at first and then stop noticing, as you stopped noticing the smell of the bamboo slips on the first day. The first twenty entries match. The household register lists the zu field as nong, and the tianbu confirms that the household holds the minimum amount of land required for the nong category. One qing for a household of one to three members. Three qing for a household of four or more. Entry twenty-one presents the first discrepancy. The household register lists the zu field as nong. The tianbu lists the land holding as zero qing. The household holds no registered land. This is a discrepancy that cannot be explained by a difference in counting conventions or a transcription error. A household that holds no land cannot be classified as nong. The nong category, by definition, applies to households that cultivate land and pay tax in grain. A household with no land cannot cultivate anything. It cannot pay tax in grain. It must be reclassified — to gong, if it practices a craft, or to shang, if it engages in trade, or to mian, if it qualifies for an exemption. You note this on a working slip. You continue.

### CHUNK audio-05-03
<!-- img: IMG-05-03 -->

The pattern repeats. Entry after entry. Nong in the register, no land in the tianbu. Or gong in the register, land held in the tianbu that suggests nong. The discrepancies accumulate. The total, when you finish, is ninety-one entries with a zu field that does not match the land-holding data in the tianbu. The most common discrepancy, accounting for forty-seven of the ninety-one, is the one you have just described: a household classified as nong that holds no registered land, or that holds less than the minimum required for the nong category. The remaining discrepancies fall into several types. Twenty-three households appear to have changed their occupation since the last census without updating their zu field. The tianbu records them as holding land and paying grain tax, but the register lists them as gong or shang. This suggests they have taken up a craft or trade while continuing to hold their land — a practice that is not prohibited but that creates a mismatch between the zu field and the actual economic activity of the household. Sixty-eight of the ninety-one are probable legacy errors. Discrepancies that have existed in the register for more than one correction cycle without being corrected. This suggests that the previous hushucao either did not perform the tax category review with the same thoroughness you are applying now, or identified the discrepancies but did not include them on the correction list, or included them on the correction list but the corrections were never applied to the register. You cannot verify which of these is the case without access to the previous hushucao's working slips, which are not in the archive and which may have been destroyed after the previous correction, as the instructions indicate that working notes are not to be preserved with the register. The working slip pile, which was one hundred and fifteen at the beginning of this phase, is now two hundred and six.

### CHUNK audio-05-04
<!-- img: IMG-05-04 -->

The nong category requires a minimum land holding, as you have seen. The grain tax paid by nong households is calculated at a rate of one-fifteenth of the harvest. It is collected by the county office in the autumn and stored in the county granary for distribution to the commandery and, ultimately, to the capital. You turn the page of the tianbu. The leather creaks. The gong category does not require a minimum land holding, because artisans are not expected to cultivate land. They pay tax in goods, assessed according to the type and quality of the craft practiced, with rates that vary by specialty and are set by the commandery rather than the county. The shang category also does not require a minimum land holding. Merchants pay tax in cash, at a rate that is higher than the grain tax paid by nong households. This rate reflects the imperial government's traditional suspicion of merchants and its preference for land-based wealth over trade-based wealth — a preference encoded in the tax regulations. The practical effect is that the shang category is financially less desirable than the nong or gong categories. This is one reason why a household that has taken up trade might prefer to remain classified as nong in the register, even if it no longer cultivates land. It is one reason why the discrepancy between the zu field and the actual occupation of the household is as common as it is. The mian category, the smallest of the four, applies to officials, students, and the infirm. It exempts the household from all tax obligations. This makes it the most desirable category from the household's perspective and the most carefully scrutinized from the county's. Any household that claims an exemption must provide documentation of its eligibility. The documentation is checked by the magistrate's office at each correction cycle. The mian category accounts for eleven of the ninety-one discrepancies. In all eleven cases, the discrepancy is the same: the household is registered as mian, but the documentation of eligibility has expired or is missing. The exemption is provisional at best and void at worst. The household will need to provide new documentation or be reclassified. You note all ninety-one discrepancies on working slips, each with a notation indicating the type of discrepancy, the zu field in the register, the zu field that the tianbu data supports, and the source.

### CHUNK audio-05-05
<!-- img: IMG-05-05 -->

You close the tianbu. The leather binding creaks one final time, louder than the previous creaks because you are pressing the cover shut with more force than necessary — not out of frustration, the work having been methodical and without surprise, which is the way you prefer it — but because the binding has swollen slightly from the humidity of the room. The scribes' room faces south. At this time of year, it is warm in the afternoon and cool in the morning. The temperature fluctuation causes the leather to expand and contract over the course of the day. The archive room, where the tianbu is usually stored, is on the north side of the compound and receives less sun. You set the tianbu on the desk, on top of the household register, which is itself on top of the commandery's copy. A stack of three documents that takes up most of the desk surface. It will need to be rearranged before you can begin the next phase. The tax category review is complete. Ninety-one discrepancies have been identified and noted. The most common — the nong household with no registered land — will be flagged on the correction list with a recommendation for reclassification. The reclassification itself is not within the scope of the correction. It will need to be performed by the magistrate's office after the corrected register is submitted. The twenty-three households that appear to have changed occupation will be flagged for review by the village heads. The sixty-eight legacy errors will be noted as such, with a notation indicating that they have been present in the register for more than one correction cycle. The eleven expired mian exemptions will be flagged for documentation review. All of these notes will be compiled on the gaibuzhuang, the correction list, which is the next and final phase before the summary report. You rearrange the desk. The tianbu goes to the side. The household register goes to the right. The commandery's copy goes to the left. The working slips are sorted by type and arranged in order. The oil in the lamp is low. You refill it from the flask that the deputy left on the shelf. The wick catches. The room is bright again. The twelfth day is over.
