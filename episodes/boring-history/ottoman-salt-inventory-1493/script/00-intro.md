### CHUNK audio-00-01
<!-- img: IMG-00-01 -->

On the seventeenth of April, in the year eight hundred and ninety-nine by the Hijri calendar, you arrive at the Isketder salt warehouse outside the town of Eskisehir, in the sanjak of the same name, at approximately the third hour after dawn, according to the muvakkit's call that morning, which you heard from the courtyard of the caravanserai where you had slept the night before. The warehouse is a long stone building with a wooden roof, built against a low hill from which, you are told, the salt has been extracted for some three hundred and seventy years, though the records for the first century of extraction were, according to the defterdar's office, lost in a fire whose date no one has been able to confirm. You carry with you the previous year's inventory register, a defter of forty-three pages, bound in dark leather, stamped with the seal of the defterdar of the sanjak, and a separate sheet, loose, bearing the new inventory instructions issued from the imperial divan in Edirne on the third of Muharram of this year. The instructions, which repeat in nearly every particular the instructions of last year, with one exception, require you to verify the quantity of salt stored in each of the seven warehouses under the jurisdiction of the Eskisehir sanjak, to weigh a sample from each lot, to check the condition of the lead seals on each door, and to record all findings in a new register, to be submitted no later than the fifteenth of Sha'ban. The exception, which appears in the fourth clause of the instructions, concerns the method of counting, about which more will be said later. The air outside the warehouse smells of dry earth and salt. You can see the door. It is closed. A guard stands beside it. You approach.

### CHUNK audio-00-02
<!-- img: IMG-00-02 -->

The guard, whose name, according to the register you carry, is Hasan, though you have not yet confirmed this, asks for your letter of appointment. You present it. He examines the seal, turns it over in his hands, and hands it back without comment. The door opens inward on iron hinges that make a sound you will hear again every morning for the next eleven days. Inside, the warehouse is dim. Light enters through two narrow windows near the roof and through the open door behind you. The salt is stored in sacks of varying sizes, arranged in rows along the walls and in two central stacks, each stack approximately twelve arşin high, though you will not measure them until tomorrow. The floor is stone, swept clean in the center, with a thin layer of salt dust near the walls where the sweeping does not reach. A table stands near the entrance, and on it, covered by a cloth, you find the warehouse keeper's own register, a smaller defter than yours, with entries in a hand you do not recognize. You set your own register on the table beside it. The warehouse keeper is not present. Hasan, the guard, tells you the keeper will come at midday. You have the morning. You open your register to the first page. The inventory from last year lists, for this warehouse alone, one thousand eight hundred and forty-seven sacks of salt, divided into three categories by quality, and seven hundred and twelve loose kile measured by weight rather than by count. Your task is to verify these numbers. You begin with the sacks nearest the door.
