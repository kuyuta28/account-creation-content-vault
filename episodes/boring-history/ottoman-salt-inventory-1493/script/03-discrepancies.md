### CHUNK audio-03-01
<!-- img: IMG-03-01 -->

The third warehouse, located in the town of Sündüs, three hours' ride to the northwest of Eskisehir, presents the first serious discrepancy of the inventory. You arrive on the ninth day, a Thursday, and begin the count on Friday morning, after waiting for the warehouse keeper, a man named Hüseyin, son of Ali, who was not present when you arrived and who, according to his neighbor, had gone to the market in the neighboring village to purchase feed for his animals, a detail that is not relevant to the inventory but which you record in your notes because the absence of the warehouse keeper at the time of your arrival is, according to the Kanunname, a minor irregularity that should be noted, not because it indicates wrongdoing but because it is, as the Kanunname puts it in clause twelve, "a circumstance that may bear upon the integrity of the count," a phrase that you have always found somewhat vague but which, you suppose, exists to remind the inspector that a keeper who is absent may have a reason for being absent, and that the reason may or may not be innocent. Hüseyin arrives at mid-morning, apologizes for his absence, and opens the warehouse. The door, when it opens, reveals a space that is, at first glance, similar to the other two warehouses: stone walls, wooden roof, rows of sacks. But the count, when you complete it on the morning of the tenth day, yields a total that differs from the register by forty-seven sacks, a number that is significantly larger than any discrepancy you have encountered in your previous assignments, and which, when you present it to Hüseyin, causes him to stare at the floor for several seconds before speaking.

### CHUNK audio-03-02
<!-- img: IMG-03-02 -->

Hüseyin's explanation, when it comes, is long and, in its details, not entirely consistent. He says that twenty-three sacks were transferred to the Isketder warehouse in the month of Muharram, on the orders of the defterdar's office, for reasons that he describes as "balancing the reserves," a term that you have not encountered in the Kanunname or in any of the instructions you have received, and which, when you ask him to clarify, he defines as the practice of moving salt from a warehouse with a surplus to one with a deficit, a practice that, while not explicitly authorized by the Kanunname, is, he says, commonly done and has been done for many years, though he cannot recall a specific instance of it being done in this sanjak before this year, or, rather, he can recall one instance, three years ago, but that involved only six sacks, not twenty-three, and the direction was opposite, from Isketder to Sündüs, not from Sündüs to Isketder. You check the Isketder register. The transfer of twenty-three sacks from Sündüs is not recorded in it. You check the appendix, the outgoing shipments, the incoming shipments. Nothing. You ask Mustafa, the Isketder keeper, when you send a messenger to him that afternoon. The reply, which arrives the following morning, states that no such transfer was received at the Isketder warehouse and that the register contains no entry for it. Twenty-three sacks, according to one keeper, transferred. According to the other, never received.

### CHUNK audio-03-03
<!-- img: IMG-03-03 -->

Hüseyin then says that a further twelve sacks were sold to a merchant named Ali, son of Hacı, who, he claims, holds a valid purchase license, though he cannot produce the license because, he says, it was returned to the kadi's court after the sale was completed, as is the practice, and he does not have a copy, because the practice, as he describes it, does not involve giving a copy to the warehouse keeper, only to the merchant and to the kadi's court, a practice that, if true, would mean that the only record of the sale is held by the kadi, which, as you know, is not how the system is supposed to work, because the Kanunname, in clause nineteen, requires that the warehouse keeper retain a copy of every purchase license, and that the copy be available for inspection during the inventory, a requirement that Hüseyin appears to have violated, or, as he frames it, misunderstood, because, he says, the requirement was explained to him by the previous keeper, his uncle, who held the position before him, as applying only to sales of first-quality salt, not second-quality, which is what the twelve sacks were, an interpretation that is not supported by the text of the Kanunname, which makes no distinction between qualities in clause nineteen, and which, you note in your register, appears to have been applied incorrectly. You ride to the kadi's court to check the license. The court is closed. It is Friday, and the kadi does not hold session on Fridays. You will have to return on Saturday.

### CHUNK audio-03-04
<!-- img: IMG-03-04 -->

The remaining twelve sacks, Hüseyin says, were spoilt, a rate of spoilage that, if accurate, would be four times the rate at Isketder and three times the rate at Kaymazz, a discrepancy within the discrepancy that you note without comment. He leads you to the corner of the warehouse where, he says, the spoilt sacks were stored before being removed. The corner is empty. There is a slight discoloration on the stone floor, a pale stain in the shape of, roughly, a rectangle, which may be salt residue or may be something else, you cannot tell. You ask when the spoilt sacks were removed. Hüseyin says they were removed in the month of Rajab, approximately two months ago, by the same laborers who assist with the inventory, and the salt was disposed of in the usual manner, which is to say it was spread on the road outside the warehouse, a practice that, while not prescribed by the Kanunname, is, he says, customary, and has the added benefit of keeping the road from becoming muddy in the rain, a benefit that, you note, does not apply in the summer months, when there is no rain, and when, as it happens, Rajab falls this year. You do not point this out. You write: twelve sacks, spoilt, disposed of on road, no samples retained, no spoilage test conducted. The absence of samples means that the spoilage cannot be verified by the method prescribed in the Kanunname, the dissolution and residue test that you were unable to perform on the four spoilt sacks at Isketder and which, you now realize, you should have performed on those sacks before leaving, because the Isketder sacks are still present and can still be tested, whereas the Sündüs sacks are gone, spread on a road, mixed with earth, unrecoverable. You make a note to test the Isketder sacks when you return.

### CHUNK audio-03-05
<!-- img: IMG-03-05 -->

On Saturday, you return to the kadi's court. The court is open, but the kadi is hearing a case involving a dispute over a water right between two villages, and the clerk, who sits at a desk near the entrance, tells you that the kadi will not be available until the afternoon, and that in the meantime, you may consult the register of purchase licenses, which is kept in a cabinet in the clerk's office, and which you are, as an imperial inspector, entitled to examine. The cabinet is wooden, with three drawers, each labeled by year. You open the drawer for the current year. The licenses are arranged in order of their number, from one to one hundred and forty-seven, which is the last license issued to date. You look for license numbers corresponding to sales from the Sündüs warehouse. You find two: license number ninety-one, for the sale of eight sacks of second-quality salt to a merchant named Hacı Ali, which is the same Hacı Ali mentioned by Mustafa at the Isketder warehouse, the same license you verified during the Isketder inventory, and which has already been accounted for; and license number one hundred and nineteen, for the sale of twelve sacks of second-quality salt to a merchant named Ali, son of Hacı, from the Sündüs warehouse, dated the eighteenth of Rabi' al-Akhir, at a price of three hundred and forty akçe per sack. The license exists. The sale is recorded. You note the license number and the date. But the price, three hundred and forty akçe per sack, is higher than the rate established by the Kanunname for second-quality salt in this sanjak, which is three hundred and twenty akçe, a difference of twenty akçe per sack, or two hundred and forty akçe total, which is, by any measure, not a large sum, but which is, strictly speaking, a violation of the price controls established by the Kanunname, and which must, according to the same Kanunname, be reported to the defterdar's office, along with the name of the merchant and the name of the warehouse keeper who authorized the sale at the unauthorized price. You write this in your register.

### CHUNK audio-03-06
<!-- img: IMG-03-06 -->

The twenty-three sacks that were transferred, according to Hüseyin, but not received, according to Mustafa, remain unaccounted for. You consider the possible explanations. The first is that Hüseyin is mistaken, and the transfer never occurred, and the twenty-three sacks were disposed of in some other way that he has not disclosed. The second is that the transfer did occur, and Mustafa received the sacks but did not record them, either through negligence or through some other motive. The third is that the transfer was ordered but never carried out, the sacks being removed from the Sündüs register but never actually moved, a bookkeeping error rather than a physical one. The fourth is that the sacks were transferred to a different warehouse, not Isketder, and Hüseyin confused the destination, a possibility that, while unlikely, cannot be ruled out without checking the records of the remaining warehouses. You cannot determine which of these explanations is correct without further investigation, and further investigation would require, at minimum, visiting the remaining four warehouses and comparing their records, a process that would add several days to the inventory and which, according to your instructions, must be completed by the fifteenth of Sha'ban, a deadline that, you calculate, is now twenty-three days away, and which, given the pace of the inventory so far, you will be able to meet only if you do not delay, which means that the investigation of the twenty-three missing sacks will have to proceed alongside the inventory of the remaining warehouses, rather than in place of it, which will require you to carry two lines of inquiry at once, a task that, while not impossible, will require careful record-keeping, and which, you suspect, will result in neither line of inquiry being pursued as thoroughly as it should be, a compromise that, as you have observed before, is the natural condition of most administrative work.

### CHUNK audio-03-07
<!-- img: IMG-03-07 -->

The fourth warehouse, in the village of Karaca, and the fifth, in the town of Alpu, present no significant discrepancies, and their inventories are completed on the eleventh and twelfth days respectively. The sixth warehouse, in the village of Beylikova, presents a discrepancy of three sacks, which are accounted for by a transfer to the Kaymazz warehouse that is recorded in both registers, confirmed by Fatima, and verified without difficulty. The seventh and final warehouse, a small storage facility in the hamlet of Söğüt, contains only one hundred and sixteen sacks and forty kile of loose salt, and its count, completed on the morning of the fourteenth day, matches the register exactly, a perfect correspondence that, in your experience, is more suspicious than a small discrepancy, because it suggests either exceptional record-keeping or a register that has been adjusted to match the count before the inspector's arrival, a possibility that the Kanunname addresses in clause twenty-three, which states that the warehouse keeper is forbidden from conducting a preliminary count of the warehouse before the arrival of the imperial inspector, a prohibition that, according to the kadi's representative, who mentions it to you during a conversation about the dispute, is widely ignored in practice, because the keepers, understandably, wish to know the state of their warehouses before the inspector arrives, and because, as he adds with a shrug, there is no penalty specified in the Kanunname for violating clause twenty-three, an omission that, in his view, renders the clause advisory rather than mandatory, a view that the defterdar's office does not share, but which, in the absence of a test case, has never been formally resolved. You note the perfect correspondence in your register, with a small mark in the margin that indicates your reservation, and you move on.

### CHUNK audio-03-08
<!-- img: IMG-03-08 -->

On the fifteenth day, you return to the defterdar's office in Eskisehir to compile the final report. The total inventory across all seven warehouses, when you sum the counts and subtract the transfers, sales, and spoilage that have been verified, leaves a discrepancy of thirty-one sacks, which, when added to the twenty-three sacks that remain unaccounted for from the Sündüs warehouse, yields a total unexplained shortfall of fifty-four sacks, a number that, you are aware, is large enough to require a formal investigation by the defterdar's office, but which, you are also aware, may never receive one, because the defterdar, who is currently in Edirne attending to a matter related to the imperial campaign, is not expected to return for several weeks, and the deputy defterdar, Ahmed, who is authorized to act in his absence, has, as he tells you when you present the report, a backlog of investigations from the previous year that he is still working through, and which includes, among other things, a discrepancy of two hundred and ten akçe in the tax collection records of the Alpu tax farmer, a case of counterfeit lead seals in the Kütahya sanjak, and an investigation into the disappearance of seventeen horses from the imperial post station in İnönü, all of which, he says, are more pressing than fifty-four sacks of salt, a prioritization that you do not dispute, because you understand, as he understands, that salt, while important, is not horses, and that a missing sack of salt, unlike a missing horse, does not represent a threat to the imperial communications network, or to the military readiness of the sanjak, or to anything, really, except the accuracy of the register, which, in the larger scheme of imperial administration, is important, but not urgent. You sign the report. Ahmed stamps it with the defterdar's seal. It is filed. You are given a receipt. The investigation into the fifty-four missing sacks, if it occurs, will be conducted by someone else, at some later date, and the results, if any, will be appended to your register in the archives. You are not required to stay. Your inventory is complete. The total count, the verified transfers, the verified sales, the spoilage, the price violation, the unexplained shortfall, the perfect correspondence at Söğüt that may or may not be suspicious, all of it is recorded in the register, which will be stored in the defterdar's archives alongside the registers of previous years, and which may, at some point in the future, be consulted by another inspector, or by a scholar, or by no one at all.
