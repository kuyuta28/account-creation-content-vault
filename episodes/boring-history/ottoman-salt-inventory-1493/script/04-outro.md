### CHUNK audio-04-01

The register, which you carry back to Edirne, is received by the imperial chancery on the twenty-ninth of Sha'ban, eleven days before the end of the month and four days after the deadline specified in your instructions, a delay that is not, according to the clerk who receives it, unusual for inventories conducted in the Anatolian sanjaks, where the distances between warehouses and the condition of the roads, which you yourself have observed, make it difficult to complete the work within the allotted time, and for which an extension is routinely granted, though the extension must be requested in advance, which you did not do, because you were not aware that one was needed, a failure that the clerk notes in the margin of the receipt he gives you, a note that will, in all likelihood, have no consequences, because failures of this kind are common and the consequences, when they are applied, which is rare, are limited to a verbal reprimand delivered by the deputy defterdar, who, as you have observed, has more pressing concerns. The register is placed in a cabinet with the other registers from this year's round of inventories, of which there are, according to the clerk, one hundred and seventy-three, covering the warehouses of eleven sanjaks, each register bound in dark leather, each stamped with the seal of the defterdar of the sanjak in which it was compiled, each containing, in its pages, the same kinds of numbers that you have spent the last two weeks recording, the same kinds of discrepancies, the same kinds of verified transfers and unverified shortfalls, the same marginal notes and the same small marks of reservation, all of them awaiting the attention of someone whose job it is to read them, which may or may not happen, and which, if it does, will produce, at best, a further report, which will itself be filed, and which may, in its turn, be read, or may not, the register remaining in its cabinet, in the chancery, in Edirne, alongside one hundred and seventy-two others, in a building that, according to a note written on the wall next to the cabinet, in a hand you do not recognize, was last painted in the year eight hundred and ninety-one.

### CHUNK audio-04-02

The investigation into the fifty-four missing sacks was, according to the records that survive in the Ottoman archives, never conducted. The register for the Eskisehir sanjak for the year eight hundred and ninety-nine, catalogued under number two thousand four hundred and seventeen, contains a marginal note, in the deputy defterdar's hand, stating that the investigation has been postponed pending the return of the defterdar, and a second note, in a different hand, stating that the defterdar has returned and that the investigation will commence in the following month, and a third note, in yet another hand, stating that the investigation has been deferred to the following year's inventory, at which point, according to the register for the year nine hundred, the discrepancy has been resolved, not by an investigation, but by an adjustment in the opening count, which now matches the previous year's closing count minus the verified transfers and sales, the fifty-four missing sacks having been, in the language of the register, "accounted for in the reconciliation," a phrase that appears frequently in the archives and which means, in practice, that the numbers have been made to agree without determining why they disagreed in the first place. The spoilage test for the four sacks at the Isketder warehouse was conducted, and the results, recorded on a loose sheet inserted between pages thirty-one and thirty-two of the register, confirm that the salt was, in fact, spoilt, with a residue exceeding the one-in-forty threshold established by the Kanunname. The price violation at the Sündüs warehouse, involving the sale of twelve sacks at three hundred and forty akçe per sack rather than the authorized three hundred and twenty, was noted in the report but does not appear to have been pursued further, and no record of a penalty or a corrective action exists in the surviving documents. The register itself, bound in dark leather, stamped with the seal of the defterdar of the Eskisehir sanjak, remains in the archives, its pages intact, its ink faded but legible, the numbers still readable, the marginal notes still clear, a document that records, in meticulous detail, eleven days of counting salt in seven warehouses, a task that was completed, a report that was filed, an investigation that was deferred, and a discrepancy that was reconciled, in the end, not by discovery but by adjustment. That is all that survives.
