On the fifth day, the inventory of the Isketder warehouse complete, you ride to the town of Eskisehir to deliver the preliminary report to the defterdar's office, a building of stone and timber near the courthouse where the kadi holds session, and it is here, in the space between these two offices, that the salt tax dispute, which has been simmering since before your arrival, becomes apparent to you. The dispute, as it is explained to you by the deputy defterdar, a man named Ahmed, son of Mehmed, who has been in his position for eleven years and who speaks with the careful precision of someone who has repeated the same explanation many times, concerns the calculation method for the salt tax, and specifically whether the tax should be assessed on the quantity of salt stored at the time of the inventory, which is the method favored by the defterdar's office, or on the quantity of salt produced during the year, which is the method favored by the kadi's court, the difference being significant because the quantity stored and the quantity produced are not the same, the stored quantity being affected by transfers, sales, spoilage, and the natural variance in production from year to year, while the produced quantity, in theory, reflects the actual output of the salt works and is therefore, according to the kadi, a more accurate basis for taxation, though the defterdar's office argues that production figures are less reliable than inventory figures because production is recorded by the salt works overseers, who have an interest in reporting lower figures, whereas inventory is verified by independent inspectors such as yourself, whose reports are, or should be, objective. You listen to this explanation while standing in the corridor outside the deputy defterdar's office, because the office itself is being repainted, a fact that is not relevant to the dispute but which you note because the smell of paint will remain in your memory associated with this conversation for as long as you live, though you do not know this yet.

### CHUNK audio-02-02

The Kanunname of eight hundred and ninety-four, which governs the salt regulations currently in force, addresses the tax calculation in its seventh clause, a clause that, according to the deputy defterdar, is unambiguous, and, according to the kadi's representative, whom you meet later the same day, is subject to interpretation. The clause states, in the Ottoman Turkish of the original, that the salt tax is to be calculated on the basis of the quantity of salt in the warehouse at the time of the imperial inventory, at the rate of three akçe per kile for first-quality salt, two akçe per kile for second-quality, and one akçe per kile for third-quality, with the proceeds to be divided, after the deduction of the warehouse keeper's salary and the cost of the lead seals, between the imperial treasury and the timar holder of the sanjak, in proportions that vary by region and are listed in a separate schedule appended to the Kanunname. The schedule, which you have read, assigns to the Eskisehir sanjak a split of sixty percent to the imperial treasury and forty percent to the timar holder, a division that was established, according to a marginal note in the schedule itself, during the reign of Mehmed the Conqueror and has not been changed since, though the timar holder, a man named Davud, who holds the land grant in exchange for military service, has petitioned twice in the past six years for a revision of the split, each time citing the increased cost of maintaining the roads that service the salt caravans, and each time being denied on the grounds that the split is established by the Kanunname and cannot be changed without a new imperial decree, a process that, as anyone who has attempted it will confirm, requires the involvement of the imperial divan in Edirne and can take several years, during which the original split remains in force.

### CHUNK audio-02-03

The kadi's objection, as presented to you by his representative, a young clerk named Abdullah, son of Yusuf, who speaks quickly and glances at the door of the defterdar's office as if expecting someone to emerge and contradict him, is not to the rates themselves, which are, he concedes, clearly stated in the Kanunname, but to the basis of calculation. The kadi, he says, holds that the word "quantity" in the seventh clause refers to the quantity of salt produced during the year, not the quantity stored at the time of inventory, and he bases this interpretation on a reading of the clause's second sentence, which states that the tax is to be assessed "on the salt that has passed through the warehouse," a phrase that, in the kadi's view, implies movement, production, the flow of salt through the warehouse rather than the static amount present on a given day. The defterdar's office, represented by Ahmed, the deputy, reads the same phrase as a reference to the inventory process itself, the "passing through" being the physical act of the inspector counting and weighing the salt, after which the tax is assessed on what has been found. You are not asked to decide between these interpretations. Your role, as stated in your instructions, is to record the quantity found and submit the register. The interpretation is a matter for the kadi and the defterdar to resolve, perhaps through the mufti, perhaps through the imperial divan, perhaps, as has happened before in similar disputes, through a compromise in which both parties agree to a calculation that satisfies neither, which is then recorded in the register as the basis for that year's tax and cited in subsequent years as a precedent, even though it was never formally adjudicated. This, you are beginning to understand, is how most things are resolved.

### CHUNK audio-02-04
<!-- img: IMG-02-04 -->

There is also the matter of the exemptions. The Kanunname, in its ninth clause, lists three categories of salt that are exempt from the tax, or, more precisely, three categories of salt for which the tax is waived and not collected, a distinction that, in practice, amounts to the same thing but which the defterdar's office insists on maintaining in the register because the imperial treasury, in its accounting, treats waived taxes and exempt salt as different entries, the former being salt that would normally be taxed but for which the tax has been forgiven by a specific act, and the latter being salt that has never been subject to the tax by virtue of its classification. The three categories are: first, salt produced for the kitchens of charitable foundations, or vakıf, which are religious endowments that operate soup kitchens, mosques, and schools, and which, under the Kanunname, are entitled to receive salt at no charge from the nearest imperial warehouse, up to a limit of fifty kile per year per foundation, a limit that, according to the deputy defterdar, has been exceeded in at least two of the four vakıf within the sanjak, though the kadi's representative disputes this, arguing that the limit applies to each foundation individually and not to the total across all foundations, an interpretation that, if correct, would mean that each of the four vakıf is entitled to its own fifty kile, for a total of two hundred, rather than a shared fifty, and that the apparent excess is in fact within the limit. The second category of exempt salt is salt used in the preparation of leather, which is classified as an industrial use and taxed at a reduced rate of one akçe per kile regardless of quality, rather than being exempt entirely, though the Kanunname's language is, in this case, ambiguous, as the relevant clause uses the word "exempt" in its first sentence and "reduced" in its second, a contradiction that has been noted in the margins of at least three copies of the Kanunname held in the kadi's archives, each with a different annotation, one of which, written in a hand you do not recognize, simply says "clarify," which no one has done.

The third category of exempt salt is salt that has been spoilt, that is, salt that has become unusable due to excessive moisture, contamination, or other causes, and which is recorded in the register as "spoilt, not taxable" and removed from the warehouse at the warehouse keeper's expense, because the Kanunname holds the keeper responsible for the condition of the salt under his care, a responsibility that, as Mustafa informs you when you ask, he accepts, as all warehouse keepers accept it, because the position is well-paid and the spoilage rate in Eskisehir is low, typically no more than two or three sacks per year, which he attributes to the stone construction of the warehouse and the relatively dry climate, though he adds, in a comment that you record in your notes but not in the register, that three years ago, before his appointment, the previous keeper lost thirty-seven sacks to a flood caused by a burst irrigation channel upstream, an event that was deemed an act of God and for which the keeper was not held responsible, though he was reassigned to a different warehouse in a different sanjak, which, if it was a punishment, was a mild one. You ask Mustafa how many sacks have been spoilt this year. He says four. You ask to see them. He leads you to a corner of the warehouse, behind the eastern wall sacks, where four sacks sit apart from the others, their fabric dark with moisture, their contents, when you open one and inspect it, a grey-white mass that clumps when pressed and emits a faint smell that is not the clean mineral smell of the dry salt but something earthier, like wet stone. You close the sack. You record: four sacks, spoilt, third-quality classification pending verification. The verification of spoilage, according to the Kanunname, requires that a sample be dissolved in water and the residue weighed, the same test used for determining quality classification, and the result recorded in the register alongside the inspector's determination. You will perform this test tomorrow, after you have completed the count at the next warehouse.

### CHUNK audio-02-06
<!-- img: IMG-02-06 -->

The dispute between the kadi and the defterdar extends, as you learn over the course of several conversations held in the corridor between their two offices, to the matter of the salt sold during the year, which, depending on which calculation method is adopted, either reduces the taxable amount, if the tax is based on the inventory, or is irrelevant, if the tax is based on production, because the salt has already been produced and the tax has already been assessed on it at the time of production, or at least it would have been, had the production figures been recorded accurately, which the defterdar's office claims they have not been, a claim that the kadi's representative rejects by producing, from a cabinet in the kadi's office, a register of production figures for the past seven years, each year's entry signed by the salt works overseer and countersigned by the kadi's clerk, a document that, the representative says, proves the production figures are reliable and have been consistently recorded. Ahmed, the deputy defterdar, examines the register, or rather, he examines the copy of it that the kadi's representative allows him to see, because the original, the representative says, is too valuable to be removed from the archive, and after examining it for several minutes, during which he turns the pages slowly and makes no notes, he says that the figures appear consistent with the defterdar's own records for the past three years, but that he cannot speak for the four years before that, because the defterdar's records for those years are, as he puts it, in transit, by which he means, as you understand from the context and from the slight pause before the phrase, that they have been lost or misplaced, and that the office is attempting to recover them from the imperial archives in Edirne, a process that, as mentioned earlier, can take several years. The dispute, therefore, cannot be resolved by reference to the production figures, because one side's figures are incomplete and the other side's figures are, according to the first side, unreliable, and there is no third set of figures, a situation that, you reflect, is not uncommon in administrative disputes of this kind, and which usually results, as you have also observed, in the adoption of whichever method produces the lower tax, because the lower tax is easier to collect and generates fewer complaints, and because the imperial treasury, in practice, is more concerned with the steady collection of a predictable amount than with the theoretical maximum, a preference that is not stated in any Kanunname but which is understood by everyone involved.

On the seventh day, you visit the second warehouse, which is located in the village of Kaymazz, approximately two hours' ride to the south of Eskisehir, along a road that, as you ride, you note is in need of repair, with several sections where the paving stones have shifted and one where a small bridge over a seasonal stream shows signs of settling on its western side, a condition that, if you were inspecting roads rather than salt, you would record in a separate register, but which, since you are not, you note only in passing, as it affects the speed of your journey and the comfort of your ride, which is not relevant to the inventory but which occupies your attention during the two hours that it takes to reach the village. The Kaymazz warehouse is smaller than the Isketder warehouse, and its keeper, a woman named Fatima, daughter of Iskender, who inherited the position from her father upon his death two years ago, a succession that, according to the register, was approved by the kadi's court on the grounds that the position of warehouse keeper is, in this sanjak, a hereditary timar of a type known as a hizmet timarı, which is granted in exchange for the service of maintaining the warehouse and which, unlike a military timar, can be inherited by a woman, provided she performs the duties herself or appoints a deputy, which Fatima has done, her deputy being her husband, a man named Ömer, who you do not meet because he is away in the town, attending to a personal matter that Fatima does not specify. The Kaymazz warehouse contains four hundred and thirty-one sacks and one hundred and ninety-three kile of loose salt. You begin the count.

The count at Kaymazz proceeds without the anomalies you found at Isketder, or rather, it proceeds with different anomalies. The total number of sacks, when you finish the count on the morning of the eighth day, is four hundred and twenty-seven, four fewer than the register indicates, and Fatima, when you ask about the discrepancy, explains that three sacks were sold in the month of Jumada al-Ula to a merchant from Bursa, and one sack was condemned as spoilt after a roof leak allowed rainwater to seep through a crack in the stone ceiling above the northeastern corner of the warehouse, a crack that, she says, has since been repaired by a mason whom she hired at her own expense, an expense for which she has not yet been reimbursed by the defterdar's office, though she has submitted the relevant receipts, which she shows you, four receipts in all, each bearing the mason's mark and the date of the repair, the latest of which is dated the nineteenth of Jumada al-Thani, approximately three weeks after the leak was discovered. The receipts appear to be in order. You note the four missing sacks and their explanations, and you move on to the weighing. The samples from the Kaymazz warehouse are, on the whole, closer to the standard weight than those from Isketder, with only one sack, number nineteen, falling outside the tolerance, at four dirhem underweight, and none falling outside on the overweight side. The complete weighing of sack nineteen reveals a shortfall of one hundred and sixty-three dirhem, which is, by the Kanunname's definition, a significant shortfall and must be reported. Fatima, when informed of this, says nothing, which you interpret as acceptance, though it may also be, you consider, a practiced silence, the kind that comes from someone who has been through an inventory before and knows that the report will be filed and that nothing will come of it for several months, if it comes of it at all, because the wheels of the imperial administration turn slowly, and a shortfall of one hundred and sixty-three dirhem in a single sack in a warehouse in Kaymazz is not, by any reasonable measure, a priority for the defterdar of the sanjak, who has, according to Ahmed, approximately three hundred and forty such reports on his desk at any given time, each requiring a response that must be drafted, reviewed, signed, and sent, a process that, for each report, takes an average of, in Ahmed's estimation, two to three months, which means that the response to the report on sack nineteen will arrive, if it arrives at all, sometime around the middle of Sha'ban, or possibly Ramadan, by which time the current year's inventory will already be underway.

The exemption for the vakıf foundations, which you encountered at Isketder, also applies at Kaymazz, but with an additional complication. The Kaymazz warehouse supplies salt to two vakıf: the Imaret of Gazi Turgud, which operates a soup kitchen in the center of the village and which is entitled, under the Kanunname, to thirty-seven kile per year, and the Zaviye of Sheikh Sinan, a small lodge for traveling dervishes located on the hill above the village, which is entitled to twelve kile per year. The total exemption for this warehouse, therefore, is forty-nine kile, which is below the fifty-kile limit and therefore, according to the kadi's interpretation, not in excess, and, according to the defterdar's interpretation, not in excess either, because the dispute over whether the limit applies per foundation or per warehouse has not, as you have noted, been resolved, and in the absence of a resolution, both parties have agreed, for the purposes of this year's inventory, to apply the limit per warehouse, a compromise that satisfies neither but allows the inventory to proceed, which is, as you have observed, the primary purpose of most compromises in administrative matters. The salt for the vakıf is stored separately, in a partitioned section near the eastern wall, behind a wooden gate that is secured with a rope rather than a lead seal, a fact that you note in the register under the heading "security" and which Fatima explains by saying that the vakıf salt is removed in small quantities throughout the year, as the foundations need it, and that it would be impractical to break and replace a lead seal each time, an explanation that is reasonable and that you accept, noting in the register that the vakıf section is secured by rope, not lead seal, and that the quantity within should be verified independently. You verify it. Thirty-seven kile for the Imaret, of which approximately twenty-two kile remain, the remainder having been distributed over the course of the year, and twelve kile for the Zaviye, of which approximately eight kile remain. The numbers are consistent with the distribution records that Fatima provides, which are kept on a separate sheet, not in the main register, and which show, for each distribution, the date, the quantity, and the signature or mark of the person who received the salt. The signatures, where they are marks rather than names, are witnessed by two people, as required by the Kanunname for distributions to vakıf. Everything appears to be in order. You close the register for this warehouse and prepare to ride to the third.
