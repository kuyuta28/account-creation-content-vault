### CHUNK audio-01-01
<!-- img: IMG-01-01 -->

The counting, as prescribed by the new instructions, must proceed from the door inward, beginning with the left-hand stack, then the right-hand stack, then the sacks along the eastern wall, then the sacks along the western wall, then any sacks stored in the rear alcove, if one exists. This order, which differs from last year's instructions only in the position of the western wall sacks, which were previously counted before the eastern wall sacks, is the one exception mentioned earlier, the fourth clause of the new instructions, and you have not been given a reason for the change. The order of counting does not, in theory, affect the total, but the change was made, and you follow it, because that is what the instructions require. You begin with the left-hand stack. It contains, according to last year's register, six hundred and twelve sacks, of which four hundred and three are first-quality salt, one hundred and eighty-nine are second-quality, and twenty are third-quality, a category reserved for salt that has absorbed excessive moisture or contains visible impurities, such as bits of clay or small stones from the extraction site. Third-quality salt, as defined in the Kanunname of eight hundred and ninety-four, issued by Sultan Bayezid in the third year of his reign, is salt that, when dissolved in water, leaves a residue exceeding one part in forty by weight, though the practical application of this test, which requires a scale accurate to a quarter of a dirhem and a vessel of known volume, has been the subject of disagreement between the warehouse keepers and the inspectors for as long as anyone in the sanjak can remember. You count the first row. Eleven sacks.

### CHUNK audio-01-02

Eleven sacks in the first row. You record this in your register, in the column headed "row count," which you have drawn yourself, since the printed forms provided by the defterdar's office have not yet arrived, and you were told in Edirne, when you received your appointment, that the forms would be sent by courier within ten days, a period that has now elapsed without arrival, and which, according to the clerk at the defterdar's office whom you spoke to three days ago, has been extended by another ten days due to a shortage of paper at the imperial chancery, a shortage that has affected several sanjaks in Anatolia this year and is, by some accounts, related to the disruption of paper shipments from the mills at Amasya following floods in the autumn, though this has not been confirmed. You use your own form. The column you have drawn is adequate. The second row also contains eleven sacks. The third row contains ten. You note the discrepancy. A row of eleven followed by a row of ten may be intentional, if the stack tapers toward the top, or it may indicate a sack that has been removed since last year's count without being recorded. You cannot determine which until you have completed the full count and compared it to the register. You continue. The fourth row: eleven. The fifth: eleven. The sixth: nine. Another discrepancy. Two sacks short from what a full row would contain. You note this as well. The stack, when you have counted every row, totals five hundred and ninety-eight sacks, which is fourteen fewer than the six hundred and twelve recorded in last year's register. The missing sacks, if they are missing and not merely miscounted, will need to be accounted for. But the accounting comes later. First, you must finish counting the warehouse.

### CHUNK audio-01-03

You move to the right-hand stack. This stack, shorter than the left, is recorded as containing four hundred and nineteen sacks. The air here is slightly warmer, or perhaps it is your imagination, as the right-hand stack is closer to the wall, and the wall, being south-facing, receives more sun through the narrow windows above. The sacks themselves are similar in appearance to those on the left — coarse woven fabric, pale where the salt has leeched through, darker at the seams where the stitching holds. You begin counting. The first row: twelve sacks, a number different from the left-hand stack's first row, which is not unusual, as the two stacks were assembled at different times and by different crews of laborers, who, according to the warehouse keeper's notes from last year, were supervised by two different foremen, one of whom, a man named Yakub, preferred rows of eleven, while the other, whose name is not recorded in the notes but who is referred to only as "the other foreman," arranged his rows in twelves, a difference in method that has no bearing on the total count but which, when noticed, can cause a brief confusion in the inspector who expects consistency and finds none. You count the second row: twelve. The third: twelve. The fourth: eleven. The fifth: ten. The stack narrows. You finish the count. Four hundred and eleven sacks. Eight fewer than the register indicates. You write this down. The total discrepancy so far: twenty-two sacks.

### CHUNK audio-01-04
<!-- img: IMG-01-04 -->

The warehouse keeper arrives at midday, as Hasan had predicted. He is a small man with a trimmed beard and a stain of salt on the left sleeve of his coat, which he does not appear to have noticed. He introduces himself as Mustafa, son of Ibrahim, and presents his own register, the one you found on the table earlier. You ask him about the discrepancy in the left-hand stack, fourteen sacks fewer than last year's count. He listens. He nods. He says that fourteen sacks were transferred to the Kaymazz warehouse in the month of Safar, on the orders of the defterdar's office, for distribution to the nüzul supplies being assembled for the imperial campaign, though which campaign he does not specify, and you do not ask, because the purpose of the transfer is not relevant to the count, only the fact of it, and whether the transfer was recorded. He shows you the entry in his own register: on the ninth of Safar, fourteen sacks, first-quality, transferred to Kaymazz, authorization number three hundred and seventy-one, signed by the deputy defterdar. You compare this to your own register. The transfer is noted in the appendix, on page thirty-seven, under outgoing shipments, with the same authorization number. The numbers agree. The fourteen sacks are accounted for. You cross out the discrepancy and write "transferred, verified" in the margin. The total discrepancy returns to eight, from the right-hand stack. Mustafa says that eight sacks from the right-hand stack were sold in the month of Rabi' al-Awwal to a merchant named Hacı Ali, who holds a valid purchase license issued by the kadi's court. The sale, he says, is recorded in his register. You check. It is recorded. On the fourteenth of Rabi' al-Awwal, eight sacks, second-quality, sold to Hacı Ali son of Mehmed, license number one hundred and twelve, price three hundred and twenty akçe per sack. The price is consistent with the rate established by the Kanunname for second-quality salt in this sanjak. You verify the license number with the kadi's register, which Mustafa produces from a cabinet near the table. License one hundred and twelve is valid. The eight sacks are accounted for. You write "sold, verified" in the margin. The discrepancy is now zero.

### CHUNK audio-01-05
<!-- img: IMG-01-05 -->

The counting of the sacks along the eastern wall proceeds without incident. Two hundred and thirty-seven sacks, as recorded, two hundred and thirty-seven counted, divided among the three qualities in approximately the same proportions as last year. The proportions, you note, are not identical. Last year's register lists one hundred and forty-one first-quality, seventy-nine second-quality, and seventeen third-quality. Your count yields one hundred and forty-two first-quality, seventy-eight second-quality, and seventeen third-quality. One sack has been reclassified from second-quality to first-quality, or a first-quality sack was counted as second-quality last year, or a second-quality sack was erroneously placed in the first-quality section at some point during the year. Any of these explanations is possible. The Kanunname does not prescribe a procedure for resolving discrepancies in quality classification between years, and the practice, as you have observed in your three previous assignments, varies by sanjak. Some inspectors reclassify to match the current count. Others note the discrepancy and move on. You note the discrepancy and move on. The western wall sacks, counted next according to the new order prescribed in the fourth clause, total three hundred and fifty-nine, which matches the register exactly, a rare occurrence that you note with a brief check mark rather than the cross you have been using to indicate verified quantities. There is no rear alcove. You confirm this by walking to the back of the warehouse and touching the wall with your hand. It is solid stone, cold, slightly damp at the base where the floor meets the wall. No alcove. You return to the table.

### CHUNK audio-01-06
<!-- img: IMG-01-06 -->

The weighing of samples, which you begin on the second day, is a process that the Kanunname devotes three full clauses to, more than it devotes to any other single procedure in the inventory. The reason for this emphasis, which is not explained in the text of the Kanunname itself but which is widely understood among inspectors, is that the weight of a sack of salt is not fixed. A sack may gain weight if it absorbs moisture from the air, which it does, slowly, during the humid months, and it may lose weight if the salt dries and fine particles escape through the weave of the fabric during the dry months. The Kanunname of eight hundred and ninety-four specifies that a sample must be taken from one sack in every ten, using the following method: the sack is to be opened, a quantity of salt equivalent to one kile is to be removed using a standard measure, this quantity is to be weighed on a scale certified by the defterdar's office, and the result is to be recorded alongside the sack's assigned number in the register. If the weight of the sample deviates from the standard weight of one kile of salt, as established by the same Kanunname, by more than three dirhem, the sack is to be set aside for a complete weighing of its entire contents, a procedure that is time-consuming and is therefore to be avoided unless necessary. The standard weight of one kile of salt, as established by the Kanunname, is three hundred and sixty dirhem, which, for the purposes of the register, is recorded as one kile, without conversion, because the register operates in kile and fractions of kile, not in dirhem, though the weighing itself is conducted in dirhem and then converted, a conversion that introduces, according to some inspectors, a small but systematic error, though this has never been formally investigated. You set up the scale near the table. Mustafa brings the standard measure, a wooden box with a metal rim, stamped with the defterdar's seal. You verify the seal. It matches the description in your instructions. You begin with the first sack in the left-hand stack.

### CHUNK audio-01-07
<!-- img: IMG-01-07 -->

The first sack yields a sample weighing three hundred and sixty-two dirhem, two dirhem above the standard, which is within the tolerance of three dirhem and therefore acceptable. You record: sack one, left stack, sample weight three hundred and sixty-two dirhem, within tolerance. The second sack: three hundred and fifty-nine dirhem, one below standard, within tolerance. The third: three hundred and sixty-one. The fourth: three hundred and sixty-three, exactly at the upper limit of the tolerance, which, according to a practice you learned from an older inspector in the Bursa sanjak, should be recorded with a note indicating that the sack is at the boundary, though this note is not required by the Kanunname, which only requires recording the weight and whether it is within or outside tolerance. You add the note anyway. It does no harm. The fifth sack: three hundred and fifty-seven, three below standard, at the lower boundary, and you note this as well. The sixth: three hundred and sixty. The seventh: three hundred and sixty-four, four above standard, outside tolerance. You stop. This is the first sack to fall outside the three-dirhem tolerance. According to the Kanunname, you must now conduct a complete weighing of the seventh sack's entire contents. You inform Mustafa, who nods without expression and sends one of the warehouse laborers to bring the sack to the scale. The complete weighing takes approximately half an hour, as each scoop of salt must be weighed separately and recorded, and the total must be calculated and compared to the expected weight of a full sack. The expected weight of a full sack of first-quality salt, as established by the Kanunname, is eleven kile, or three thousand nine hundred and sixty dirhem. The actual weight of the seventh sack is four thousand one hundred and thirty-seven dirhem, which is one hundred and seventy-seven dirhem above the expected weight. The sack is overweight. This is unusual. Most discrepancies are shortfalls. An overweight sack may indicate that extra salt was added, or that the sack has absorbed significant moisture, or that the original weighing when the sack was filled was inaccurate. You record the result. The Kanunname does not prescribe a specific procedure for overweight sacks, only for underweight ones, which must be reported to the defterdar's office for investigation. You record the overweight finding in a separate column, which you label "excess," and continue.

### CHUNK audio-01-08
<!-- img: IMG-01-08 -->

The remaining sacks in the left-hand stack, sampled at the rate of one in ten, yield weights within the tolerance, with the exception of sack number thirty-one, which is four dirhem underweight, and sack number fifty-three, which is five dirhem overweight, both outside the three-dirhem limit, both requiring complete weighing. Sack thirty-one, upon complete weighing, comes to three thousand eight hundred and twelve dirhem, one hundred and forty-eight dirhem under the expected weight, a shortfall that, according to the Kanunname, must be reported. Sack fifty-three, upon complete weighing, comes to four thousand and ninety-one dirhem, one hundred and thirty-one dirhem over the expected weight, which, as before, you record in the "excess" column. You are now, at the end of the second day, in possession of three anomalies: one overweight sack, one underweight sack, and one overweight sack, all from the left-hand stack alone. The remaining stacks have not yet been sampled. You close your register. The warehouse is dark now. The narrow windows show nothing but the last grey of evening. Mustafa has lit a small oil lamp near the table, and by its light you review the day's entries. Three anomalies in fifty-nine sampled sacks, a rate of approximately one in twenty, which, you recall from a conversation with another inspector in the Kütahya sanjak two years ago, is higher than the usual rate of one in thirty, though he may have been estimating, and you may be misremembering the number. It does not matter. The numbers are recorded. You return to the caravanserai.

### CHUNK audio-01-09

The third day is given over to the checking of the lead seals on each door of the warehouse, of which there are three: the main entrance, which you have been using, the side entrance on the eastern wall, which is sealed and has not been opened, according to Mustafa, since the last inventory, and a small door at the rear, near where you touched the wall yesterday, which is also sealed and which gives access to a shallow storage area beneath the floor, used, Mustafa says, for storing the wooden measures and the certified scale when they are not in use, and which is not, strictly speaking, a warehouse door but is classified as one for the purposes of the seal inventory because it opens from the exterior. The lead seal on the main entrance was broken by Hasan when you arrived on the first day and has since been replaced with a new seal bearing the date of your arrival and your name, which you stamped into the lead using a small iron die that you carry for this purpose. The side entrance seal is intact. You examine it. It bears the date of the previous year's inventory, the seventeenth of Rabi' al-Thani, eight hundred and ninety-eight, and the name of the inspector who conducted it, a man named Süleyman, son of Abdülkerim, whose name you do not recognize but whose stamp is clear and regular. The rear door seal is also intact, with the same date and the same name. You record both. The seals are verified. You also record the condition of each door: the main entrance, iron hinges, functional, no visible damage; the side entrance, wooden frame, slight warping at the upper right corner, noted; the rear door, wood and iron, rust on the lower hinge, noted. The seal inventory for this warehouse is now complete. You turn to the loose salt, the seven hundred and twelve kile that are stored not in sacks but in bulk, in a partitioned section of the warehouse near the eastern wall. This will require a different method of counting. You will need the large measure, the one reserved for bulk salt, and additional laborers to assist with the shoveling. Mustafa says he will arrange for three men to be available in the morning. You nod. You are aware that the bulk counting is the slowest part of the inventory, and that it will likely take two full days, perhaps three if the moisture content is high and the salt clumps, as it sometimes does in the spring, when the ground outside is still soft from the winter rains and the dampness seeps through the stone floor in ways that the Kanunname's authors, writing in Edirne, where the climate is different, may not have anticipated. You close your register for the day. Three warehouses remain after this one. The inventory is proceeding on schedule.
