Day ten. The final report.

You pull a fresh sheet of papyrus from the desk drawer. Someone has placed it there overnight. You do not know who. You do not ask. The papyrus is of good quality, the kind reserved for documents that will travel upward through the administrative hierarchy, documents that will be read by persons of importance, documents that will be stored in archives for longer than you will live.

You mix the ink. The ink is black, from a small pot kept on the desk corner. You do not know who provided the ink. You do not ask. The ink is sufficient. The pen is sharpened. You begin.

The report begins with the date. The year, the month, the day. The governor's name. The province name. The purpose: the annual census processing for the province, submitted by the census office for review.

The body of the report contains the statistics. The statistics are the same statistics you have accumulated over the past nine days, written now in formal administrative style, the numbers spelled out in words rather than figures, the Latin correct for official correspondence. The statistics are: total declarations received, total declarations processed, total population as estimated from household counts, total discrepancies identified, total discrepancies resolved, total discrepancies referred.

You do not include the names. The names are in the declarations, which are stored in the registry shelf, available for review. The report contains only the numbers. The numbers are what the governor requires.

You complete the report. You set the papyrus aside to dry. No spell-check. No grammar-check. No auto-save. If you make an error, you start over. The ink dries slowly. You do not rush it. You have time. The census deadline is not today. Today is only the day of the report. Tomorrow the report will be reviewed. The day after, perhaps, the governor will sign it. The process continues. The process always continues.

You stand. You stretch. The office is quiet. The other clerks have departed. The lamp burns low. The window shows the last light of afternoon fading into evening. The dust motes hang in the light. They have been hanging in the light for ten days. They will be hanging in the light after you are gone. The dust does not care about the census. The dust persists.

### CHUNK audio-04-02 (~320 words)

The report is dry. You roll it carefully, taking care not to smudge the ink. The roll is of good size, compact, the kind that fits easily into a courier's satchel. You place it in the center of your desk. You wait.

The evening courier arrives at the ninth hour. He is a man you have seen before, the same courier who brought the declarations on the first day. He looks at the rolled report. He looks at you. He asks if this is the census report for the year. You say that it is. He asks if it is sealed. It is not yet sealed. You seal it now.

The sealing wax is from a small dish kept on the desk corner. You do not know who provided the sealing wax. You do not ask. The wax is sufficient. You press your personal seal into the wax. The impression is clear: your mark, your identity in the administrative system. The mark will be recognized. The mark will be recorded. The mark will persist longer than you will live.

The courier takes the report. He places it in his satchel. No email. No scanned copy. No PDF attachment. No digital signature. The original travels by horseback. It will take a week to reach the governor's office, assuming the roads are clear, assuming the courier does not fall ill, assuming no bandits, assuming no floods. The process continues. The report will travel to the governor's office. The governor will review it, or delegate its review, or set it aside for a time and review it later. The statistics will be compiled. The tributum assessments will be adjusted. The discrepancies will be pursued, or not pursued. The system will continue.

You gather your working papers. The wax tablet, filled with notations from the past ten days. The bundles of processed declarations, ready for storage in the registry shelf. The previous census records, to be returned to their place. The supplies inventory, to be updated. The red ink pot, empty. The black ink pot, nearly empty.

You do not know what tomorrow will bring. You know only that it will not bring the census. The census is done. The census is always done, and then it begins again. Four years from now, the declarations will arrive again. The piles will form again. The cross-reference will begin again. The discrepancies will be identified again. The report will be written again.

This is the system. This is what the census is. It is not the people. It is the records of the people. You have been processing the records. You will do it again.

You step into the hallway. The doorkeeper nods. You nod back. You depart.
