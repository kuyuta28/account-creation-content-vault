Day four. The declaration processing is complete. You know this because the pile of unchecked declarations is gone. In its place: the two-dot pile (seventeen items), the single-dot pile (items checked but flagged for minor notes), and the checked-and-filed pile, which now contains the remaining seven thousand three hundred and ninety-five declarations, bundled by district, ready for storage.

No spreadsheet. No database. No cloud storage. No backup. No version control. The declarations are on papyrus. If the warehouse burns, they are gone. If the river floods, they are gone. If someone spills wine on them at a banquet, they are gone. You have been trained not to think about this.

Your desk is clearer than it has been in three days. The lamp oil is fresh. The wax tablet has been wiped clean and reused — the old notations no longer needed, their purpose served. You begin the cross-reference.

The previous census records are in the registry shelf, where you left them. Four years ago, the same process was performed by your predecessor. You do not know who your predecessor was. The records do not say. The records say only: census completed, fifth year of the reign of Tiberius Caesar, the governor's name, the province name, the total population as recorded.

The total was different four years ago. You do not yet know how different. You will find out.

You pull the first bundle. Pagus Augustus. The current declarations, freshly processed. The previous declarations, four years old, their papyrus older, their ink more faded, their seals showing the weathering of four years of storage. You lay them side by side. The bundle from four years ago is thinner. Fewer declarations. The population of Pagus Augustus has grown, or at least, more declarations are being submitted. The difference is not necessarily growth. The difference might be better compliance, better enforcement, better access to the declaration process.

You do not know which. You compare only what is in front of you.

The previous census for Pagus Augustus lists one hundred and three declarations. The current census lists one hundred and seven. Four additional declarations. The four might be new households formed since the previous census. They might be households that failed to declare four years ago and are declaring now under threat of penalty. They might be households that have moved from other districts. They might be errors.

You begin the column comparison. This is your method: create two columns, current and previous. For each name in the current declaration, check whether the same name appears in the previous census. Mark matches. Mark non-matches. Investigate non-matches.

You do not investigate yet. You only identify.

The column method takes time. You knew this before you began. You did not fully understand how much time until you were in the middle of it, the bundles spread across your desk, the previous declarations in one stack and the current declarations in another, your wax tablet recording the match status for each entry.

Of the one hundred and seven current declarations from Pagus Augustus, ninety-four find a match in the previous census. Thirteen do not. The thirteen non-matches fall into three categories: households that have moved from other districts (new to this pagus), households that were not declared four years ago (new compliance or evasion), and households that have formed since the previous census through marriage, inheritance, or other division of existing familias.

You do not yet know which category each non-match belongs to. You only know they do not match.

You mark the thirteen with a small check in a circle. Circle-check means: does not match previous, reason unknown.

You move to the previous declarations from Pagus Augustus. Ninety-three find matches in the current census. Ten do not. The cross-reference for Pagus Augustus is complete. You have:

- Non-matched current: 13 (circle-check)

### CHUNK audio-02-03 (~500 words)
<!-- img: IMG-02-03 -->

Day five. Pagus Martius is complete. Pagus Veterensis is half-done. The cross-reference continues.

The tracking of births and deaths is this: the census records do not directly record births and deaths. The census records only what is declared at the time of declaration. The tracking of births and deaths must be inferred from the difference between the current familia count and the previous familia count, adjusted for the known variables of marriages (which add persons to a familia) and relocations (which move persons between familias without changing the total).

For Pagus Martius, you have inferred the following: twelve households have recorded increased familia counts that cannot be explained by relocation. The increases range from one to three persons per household. These are, in the absence of other information, inferred to be births. You do not know if they are accurate births. They might be persons who were missed in the previous census and are now being added. They might be persons from other households who have merged with the declaring household. The inference is not certainty.

You record the inferred births. You do not verify them. Verification is not your task.

In the previous census for Pagus Martius, eight households have recorded decreased familia counts that cannot be explained by relocation. These decreased counts, in the absence of other information, are inferred to be deaths. Some of the decreases are of one person. Some are of two or three. One household has gone from a familia of nine to a familia of two — a reduction of seven persons. You do not know what happened to the seven. You do not investigate. You record the number. You mark it for the governor's review.

The governor will not review it. You know this. The governor receives a summary. The summary contains totals. The summary does not contain individual household anomalies except where the anomaly is so large that it cannot be omitted. A reduction of seven is large. A reduction of one is not.

You continue.

Pagus Veterensis yields its patterns. The district is older, more established, less subject to population movement. The inferred births and deaths are fewer, spread across a longer time period. The previous census records show a population that was already recorded, already known, already incorporated into the tributum system. There is less drama here. There is only the continued record of continued existence.

You find a declaration where the head of household has the same name as the previous census — Gaius Volusius, forty-seven years now, forty-three then — but the property assessment has changed. Four years ago: three iugera of farmland, one olive grove, twelve iugera of grazing land. Now: three iugera of farmland, one olive grove, eleven iugera of grazing land. One iugerum of grazing land has been removed from the assessment.

You do not know why. The declaration does not explain. The change might be accurate — land sold, land subdivided, land lost to flooding or dispute. The change might be error. The change might be evasion.

You mark it with a circle-check and note the discrepancy in your working paper. You will return to this. You always return.

### CHUNK audio-02-04 (~500 words)
<!-- img: IMG-02-04 -->

The afternoon of day five. The tracking of relocations is the most difficult part of the cross-reference. Persons who have moved from one district to another have not necessarily informed either district of their move. They have simply moved. The census declaration system relies on self-reporting, and persons who are evading tributum obligations have little incentive to report their relocation accurately.

You have devised a method for tracking potential relocations: when a name appears in the current census of one district but not in the previous census of any district, and when the same name appears in the previous census of another district, you infer that the person has relocated. This is imperfect. Two persons can share the same name. The inference might be wrong. But it is the best you can do with the information available.

The cross-reference for Pagus Veterensis reveals six potential relocations. Two persons have apparently moved into the district from Pagus Secundus. Four persons have apparently moved out of the district to destinations unknown. You record the names. You note the potential relocations. You do not verify. Verification is not your task.

The deaths are more certain. A household that existed four years ago and does not exist now, where the property has been transferred to another household or remains unclaimed, is most likely a household that has experienced the death of its head without proper succession. The property becomes estate property. The estate property is administered by the governor's office. The tributum liability continues until the property is formally reassigned or the estate is resolved.

You find three such cases in Pagus Veterensis. The property assessments are still on the books. The tributum continues to be assessed. The original declarants are dead. Their successors have not come forward to declare. The system has not registered the change.

You mark these three for the governor's review. These are the ones the governor will not ignore. Three cases of apparent intestate succession. The estate property accumulating. The tributum assessed but potentially uncollectable.

You do not know what happens to the estate property after it is unresolved for too long. That is not your department. Your department is identification. You identify. You record. You mark for review.

The cross-reference continues. The lamp burns lower. The evening approaches.

The names are entered in order. The matches are noted. The non-matches are marked with their respective symbols — circle-check for new entries, triangle-check for absent entries.

You have processed eight of twelve pagi. Pagus Augustus through Pagus Quintus, complete with full cross-reference. Pagus Sextus through Pagus Septimus, declarations processed but cross-reference not yet begun. The remaining four pagi will be processed tomorrow.

The statistics accumulate. Across eight pagi, you have recorded:
- Total current declarations: 2,847
- Total previous declarations (matching districts): 2,691
- Inferred births since previous census: 89
- Inferred deaths since previous census: 67
- Inferred relocations (inbound): 23
- Inferred relocations (outbound): 31
- Non-matching entries requiring investigation: 94

The numbers do not reconcile perfectly. They never do. The census is an approximation. The approximation is good enough for the purposes of tributum assessment, which does not require precision to the individual level but only to the aggregate. The governor's report will show a population of approximately 2,847 households, representing approximately 11,000 to 12,000 persons, depending on the average familia size used for the estimate.

The average familia size is a policy decision. It is not your decision. The governor's office uses a standard multiplier. You do not know what the multiplier is. You do not need to know. You only provide the household count. Someone else does the multiplying.

The cross-reference of Pagus Sextus yields nothing unusual. The district is well-established, compliant, its declarations submitted on time and in proper form. The previous census shows 71 households. The current census shows 74. Three new households formed in four years. The math is clean. The entries match. There are no non-matches requiring investigation.

Pagus Septimus yields more. The district is larger, more complex, its population more mobile. You find twelve non-matches in the current census — names not present in the previous census. You find nine names in the previous census that do not appear in the current census.

You note each one. You mark each one. You will investigate the marked ones when the cross-reference is complete. Tomorrow. There is always tomorrow.

The afternoon of day six. The cross-reference is nearly complete. Pagus Octavus, Pagus Nonus, Pagus Decimus, Pagus Undecimus — four pagi remain. You work through them steadily, the double column method applied to each, the matches and non-matches recorded, the inferred demographic changes noted.

Pagus Octavus is coastal. Its population moves with the trade seasons. You find evidence of this in the non-matches — persons present four years ago who are not present now, persons not present four years ago who are present now. The pattern is consistent with seasonal migration. The pattern is not actionable. Seasonal migrants are difficult to track. They do not stay long enough to establish permanent residence. They do not always declare. The census does not capture them accurately. This is known. This is accepted.

Pagus Nonus is inland, agricultural, stable. The cross-reference is straightforward. Matches line up. Non-matches are few. The inferred births and deaths are within normal ranges. The district is unremarkable in the best possible way.

Pagus Decimus is new. It was not a district four years ago. It has been created from parts of two other pagi since the previous census. There are no previous declarations for Pagus Decimus because Pagus Decimus did not exist. The current declarations for Pagus Decimus are being treated as all-new entries, circle-checked by default. This is irregular. You note the irregularity. You proceed.

Pagus Undecimus is the last. It is also the largest, having absorbed territory from districts that have been merged over the years. The previous declarations are incomplete, stored in bundles that do not quite match the current district boundaries. You do the best you can. You mark the uncertainties. You complete the cross-reference.

### CHUNK audio-02-07 (~500 words)
<!-- img: IMG-02-07 -->

The afternoon of day six continues. The cross-reference has revealed patterns you did not expect.

The pagi near the coast show population increase. The pagi inland show population decrease. This is not surprising — persons move from rural areas to coastal trading towns, seeking employment in the merchant houses, the shipping firms, the warehouses that handle goods from across the Mediterranean. The census does not record why people move. It records only that they have moved.

You infer the movement from the non-matches. In Pagus Octavus, the coastal pagus, you have seventeen non-matches from the previous census — households present four years ago that have not declared in the current census. But you also have twenty-three new households in the current census that were not present four years ago. The net is positive: six additional households. The population of Pagus Octavus has grown.

In Pagus Nonus, the inland agricultural pagus, you have twelve non-matches from the previous census and only seven new households in the current census. The net is negative: five fewer households. The population of Pagus Nonus has declined.

You do not know what this means for the province. You do not know whether population movement is expected or concerning. You do not know whether the governor has policies to address inland depopulation. These are not your questions. Your questions are only: what is the record, and what does the record show.

The record shows movement. The record always shows movement. People are born. People die. People move. The census captures the captures of this, imperfectly, approximately, in the declarations submitted by household heads who may or may not understand their obligations, who may or may not report accurately, who may or may not have time to report at all.

You set the inference aside. You continue.
