### CHUNK audio-01-01 (~500 words)
<!-- img: IMG-01-01 -->

You pull the first declaration from the pile. The papyrus is rough under your fingertips, the edges soft from handling. There is no CTRL+F. There is no search function. There is no spreadsheet with automatic totals. There is only the pile, and you, and the lamp.

The ink is black, applied with a reed pen, the handwriting steady and familiar — the same clerk who wrote the previous census, four years ago, his hand has not changed. No digital records. No backup copies. No way to cross-reference except by hand, one declaration at a time.

The declaration reads: Gaius Volusius, head of familia, forty-three years of age, residing in Pagus Augustus, District Seven. The familia consists of six persons. The property assessment lists three iugera of farmland, one olive grove, twelve iugera of grazing land. The livestock count: four cattle, thirty-one sheep, two horses.

You set this declaration aside. You mark it with a small dot in the margin — this is the system you have devised, a dot means checked, nothing more. No color-coding. No flags. No automatic reminders. You invented this system because you had to invent something, because the system did not provide it.

The unchecked declarations wait in their stack. The pile is not organized by district. It is organized by when the couriers arrived, which is not the same thing.

No sorting algorithm. No filter by pagus. No alphabetical order. No dropdown menu. You reach for the next one.

You reach for the next one. Marcus Aemilius, thirty-one years, Pagus Martius, District Twelve. Familia of four. Two iugera. No olive grove. Four cattle, nine sheep. The ink is slightly smudged where the wax seal was pressed too hard, a small blob of red spreading into the margin. You note this. You do not know what you note it for. It simply seems worth noting.

The room is warm. The oil lamp on your desk has burned for four hours now. The smell is the smell you know from other years in this office: lamp oil, old papyrus, the faint sweetness of resin from the sealing wax, and beneath it all, the particular mustiness of records that have been stored too long in too little ventilation. Your predecessor left a clay cup on the desk. You do not know where your predecessor went. It is not your place to know.

You reach for the third declaration. Lucius Cornelius, twenty-seven years, Pagus Veterensis, District Three. The familia consists of — you pause. The number has been written over, the original mark crossed out with a single horizontal line, the correction written above in the same hand. Seven persons. The original number, beneath the cross-out, is five.

You do not know why the correction was made. You do not know who made it. It could have been the declarant, revising before submission. It could have been the receiving clerk, noticing an inconsistency. It could have been your predecessor, four years ago, preparing these for whatever came next. The correction is not attested. The line is simply there.

You mark this one differently. You mark it with two dots. Two dots means: requires attention. You do not yet know what the attention will be.

The pile has not seemed to diminish. Outside, the afternoon light is changing. The census office closes at the tenth hour. You have four hours remaining.

You reach for the fourth declaration.

### CHUNK audio-01-02 (~500 words)
<!-- img: IMG-01-02 -->

The light has changed. The oil lamp is burning lower. You have processed thirty-one declarations in the first afternoon. Thirty-one out of seven thousand four hundred and twelve. The arithmetic is not favorable. You know this. You have worked in census offices before. You understand the mathematics of large numbers and small offices.

The declarations from Pagus Augustus are complete. District Seven has yielded one hundred and seven declarations. District Twelve has yielded eighty-nine. District Three, the oldest district, has yielded one hundred and forty-three. These numbers are written on a wax tablet beside your lamp, the stylus impressions still fresh enough to smudge if you breathe on them wrong.

You have identified three requiring two-dot attention. The Lucius Cornelius correction. A declaration from a Gaius Septimius where the seal does not match the name — the impression shows a dolphin, but Gaius Septimius is registered under the sign of the eagle. And a third, from a woman named Gaia, where the head of familia is listed as deceased but the property assessment remains unchanged from the previous census, four years ago, as though the land persists without its holder.

You do not know what to do with these yet. You set them in a separate pile. The pile is very small. The pile is not encouraging.

The evening courier arrives at the ninth hour. He brings a sealed packet from the coastal district, Pagus Altanus, where the salt trade makes the population more fluid, people coming and going with the seasons. The packet contains forty-three declarations. You sign for them in the register. Your signature is the same signature you used four years ago, and four years before that. It has not changed. You have not changed. The register has your name in it three times now, each time in the same hand.

You open the packet. The first declaration is from a man you do not know. He is twenty-nine years old. He has a familia of three. He owns no land. He has listed himself as a merchant, operating in the harbor district. His property assessment is zero. His tributum contribution, calculated at two denarii per head of adult male population, is four denarii for the year.

You note the calculation. You do not check it. The mathematics of small numbers is easier than the mathematics of large ones, and you have learned not to trust your own arithmetic when the numbers grow beyond what you can hold in your head without speaking the digits aloud.

You set this declaration in the checked pile. You reach for the next one.

The lamp flickers. The oil is running low. The pile has not diminished. Outside, the street sweepers are finishing their rounds. Tomorrow will be like today. The day after will be like tomorrow. You continue.

### CHUNK audio-01-03 (~500 words)
<!-- img: IMG-01-03 -->

Day two. You arrive before the office opens. The doorkeeper knows you now. He nodded when you passed yesterday. Today he nods again. This is the extent of your social relations in this office: the doorkeeper's nods, the courier's brief acknowledgments, the distant presence of the governor's secretary somewhere in the building, reviewing documents you will never see.

The declarations from yesterday's late delivery have been added to the piles. Pagus Altanus is now represented. Pagus Secundus, the inland district, has sent its batch — seventy-one declarations, all of them in the same hand, a single scribe employed by the district governor to handle all declarations for his jurisdiction. This is irregular. Declarations are supposed to be in the declarant's own hand, or at minimum, attested by a witness. But the paperwork is overwhelming for the smaller districts, and exceptions have been made.

You do not make the exceptions. You only process them.

The pagi system is this: the province is divided into administrative districts, each pagus responsible for its own census declarations. The declarations are collected by local officials — the magistri pagi in the rural areas, the aediles in the towns — and submitted to the provincial census office in batches. The batches arrive on no particular schedule. The roads determine the timing. Rain delays the couriers. The couriers do not care about your processing schedule.

You turn to the pagi in order. This is your method: work through the districts sequentially, from Pagus Augustus through Pagus Undecimus, twelve pagi in total. This way you will not lose track of where you are. This way, when the report is compiled, the governor will be able to see which districts submitted on time and which did not.

Pagus Augustus yielded one hundred and seven. You processed all one hundred and seven. You found one requiring two-dot attention.

Pagus Martius yielded eighty-nine. You found none requiring two-dot attention.

Pagus Veterensis yielded one hundred and forty-three. You found four requiring two-dot attention. One was the Lucius Cornelius correction. One was the seal mismatch. One was the Gaia property question. The fourth is new: a declaration from a Sempronius listing a familia of twelve, but the individual names, when counted, total eleven. The mathematics does not reconcile. Someone is missing. You do not know who.

You mark the Sempronius declaration with two dots. You place it in the two-dot pile. The two-dot pile is growing.

The morning is half gone. The pile has not diminished in proportion to your efforts. You understand this about census work: the work expands to fill the time available. If you had a year, you would find a year's worth of things to check. If you had a day, you would find a day's worth. The declarations do not care about your timeline.

You reach for the next declaration. Pagus Secundus, first of the batch. A farm near the river. A familia of eight. Landholdings spread across three separate plots. The ink is faded, as though the scribe's ink was watered down, or as though the original document was exposed to moisture before it was copied. The assessment is difficult to read. The number might be twelve iugera. It might be twenty iugera. The difference matters.

You do not know what to do with difficult-to-read assessments. You could request clarification from the district. You could estimate based on the property's location relative to known measurements. You could flag it for the governor's review. You choose none of these options. You mark it with a small question mark in the margin. You will return to this later. You always return to everything later.

You continue.

### CHUNK audio-01-04 (~500 words)
<!-- img: IMG-01-04 -->

The afternoon of day two. You have completed two pagi. Pagus Augustus, Pagus Martius, Pagus Veterensis, Pagus Secundus. Four out of twelve. The arithmetic is improving. If you maintain this pace, you will finish in six days. You know you will not maintain this pace. The coastal districts have more declarations. The inland districts have fewer but more complex property arrangements. The pagi near the city have declarations in better handwriting, from clerks who own proper ink and know how to mix it. The remote pagi have declarations in whatever the declarant could manage.

You have just processed a declaration from Pagus Quintus, a remote district in the hills where the soil is thin and the olives grow poorly. The declaration is from a woman named Aurelia, head of familia, sixty-one years of age. She has declared a familia of one. She is alone. The property assessment lists a small house, one iugerum of marginal land, two goats.

You do not know why you remember this declaration. There are seven thousand four hundred and twelve of them. Most of them blur together, the names interchangeable, the numbers forgettable. But Aurelia of Pagus Quintus stays with you. Sixty-one years old. Alone. A house that is too large for one person, land that produces too little to be worth listing. She could have declared herself as part of someone else's familia. She chose not to. For this she pays tributum as a single head of household. The mathematics of this is unfair in a way you can see clearly but cannot express anywhere that matters.

You set Aurelia's declaration in the checked pile. You do not mark it with anything. It requires no two-dot attention. It is simply a person who has been recorded.

The familia count statistics are accumulating on your wax tablet. Through four pagi, you have processed four hundred and ten declarations, covering a recorded population of one thousand seven hundred and twenty-three persons. This is not the actual population. The census always misses some. It misses the people who do not wish to be counted, the people who cannot afford to be counted accurately, the people who die between declaration and processing, the people who are born after the declaration deadline, the people who have moved and not updated their registration, the people who exist in the margins of the system in ways the system does not acknowledge.

You do not think about this. You have been trained not to think about it. The census records what is declared. You check what is declared. You do not investigate what is not declared.

The lamp oil is fresh. The afternoon has several hours remaining. You reach for the next declaration. Pagus Quintus, second of the batch. A man named Quintus Petillius, forty-four years, familia of five, farm of six iugera near the river that runs through the district. The river has flooded twice in the past four years, according to the flood records kept by the aediles. The property assessment has been reduced both times, the reduction noted in the margin with the official's seal.

You check the seal. It matches. The reduction is legitimate. You mark the declaration with a single dot. You continue.

### CHUNK audio-01-05 (~500 words)
<!-- img: IMG-01-05 -->

Day three. The pile from Pagus Quintus is complete. Forty-seven declarations, covering the hill farms and the river settlements. You found three requiring two-dot attention. One is the Aurelia question — though you did not mark it, you have thought about it again. One is a property dispute between two neighbors, each claiming an additional iugerum that the other has listed as theirs. One is a declaration where the declarant appears to have died between the declaration date and the submission date, the seal applied by an heir who did not update the head-of-familial listing.

These things happen. You do not know what happens to the tributum when a declarant dies mid-process. That is not your department. Your department is the processing of what has been declared, and the identification of what requires further attention.

The Pagus Sextus declarations arrive in the morning post. Sixty-three declarations. You process them in order. The handwriting improves as you work through the batch — the district scribe is competent, his reed pen well-maintained, his ink properly mixed. The declarations from Pagus Sextus are a pleasure to process, in the limited sense that any declaration processing can be pleasurable.

You find no two-dot items in Pagus Sextus. You note this. You do not know what it means. Perhaps the district scribe is more careful than others. Perhaps the population of Pagus Sextus is simply more honest. Perhaps the properties are easier to assess, the land less contested, the familiae more stable. You do not investigate. You note.

By midday, Pagus Sextus is complete. By afternoon, Pagus Septimus is half-done. This district is larger — eighty-nine declarations. You work through them steadily. The sun moves across the sky. The lamp is not needed during the daylight hours, when the office windows admit enough light to read by. The windows are small, set high in the walls, designed to admit light without admitting the wind or the dust or the noise of the street. They are the kind of windows that make you feel you are somewhere separate from the world.

Aurelia comes to mind again. You push the thought aside. You are not assigned to feel sympathy. You are assigned to process declarations.

A declaration from a Gaius Memmius. Familia of nine. Property assessed at forty-seven iugera, making him one of the larger landowners in the district. The property is listed as including a villa, two barns, a wine press, and olive press. The livestock count is extensive: fourteen cattle, ninety-three sheep, eight horses, and — you pause at this — one camel.

A camel. In a Roman provincial district. You do not know what a camel is doing in this province. Camels are from the south, from the desert provinces, from places where horses do not thrive. Camels do not belong here. And yet the declaration lists one camel, and the assessment value reflects the presence of unusual livestock.

You mark this with a single dot. You make a note in your wax tablet: Gaius Memmius, Pagus Septimus, unusual livestock item. Camel. Requires no immediate action. Noted for completeness.

You continue. The afternoon continues. The declarations do not end.

### CHUNK audio-01-06 (~500 words)
<!-- img: IMG-01-06 -->

Day three ends. You have completed six pagi. Pagus Augustus through Pagus Sextus and halfway through Pagus Septimus. The arithmetic holds. You are on pace to finish the declaration processing in approximately four more days. After that, the cross-referencing begins. After that, the discrepancies.

The two-dot pile contains seventeen items. Seventeen out of seven thousand four hundred and twelve. This is a small number. You tell yourself this. Two-tenths of one percent. The system is working. The declarations are being processed accurately. The discrepancies are being identified for further attention.

You do not know what further attention means. You do not know who provides it. You know only that your job is to identify, and that someone else's job is to act. The someone else is not your concern.

The evening courier brings a message from the governor's secretary. The governor wishes to know the status of the census processing. The census deadline is approaching. The census deadline is always approaching. There is never a time when the census deadline is not approaching, in the way that winter is always approaching, in the way that the end of the fiscal year is always approaching. You write a brief reply: processing on schedule, six of twelve pagi completed, discrepancies being identified and catalogued.

You do not mention Aurelia. You do not mention the camel. These are details. The governor wants numbers. The numbers are: six of twelve. Discrepancies: seventeen. You provide these. You sign the message. The courier departs.

The office empties. The doorkeeper extinguishes the street lamp and locks the outer door. You remain for a time in the dark, sitting at your desk, the piles of unchecked declarations waiting for tomorrow. The darkness is not complete. A single light remains — a small oil lamp left burning near the registry shelf, where the previous census records are stored. The registry shelf holds the records from four years ago. In four years, these records will be moved to the archive shelf, and new records will take their place. This is how it goes. The census repeats. The records accumulate. The people being counted remain, for the most part, unaware of the process that defines their obligations to the state.

You stand. You gather your wax tablet, your stylus, your personal seal. Tomorrow you will return. Tomorrow the pile will be smaller and the cross-referencing will begin.

You step into the hallway. The doorkeeper nods as you pass. You nod back. This is the extent of your social relations. It is enough.

### CHUNK audio-01-07 (~500 words)

Day four. The declaration processing continues. You have developed a rhythm.

The rhythm is this: you pick up a declaration, you read it, you check it against the format requirements, you mark it with a dot, you set it in the checked pile, you pick up the next declaration. The rhythm does not vary. The rhythm cannot vary. Variation introduces error. Error must be caught. Catching error takes time. The time is not available.

The pagi are being processed in order. Pagus Quartus, Pagus Quintus, Pagus Sextus, Pagus Septimus. You are halfway through Pagus Septimus, the large district near the river, where the farms are established and the declarations are mostly in order. The district scribe here is competent. The declarations are complete. The seals are correct. The property assessments are within expected ranges.

You find, in Pagus Septimus, a declaration from a woman named Gaia Sexta. She is forty-seven years old. She is head of familia. She has a familia of one. She declares a small property: a house, half an iugerum of garden land, two goats. She has no children listed. No spouse listed. She lives alone.

You do not know why you pause at this declaration. You pause at many declarations. But this one stays with you. Gaia Sexta, forty-seven years old, living alone, half an iugerum of garden land, two goats. The declaration does not say anything more. The declaration is only the numbers. The numbers are only the beginning of a life, which the declaration does not contain.

You mark the declaration with a dot. You set it in the checked pile. You continue.

The afternoon continues. The lamp burns. The declarations do not end.

### CHUNK audio-01-08 (~500 words)
<!-- img: IMG-01-08 -->

Day five. The declaration processing is nearly complete.

Pagus Septimus is done. Pagus Octavus, Pagus Nonus, Pagus Decimus, Pagus Undecimus. These are the remaining pagi. You work through them steadily. The rhythm does not vary.

Pagus Octavus is coastal. Its declarations include references to shipping, to harbor fees, to the trade in goods that arrive by sea. The declarants here are different from the inland declarants. They have more property, or they claim more property, or they declare property that includes warehouses and docking facilities and the right to operate in the harbor district. The tributum assessment for harbor property is calculated differently. The assessment is not your concern. The assessment is calculated by the financial office. You only process the declaration.

Pagus Nonus is inland, agricultural, its declarations marked by the particular concerns of farmers: land quality, irrigation rights, the proximity of the nearest road, the availability of labor. The declarants here do not mention shipping. They mention rain, or the lack of rain, or the river that flooded last winter and damaged the bunds along the eastern field. These mentions are not part of the declaration form. They appear in the margins, where the declarant has written more than required. You do not know why. You do not ask. You note the margin comments in your working paper. You do not know why you note them. You note them anyway.

Pagus Decimus and Pagus Undecimus are the last. You complete them by midday. The declaration processing is done. The pile of unchecked declarations is empty. In its place: the two-dot pile, the single-dot pile, the checked-and-filed pile. The two-dot pile contains the items requiring further attention: seventeen items, out of seven thousand four hundred and twelve. The mathematics of this is favorable. The mathematics of this is always favorable. The mathematics of large numbers obscures the individual items that compose it. Seven thousand four hundred and twelve is a number. The seventeen items within that number are persons. The persons are not visible in the mathematics. The persons are only visible when you look at the individual items. You have looked. You have identified. You have marked. You have done your task.

The cross-reference begins tomorrow. Tomorrow the double column. Tomorrow the comparison. Tomorrow the identification of what has changed and what has not changed and what requires further attention.

You set your desk in order. You clean your reed pen. You close the ink pot. You extinguish the lamp. You step into the hallway. The doorkeeper nods. You nod back. You depart.
