### CHUNK audio-00-01 (~320 words)

You arrive at the census office before the doors open. The street is quiet. The stone steps are cold under your sandals. You have been assigned here three days ago, transferred from the regional office, though you did not request the transfer, and you do not know who did. The assignment is a small square of papyrus with the governor's seal, which you folded into your tunic and carried here, the edges already softening from the warmth of your body.

The door is opened by a man you do not know. He is older than you, his tunic stained at the sleeves, his hands carrying the particular ink-mark of a clerk who has worked in this office for many years. He looks at your assignment notice. He nods. He steps aside. He does not introduce himself. He does not ask your name.

The interior of the office is a long room with small windows set high in the walls. The windows admit light but not wind. The light is grey at this hour, morning light, the particular light of a provincial capital in the year five after the beginning of the common era. The room smells of lamp oil and old papyrus and something else, something faintly sweet, which you will later identify as the resin from the sealing wax used on the packets that arrive each day from the districts.

Your desk is in the corner. It is a wooden desk, worn smooth at the surface from years of use, with a single drawer that sticks when you pull it. Inside the drawer: a reed pen that is not yours, a clay cup with someone else's lip marks on the rim, a small knife for sharpening the pen, and nothing else. You do not know who used this desk before you. No one has told you. No one will tell you.

You sit. You wait. No computer. No monitor. No keyboard. No chair that adjusts in five directions. The chair is wooden. The desk is wooden. The drawer sticks. This is how it has been for decades. This is how it will be for decades after you are gone.

The doorkeeper returns to his post. The morning light strengthens. The office fills with the sounds of the street outside, muted by the stone walls, distant, irrelevant. You have been assigned to the census office. You do not yet know what this means.

You will learn.

### CHUNK audio-00-02 (~320 words)

The declarations arrive at the tenth hour. A courier you do not know brings them, wrapped in cloth, sealed with the district mark. He places the bundle on your desk. He does not speak. He departs. The bundle is heavy. You estimate seven thousand, perhaps eight thousand individual declarations, each one a household's report of its members, its property, its obligations.

You cut the twine. The bundle splits. The declarations fan across your desk in no particular order, papyrus sheets of varying quality, some new and white, some old and yellow, some in good handwriting, some in hands that barely hold the pen. There is no CTRL+F. There is no search function. There is no spreadsheet with automatic totals. You do not know who wrote these. You do not need to know. You need only process them.

You pick up the first declaration. The seal is intact. The handwriting is formal, practiced, the hand of someone who writes declarations for a living, or who has written this particular declaration with unusual care. The declaration reads: Lucius Acacius, head of familia, thirty-seven years of age, residing in Pagus Augustus, District Seven. The familia consists of four persons. The property assessment lists two iugera of farmland and an olive grove of one iugerum.

You read the declaration. You set it aside. You pick up the next one.

There are seven thousand four hundred and twelve of them. You do not yet know this number. You will learn it over the coming days, as the arithmetic accumulates, as the piles are sorted and counted and bundled. For now, there is only the first declaration, and then the second, and then the third, and the stack does not seem to diminish.

The lamp is lit. The door is closed. The light does not change for many hours.

You reach for the fourth declaration.
