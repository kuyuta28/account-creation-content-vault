Day seven. The discrepancies begin.

You pull the two-dot pile from declaration processing — seventeen items — and the circle-checked non-matches from cross-reference — one hundred and forty-three items. You combine them. The total is one hundred and sixty items requiring investigation. You do not have time to investigate all one hundred and sixty before the census deadline. You know this. You proceed anyway.

The red ink is on your desk. You have a small pot of cinnabar-based ink, used exclusively for corrections and official notations. The ink is thick. It sticks to the reed pen differently than the black ink you use for processing. It requires more pressure to apply. It dries slowly. It leaves marks that cannot be easily erased.

You open the first two-dot item: Lucius Cornelius, Pagus Veterensis. The familia count correction. Five changed to seven. You pull the previous declaration for Lucius Cornelius from four years ago. The previous declaration shows a familia of five. You do not know why the current declaration shows seven. You do not have the documentation to explain the change. The correction was made before submission. The correction was not attested.

You mark the entry. Red ink, horizontal line, marginal notation: "Familia count discrepancy. Previous: V. Current: VII. Correction unauthenticated." This is all you can do. You do not have the authority to reject the declaration. You do not have the authority to accept it without explanation. You mark it for the governor's review. The governor will decide.

You proceed to the second two-dot item: Gaius Septimius, Pagus Martius. The seal does not match the name. You pull the declaration. The seal impression shows a dolphin. The registered seal for Gaius Septimius, according to the district record, is the eagle. These are different symbols. They might represent different persons — someone using a seal that is not their own, perhaps a family seal borrowed or stolen or inherited without proper notification. They might represent an error — the wrong seal applied to the wrong declaration by mistake.

You examine the seal impression. The dolphin is well-made, the lines clean, the wax properly mixed. The declaration itself is in proper form, the handwriting consistent, the property assessment within expected ranges. It does not look fraudulent. It does not look like a mistake either.

You mark it. Red ink: "Seal mismatch. Registered: eagle. Applied: dolphin. Authenticity uncertain." You place it in the pile for the governor's review.

You continue. The red ink marks accumulate. The discrepancies are categorized as you work: documentation errors, seal mismatches, property disputes, familia count discrepancies, absence of required attestations. Each category requires different handling. Some can be resolved at the provincial level. Some must be referred to the governor. Some, you suspect, will simply persist, marked but unresolved, acknowledged but not addressed.

The morning passes. The red ink pot is half-empty. You have processed twelve discrepancies. You have one hundred and forty-eight remaining.

### CHUNK audio-03-02 (~500 words)
<!-- img: IMG-03-02 -->

The afternoon of day seven. The categories of discrepancy are becoming clear.

You have organized the one hundred and sixty items into five categories:

**Category One: Documentation errors.** Declarations submitted with missing information, incorrect formats, or mathematical inconsistencies that can be corrected from available sources. These are the easiest. A missing witness attestation can sometimes be reconstructed from the district record. A mathematical inconsistency can be checked against the property assessment. These total forty-seven items. You can resolve most of these yourself.

**Category Two: Seal mismatches.** Where the seal applied to the declaration does not match the registered seal for the declarant. These total nineteen items. You do not have the authority to determine whether a seal mismatch represents fraud or error. These must be referred to the governor. You note each one. You proceed.

**Category Three: Property disputes.** Two or more declarations claiming the same piece of land, or boundary inconsistencies between adjacent properties, or assessments that do not match the physical characteristics of the land as described in the district survey. These total twenty-three items. Property disputes are handled by the governor's legal office. You do not adjudicate property disputes. You only identify them.

**Category Four: Familia count discrepancies.** Cases where the declared familia size does not match the previous census, or where corrections have been made without proper authentication, or where persons appear to have been added or removed without explanation. These total thirty-one items. Some of these are explainable — births, deaths, marriages, relocations. Some are not. The unexplained ones must be referred.

**Category Five: Missing declarations.** Cases where a household existed in the previous census but has not submitted a declaration in the current census. These total forty items. Some of these are explainable — the household has moved, the household has dissolved, the household has been merged with another. Some are not. The unexplained ones represent potential tax evasion.

You do not know which category each of the forty missing declarations falls into. You know only that they have not declared. You know only that someone should follow up. Following up is not your task. Compiling the list of those who have not followed up is your task. You have compiled the list.

No email. No phone. No instant messenger. No pigeon post for urgent matters. The only way to follow up is to send a courier on horseback, which costs money, which requires authorization, which takes weeks. By the time the courier arrives, the census deadline has passed. So you do not follow up. You compile the list. You note the names. You hope someone else will do something, somewhere, sometime.

The red ink is running low. You request more from the supplies office. The supplies clerk notes your request. No online order. No next-day delivery. You wait for the clerk to retrieve the ink from the storage room, to note it in the ledger, to hand it to you across the counter. The red ink is issued. You continue.

Day eight. Property boundary disputes.

You pull the twenty-three property disputes from the discrepancy pile. You spread them across your desk. Each one represents a conflict between two declarations, or between a declaration and the district survey, or between a declaration and the physical evidence available from neighboring declarations. Each one requires resolution. You do not resolve them. You prepare them for resolution by others.

The first dispute: Gaius Volusius and Marcus Aemilius, both of Pagus Augustus, both claiming an additional iugerum of land on the eastern boundary of their respective properties. The land in question is small, marginal, probably not worth the dispute. But the declaration system does not allow for ambiguity. Each iugerum must be assigned to someone. The two declarations cannot both be accurate.

You pull the district survey from four years ago. The survey shows the boundary as vague, a stone marker that has been moved or lost or never properly placed. The survey does not resolve the dispute. The survey acknowledges the dispute.

You mark both declarations: "Boundary dispute, eastern iugerum, competing claims." Red ink. You note the survey information. You attach the survey notation to both declarations. You place them in the property dispute pile for the governor's legal office.

The second dispute: a property listed in the previous census as belonging to a Sempronius, now declared by a Numicius. The Numicius claims the property was purchased at auction three years ago, following the intestate death of the Sempronius without heirs. You have the Sempronius declaration from four years ago. The Numicius has provided a bill of sale. The bill of sale is in proper form, witnessed, sealed.

You do not verify the bill of sale. You do not have the means to verify. You accept the documentation and note the chain of title. The property has changed hands. The tributum liability has transferred. The current declaration is consistent with the claimed ownership.

You mark the Numicius declaration with a green check — not a discrepancy, a resolution. The dispute is resolved. The property is Numicius's. The tributum will be assessed accordingly.

You continue through the property disputes. Some are resolved. Some are referred. The desk fills and empties and fills again. The morning passes. The afternoon begins.

The afternoon of day eight. Missing persons and duplicate entries.

You pull the forty missing declarations — households that existed four years ago and have not declared in the current census. You pull the nineteen duplicate entries — names that appear in the current census more than once, in different districts or in the same district under slightly different forms.

The missing declarations are the larger problem. Forty households. You do not know why they have not declared. You will not know, unless someone investigates. You have prepared the investigation list. Names, addresses, previous property assessments. The governor's office will decide whether to pursue. The census deadline is approaching. Pursuing forty missing households will take time. Time is what the census deadline does not allow.

You note each missing declaration. You mark each one in red ink: "Non-filing. Previous status: [recorded/absent]. Investigation recommended." You do not investigate. You note.

The duplicate entries are more tractable. A man named Quintus appears in Pagus Martius and in Pagus Secundus under slightly different names — Quintus Aemilius in Martius, Quintus Aemilius Maximus in Secundus. The property assessments are different. The familia sizes are different. The handwriting on the declarations is identical.

You do not know what this means. It could be two different persons with similar names. It could be one person who has declared twice, in two different districts, perhaps to evade tributum obligations that scale with declared property. It could be an error — the same declaration submitted to two districts by mistake, the copyist's duplication.

You mark the duplicate for investigation. You note the identical handwriting. You note the different property claims. You do not resolve. You prepare for resolution by others.

A woman named Gaia appears in Pagus Quintus, listed as head of familia, declared deceased in the current census. The previous declaration from Pagus Quintus shows Gaia, sixty-one years old, alone, a small house and marginal land. The current census shows her as deceased, with no successor declaration, the property temporarily held by the governor's estate office.

You do not know when Gaia died. You do not know whether she died before or after the declaration deadline. You do not know whether she had heirs who should have declared in her place. You know only that she has been marked deceased, and that her property has not been reassigned.

You mark the entry. You note the estate office involvement. You proceed.

Day nine. The adjustment process.

The adjustment process is this: where a discrepancy can be resolved from available documentation, you resolve it and note the resolution in the margin of the declaration. Where a discrepancy cannot be resolved from available documentation, you refer it to the appropriate authority and note the referral.

You have worked through the discrepancy pile for two days. You have resolved seventy-three items. You have referred fifty-four items. You have thirty-three items remaining.

The resolved items are categorized as follows:

**Property corrections:** Where the declaration contained a mathematical error that could be verified against the property assessment, you have corrected the declaration and noted the correction. These total nineteen items.

**Attestation additions:** Where a declaration was missing a required witness attestation, but the witness could be identified from the district record, you have added the attestation notation. These total twelve items.

**Familia count adjustments:** Where a familia count discrepancy could be explained by documented births, deaths, marriages, or relocations, you have noted the explanation. These total fifteen items. Some of these explanations are inferred — you do not have birth certificates, you have the difference between two numbers. The inference is noted as inference, not fact.

**Chain of title resolutions:** Where property disputes were resolved by documentation of sale, inheritance, or other transfer, you have noted the resolution. These total eight items.

**Duplicate consolidations:** Where duplicate entries were identified as the same person, you have consolidated the entries and noted the consolidation. These total four items.

**Category changes:** Where a declaration was initially categorized as requiring investigation but additional review revealed no discrepancy, you have recategorized the declaration as resolved. These total fifteen items.

The referred items are categorized as follows:

**Seal mismatches requiring investigation:** Nineteen items. Referred to the governor's office for fraud investigation or clarification.

**Property disputes requiring adjudication:** Fifteen items. Referred to the governor's legal office.

**Missing declarations requiring pursuit:** Twelve items. Referred to the governor's enforcement office.

**Familia discrepancies unexplained:** Eight items. Referred to the governor's census office for follow-up with the declarant.

You do not know what will happen to the referred items. You know only that they have been referred. The process continues. The referrals travel through the system. Some will be resolved. Some will not. You do not know the ratio.

You complete the adjustment notation on the last item. The red ink pot is empty. The discrepancy pile is gone. Tomorrow: the final report.

The afternoon of day nine. Referrals and escalations.

You have prepared the referral documents. Each referred item has a summary page, attached to the original declaration, describing the discrepancy, the documentation available, the resolution attempted, and the reason for referral. The summary pages are written in the formal administrative style required for official correspondence. The style is not your style. The style is the style required. You comply.

The referrals are bundled by category. The seal mismatch referrals go to the governor's fraud investigation office. The property dispute referrals go to the governor's legal office. The missing declaration referrals go to the governor's enforcement office. The unexplained familia discrepancies go to the governor's census office. Each bundle is wrapped in cloth, sealed with your personal seal, labeled with the category and count.

You do not know what the enforcement office does with missing declarations. You know only that the enforcement office exists, that it has a budget, that it employs persons whose task is to pursue non-filers. You do not know the success rate. You do not know the cost per declaration recovered. You do not know whether the cost of pursuit exceeds the tributum liability of the non-filers. These are not your questions.

The referrals are ready for dispatch. Tomorrow they will be collected by the governor's courier and distributed to the appropriate offices. The process will continue. The process always continues.

You gather your working papers. The wax tablet, filled with notations from the past nine days. The bundles of processed declarations, ready for storage. The previous census records, to be returned to the registry shelf. The supplies inventory, to be updated with the red ink usage.

You do not know what tomorrow will bring. You know only that it will be the tenth day, and that the census processing is nearly complete. The governor's summary report awaits. The statistics await. The final compilation awaits.

You stand. You stretch. The office is quiet. The other clerks have departed. The lamp burns low. The window shows the last light of afternoon fading into evening. The dust motes hang in the light. You gather your things. Tomorrow: the final report. Tomorrow: the governor's review. Tomorrow: the end, or the beginning of the end, which is not the same thing.
