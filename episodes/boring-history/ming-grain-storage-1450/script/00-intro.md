In the first year of the Jingtai reign period, which is to say the year fourteen hundred and fifty by the Western calendar, the Ministry of Revenue of the Ming dynasty issued a set of administrative directives concerning the inspection of grain storage facilities throughout the empire. These directives, which were transmitted to provincial administrations by means of official courier between the second and fourth months of that year, constituted the forty-third revision of inspection protocols that had been in continuous use, with modifications, since the Hongwu reign period some eighty years earlier.

### CHUNK audio-00-02
<!-- img: IMG-00-02 -->

The subject of this recording is the grain storage inspection system of the Ming dynasty as it existed in the year fourteen hundred and fifty. It is documented in considerable detail in surviving records, including the Da Ming Huidian, the Collected Statutes of the Ming, as well as in various local gazetteers from prefectures and counties that participated in the inspection cycle of that year.

What follows is an account of how grain was stored, measured, classified, and reported upon by the officials responsible for its management. The account will proceed in a methodical manner, beginning with the structure of the administrative hierarchy that oversaw the granary system, and continuing through the technical procedures of inspection, before concluding with a review of the documentation practices applied when losses or deterioration were identified.

### CHUNK audio-00-03
<!-- img: IMG-00-03 -->

The Ming dynasty, at the time under consideration, administered a territory that extended from the northeastern frontier at Liaodong to the southern coastline bordering the sea, and from the western reaches near the Jiayuguan pass to the eastern seaboard. Within this territory, grain storage facilities of various classifications had been established at regular intervals corresponding to the administrative divisions of the empire, which consisted of provinces, prefectures, sub-prefectures, and counties.

The number of granary facilities active in fourteen hundred and fifty is not recorded with complete precision in any single surviving document. Estimates derived from provincial records that have been compiled by later historians suggest a total of somewhere between two hundred and twenty and two hundred and forty-seven major storage complexes, not including smaller auxiliary facilities attached to military garrisons and postal relay stations. For the purposes of this account, the figure of two hundred and thirty will be used, acknowledging that this represents a working approximation rather than a verified count.

Each of these facilities was subject to a cycle of inspections conducted at intervals specified in the relevant administrative codes. The basic inspection cycle, as revised in the forty-third edition of the protocol document referenced above, required that each major granary facility receive no fewer than three formal inspections per year, with additional inspections to be conducted following any incident of flooding, fire, pest infestation, or structural damage to storage buildings.
