# Section 1 — The Jiaozi System (~1400 words)

### CHUNK audio-01-01

The administrative problems that led the Song government to intervene in the private jiaozi system were of several distinct types. The first and most immediately significant was the problem of over-issuance. Several of the commercial houses operating in the jiaozi business had issued certificates in excess of their coin reserves, a practice that created obligations they were unable to fulfill when certificate holders attempted to redeem their deposits. The resulting failures of commercial houses generated financial losses for merchants who held unredeemable certificates and disrupted normal commercial activity in affected areas.

The second problem was one of standardization and fraud. Because the private jiaozi were issued by multiple commercial houses, they differed in their physical format, their terminology, and the authentication marks used to establish their validity. This diversity facilitated forgery, since counterfeit certificates were more difficult to detect when no single standard existed against which they could be compared. Officials in the Sichuan administration had received complaints about circulating forgeries on multiple occasions prior to ten twenty-three, and several prosecutions for counterfeiting appear in the local administrative records of the period.

The third problem was one of geographic scope. The private jiaozi were accepted primarily within Sichuan, and their circulation depended on the credit of individual issuing houses rather than on any governmental authority. This limited their usefulness for interregional trade and made them unsuitable as instruments of government fiscal management.

The Jiaozi Wu established in ten twenty-three operated under the authority of the Fiscal Intendancy of Sichuan, which was the principal fiscal administrative body for the region. The office was staffed by a complement of officials, clerks, and technical workers whose duties encompassed the printing, distribution, accounting, and eventual retirement of jiaozi certificates.

The printing of jiaozi required specialized equipment and trained craftsmen. The certificates were printed from carved wooden blocks onto paper produced specifically for this purpose. The paper used in official jiaozi production was manufactured from a combination of mulberry bark and other fibrous materials, and its production was kept under administrative control to prevent its unauthorized use. The printing blocks were held in the custody of designated officials and were subject to periodic inspection to detect signs of unauthorized reproduction or deterioration.

Each denomination of jiaozi was printed in a distinctive design, and the finished certificates were authenticated by the application of official seals. The seals used for this purpose were distinct from those used in general administrative correspondence and were held under separate custody arrangements specified in the operational regulations of the Jiaozi Wu.

### CHUNK audio-01-03

The circulation period of individual jiaozi certificates was set at two years from the date of issue. At the conclusion of this period, the certificates were to be exchanged by their holders for newly printed certificates of the same value, a process known as currency rotation. The rotation system served several purposes. It allowed the authorities to withdraw deteriorated or damaged certificates from circulation and replace them with fresh ones. It also provided a mechanism for controlling the total volume of certificates in circulation, since the exchange rate between old and new certificates could in principle be adjusted by administrative decision, although the routine practice was exchange at par.

The fee charged for the exchange of old certificates for new ones was set at thirty cash per string of one thousand, which represented a charge of approximately three percent, similar to the fee that private jiaozi houses had previously charged. This fee constituted a source of government revenue and was formally recorded in the fiscal accounts of the Jiaozi Wu. The annual revenue generated by this fee, as documented in surviving fiscal records from the period, varied with the volume of certificates in circulation and the regularity with which holders sought exchange, but it represented a consistent and predictable income for the regional fiscal administration.

The system established in ten twenty-three remained the basic framework for paper currency management in Sichuan throughout the early and middle Song dynasty. Subsequent modifications to the operational regulations of the Jiaozi Wu addressed practical problems as they arose, but the core structure of a government monopoly issuing certificates on a fixed rotation schedule with standardized denominations and centralized printing facilities remained unchanged for several decades following the initial establishment of the office.
