### CHUNK audio-00-01

In the second year of the Tiansheng reign period, which corresponds to the year ten hundred and twenty-three by the Western calendar, the administration of the Song dynasty established a government bureau in the city of Chengdu in Sichuan province for the purpose of issuing printed certificates of monetary value. These certificates, known as jiaozi, had existed in various private forms since approximately the late tenth century, but the establishment of the Jiaozi Wu, the Jiaozi Office, in ten twenty-three marked the first instance in recorded history of a central government assuming direct control over the issuance of paper currency on a systematic basis.

The decision to establish a government monopoly over the issuance of jiaozi was preceded by a period of administrative review lasting approximately three years, during which officials of the Bureau of Revenue and the Fiscal Intendancy of Sichuan examined the existing private jiaozi system and prepared a series of memoranda recommending the steps necessary for its regularization. The memoranda, several of which are preserved in the Song Huiyao Jigao, a collection of Song administrative documents compiled in later centuries, document the reasoning of the officials involved and provide a reasonably detailed account of the administrative problems that the private system had generated.

### CHUNK audio-00-02
<!-- img: IMG-00-02 -->

The private jiaozi system, as it existed prior to ten twenty-three, had developed from the practice of merchants in Sichuan depositing iron coins with established commercial houses and receiving in return a certificate acknowledging the deposit. Sichuan had been using iron coins rather than copper coins since the early decades of the Song dynasty, owing to a shortage of copper in the region, and the weight and bulk of iron coins made large commercial transactions physically inconvenient. A payment of one thousand iron coins, which represented a relatively modest commercial sum, weighed approximately six and a half kilograms. For transactions involving tens of thousands of coins, the logistical burden was substantial.

The commercial houses that issued private jiaozi charged a fee for the service, typically set at three percent of the deposited sum, and the certificates circulated among merchants as a substitute for physical coin. By the end of the tenth century, a small number of well-established commercial houses in Chengdu had come to dominate this business, and the certificates they issued were widely accepted within Sichuan.
