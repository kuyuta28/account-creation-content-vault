Quality classification in the Ming granary system carried direct administrative consequences. The grade assigned to stored grain determined which distributions could be made from a given facility and at what point grain was required to be disposed of rather than held in continued storage. The five quality classifications recognized in the system — superior, good, middling, inferior, and damaged — were not merely descriptive categories but administrative designations with specific procedural consequences for each class.

The criteria for each quality classification were set out in a technical appendix to the granary administration code. This appendix, which ran to some forty-two sections in the version current in fourteen hundred and fifty, specified observable characteristics that an official was expected to assess using the senses of sight, smell, and touch. Instrumental measurement was not employed in the quality assessment process; the code's framers had determined that procedures relying on the trained judgment of qualified officials, combined with standardized descriptive criteria, produced more reliable results than instrument-based approaches, given the variability of available instruments across different regions of the empire.

The visual assessment criteria for grain quality covered color, luster, uniformity of grain size, integrity of the grain surface, and the presence or absence of foreign matter. Superior-grade grain was described as having a color consistent with the natural color of the variety in question, a surface luster indicating adequate but not excessive moisture content, and a uniformity of grain size that did not deviate substantially from the standard expected for the variety. The specific colors associated with each grain variety in superior condition were described in a subsidiary table appended to the technical appendix.

Good-grade grain was described in terms of slight deviations from the superior standard on one or more dimensions, with specific thresholds described for each characteristic. For example, a slight change in surface color toward the yellow-grey range was acceptable in good-grade grain provided that the change affected no more than a specified proportion of the grain in the sample under examination. The proportion threshold was stated in the code as not more than one part in twelve, meaning that if an examiner, when drawing a sample of twelve grains from the delivery or pile under assessment, found one grain showing color change, this was consistent with the good classification.

Middling-grade grain was described as showing more pronounced deviations from the standard on at least two dimensions, or a more severe deviation on one dimension, with thresholds again specified for each characteristic. The code's description of the boundary between good and middling grades acknowledges, in its prefatory remarks, that the distinction is one where the judgment of the individual official plays a significant role, and recommends that, in cases of uncertainty, the lower of the two applicable grades be assigned.

Moisture was among the most carefully scrutinized aspects of grain quality evaluation. Both insufficient and excessive moisture were associated with deterioration in storage. Grain with insufficient moisture was described as prone to brittleness, cracking, and loss of mass during handling, while grain with excessive moisture was described as susceptible to mold growth, fermentation, and heating within the storage pile, all of which could accelerate deterioration.

The assessment of moisture was conducted by touch and by an empirical test described in the code as the pressing test. For the pressing test, the examiner took a small quantity of grain between the thumb and forefinger and applied pressure. The response of the grain to pressure was to be described in terms of resistance, deformation, and audible cracking. Grain of appropriate moisture content was expected to resist pressure without deforming or cracking. Grain with excessive moisture was expected to feel compressible and to leave a slight impression on the skin of the examiner's finger. Grain with insufficient moisture was expected to crack audibly under even light pressure.

These observations were supplemented by a sensory assessment of the grain's surface when handled in bulk. Superior and good-grade grain, when plunged into briefly and withdrawn, was described as leaving the hand dry and without residue. Grain with borderline moisture content was described as leaving the hand with a slight sensation of coolness after withdrawal. Grain with notably excessive moisture was described as leaving visible condensation on the hand or a distinct sensation of dampness.

The assessment of odor was also included in the quality evaluation procedure. Each grain variety was associated with a characteristic natural odor described in summary terms in the technical appendix. Deviations from the natural odor were classified as absent, slight, moderate, or strong. The presence of an odor described as sour or fermented was associated with the inferior or damaged classification depending on intensity. The presence of an odor described as earthy or musty was inconsistent with the superior or good classification and required assignment to middling at best. An odor described as putrid or strongly fetid required assignment to the damaged classification regardless of visual assessment results.

The recording of quality assessment findings in official documents was governed by provisions specifying that the finding be recorded using the prescribed classification term without elaboration, unless the inspecting official wished to add a notation explaining an unusual or borderline finding. Such notations were to be brief and to describe observable facts without speculation as to causes. The notation might state, for example, that grain in a specific storage area had been assigned to the middling classification due to the presence of surface color change affecting approximately one part in nine of the sample examined, and that an odor consistent with early-stage moisture exposure had been detected. This type of notation would allow a reviewing official at a higher level to assess whether the assignment of classification had been consistent with the applicable criteria.

Pest assessment was required in both formal inspections and monthly reports. The primary pests of concern in Ming granary facilities were rodents, specifically rats and mice, and a category of grain-boring insects described collectively in the code as chu, a term that encompassed various species of weevil and moth larvae. The code specified separate assessment and recording procedures for each pest category.

For rodent assessment, the inspecting official was required to examine the floors, walls, and base structures of all storage buildings for evidence of rodent activity. The evidence indicators specified in the code included burrow openings in earthen floors or wall bases, rodent droppings, gnaw marks on wooden structural elements, and the presence of rodent nesting material. The assessment finding was to be recorded as one of three designations: absent, meaning no evidence detected; present-minor, meaning evidence detected but confined to peripheral areas and not indicating active infestation of stored grain; or present-significant, meaning evidence indicating active access by rodents to stored grain areas.

For the insect pest assessment, the procedure was different because the evidence indicators were less readily visible. The inspector was required to take samples from the stored grain, collecting material from at least three points within each storage building: one from the surface of the pile or collection, one from the middle depth, and one from near the base. Each sample was to be examined visually for the presence of live insects, dead insects, larval casings, webbing material, and insect-damaged grain. The quantity of damaged grain present in each sample was to be estimated as a proportion of the total sample, and this proportion was to be recorded in the inspection report.

The threshold levels specified in the code for pest-related quality downgrade were as follows: if insect-damaged grain in a sample exceeded two percent of the sample by count, the entire storage area from which the sample was drawn was to be reclassified from its current quality designation to the next lower classification. If damaged grain exceeded five percent, the area was to be reclassified two classifications lower. If damaged grain exceeded ten percent, the entire lot was to be classified as damaged, regardless of its prior classification. The final threshold admitted no partial exceptions.

These thresholds created an administrative incentive for timely pest control intervention, since a finding of significant insect infestation triggered not only a quality reclassification but also mandatory documentation of the finding in the monthly report for the current period and in the subsequent formal inspection report. Repeated findings of insect infestation at the same facility over consecutive inspection periods were to be specifically noted in communications forwarded to the prefectural office, and facilities with chronic pest problems were subject to additional oversight measures that included unannounced supplementary inspections.

The application of the five-grade classification system in practice raised a number of procedural questions that were addressed over time through supplementary administrative guidance. Some of these questions involved the treatment of mixed-grade lots, meaning situations where different portions of the grain stored in a single building or pile had been assessed as belonging to different quality classifications.

The standard practice for mixed-grade lots, as described in the code, was to segregate the grain into separate storage areas according to classification before recording the quantities of each classification in the inventory register. This segregation was to be completed within five days of the inspection finding that revealed the mixed-grade condition. Grain lots that were inspected and found to be of uniform quality were to be marked with a grade marker, consisting of a slip of prescribed paper bearing the classification designation and the date of assessment, attached to the wall of the storage area at a height of five chi from the floor.

In practice, the complete segregation of mixed-grade grain was not always feasible within the specified time frame, and the code provided for a reporting procedure to be followed when segregation had not been completed by the required date. In such cases, the entire mixed-grade lot was to be recorded at the classification of the lowest-grade portion present, until segregation was completed and confirmed by a subsequent inspection visit. This provision created a significant practical incentive for granary managers to complete segregation promptly, since recording an entire large lot at the inferior classification rather than only the inferior portion had immediate implications for the facility's performance metrics.

The tracking of performance metrics for individual facilities was conducted through a numerical scoring system described in a subsidiary section of the administrative code. Under this system, each facility received an annual score based on the proportion of its stored grain that fell into the superior and good classifications combined, the proportion in the middling and inferior classifications, and the proportion in the damaged classification. Facilities where the superior-and-good proportion exceeded seventy percent of total holdings were designated as first-class facilities and were eligible for favorable notation in the annual performance assessments of their managing officials. Facilities where the damaged proportion exceeded five percent of total holdings were designated as watch-list facilities and were subject to enhanced oversight.

The scoring system was applied uniformly across all facility types and regions, which meant that facilities in humid southern provinces, where moisture-related quality degradation was structurally more prevalent than in northern regions, operated under the same grading expectations as facilities in arid northern provinces. This structural inequity was noted in certain administrative memorials from officials in the Jiangnan and Fujian regions, and the code's supplementary guidance acknowledged that regional climatic factors could be taken into account in the annotation of performance assessments, though no formal adjustment to the numerical thresholds was made.

Quality findings were recorded in official documents according to a format specified in the administrative code with notable precision. The specification of recording format had both a practical dimension, in that standardized formats enabled more efficient review by officials at higher administrative levels, and a legal dimension, in that the format of a document could be relevant in cases where the accuracy of a record was disputed.

The inventory register at a granary facility was structured as a series of entries, each entry corresponding to a specific batch of grain. A batch was defined as a quantity of grain of a single variety and quality classification that had been received or originated as a unit, meaning that it had arrived at the facility at the same time from the same source, or had been identified as a discrete lot in the course of an inspection. Each batch was assigned a reference number using a sequential numbering system that was reset at the beginning of each year.

The entry for each batch in the inventory register consisted of a header line recording the batch reference number, the grain variety, the quality classification, the date on which the batch had entered its current classification status, and the quantity in shi and sheng. Subsequent lines in the entry recorded any changes to the batch's status over time, including quality reclassifications, partial distributions, and any merging with or splitting from other batches. The final line of each entry, when applicable, recorded the disposition of the batch — either distribution, transfer, or disposal — along with the date and authorization document reference for that disposition.

### CHUNK audio-02-10
<!-- img: IMG-02-10 -->

The requirement to record each quality reclassification in the batch entry meant that the history of a given lot of grain could be reconstructed from the inventory register, at least in principle. In practice, the completeness and accuracy of these records varied across facilities, and formal inspections regularly identified cases where reclassifications had been reflected in the current inventory summary but had not been properly documented in the batch entries. Such cases were to be noted in the inspection report and required a corrective entry to be made by the granary director within ten days of the inspection.

The batch entry system also provided the mechanism by which the year-end inspection reconciliation was conducted. During the year-end inspection, the inventorying official was required to verify that all batches currently listed as active in the inventory register were physically present in the facility, and that the quantity and classification of each active batch as recorded was consistent with the physical assessment. Any batch listed as active in the register that could not be physically located, or that was found to be of a substantially different quantity or classification than recorded, was subject to a discrepancy finding that required formal reporting and explanation.

The relationship between volumetric measurement and weight was of practical importance in the granary system because grain was sometimes transported and sold by weight rather than by volume, and the conversion between the two systems of measurement created opportunities for both administrative confusion and deliberate manipulation. The code addressed this issue through provisions specifying standard conversion factors for each grain variety, with the understanding that these factors represented idealized values applicable to grain of the superior or good quality classification at the standard moisture content associated with that classification.

For milled rice, the standard conversion factor specified in the code was one shi of volume equivalent to one hundred and twenty jin of weight. The jin was a unit of weight approximately equivalent to five hundred and ninety-six grams in modern measurement, making the conversion factor approximately equivalent to seventy-one point five kilograms per shi. For unhusked rice, the standard conversion was one shi of volume equivalent to one hundred jin of weight, reflecting the lower density of unhusked grain due to the presence of the husk.

For millet, the standard conversion was one shi equivalent to ninety-five jin. For wheat, it was one shi equivalent to one hundred and fifteen jin. For sorghum, it was one shi equivalent to one hundred and five jin. These conversion values were derived from empirical averages accumulated over the first decades of the dynasty and were understood by officials familiar with granary administration to be approximations that would deviate from any specific measurement in proportion to the deviations of the specific grain from the standard moisture content and variety characteristics on which the averages had been based.

The use of weight as a crosscheck on volumetric measurement was permitted in formal inspections, and some officials employed weighing as a way of verifying the accuracy of volume-based inventory records. If the weight of a sample lot, when converted to equivalent volume using the standard factors, differed from the recorded volume by more than three percent, this discrepancy was to be noted and the underlying cause investigated. Common causes identified in inspection records include variations in husking and milling level, unusual moisture conditions, and in a smaller number of cases, deliberate substitution of heavier non-grain material in storage piles.

The weighing equipment used for these crosschecks consisted of a beam balance of standardized design, with a set of counterweights in denominations that allowed measurements to be made in jin, liang, and qian. The liang was one-sixteenth of a jin, and the qian was one-tenth of a liang. Weighing equipment was subject to the same certification requirements as measuring boxes, and uncertified weighing equipment was not to be used for official measurements. The use of uncertified equipment, if discovered during an inspection, was to be reported as an irregularity in the inspection record.

Grain that remained in storage beyond a certain period was subject to specific protocols governing its reassessment and, if necessary, its disposal. The storage duration threshold that triggered mandatory reassessment varied by grain variety and storage conditions, but the general principle specified in the code was that no grain should remain in active storage for longer than three years without having been physically inspected and its quality classification confirmed within the preceding twelve months.

The rationale for the three-year maximum, as expressed in the prefatory remarks to the relevant section of the code, was that grain held for longer than this period was at substantially elevated risk of quality deterioration, particularly in the southern provinces where climate conditions were less conducive to long-term storage than in the north. The three-year limit was therefore intended as a planning guideline rather than an absolute prohibition, and the code provided for exemptions in cases where supply conditions made it impractical to distribute grain within the standard period.

In practice, the distribution of older grain before the acquisition of new season grain was a management principle explicitly encouraged in the administrative code through a provision known as the first-in-first-out directive. This directive stated that, when grain was to be distributed from a facility holding multiple batches of the same variety, the oldest batch by date of receipt was to be distributed first, unless the quality classification of the older batch was lower than that of a newer batch, in which case the lower-classification batch was to be distributed first regardless of age.

The first-in-first-out directive was also applied at the level of individual batches when a partial distribution was made from a batch. In such cases, the grain was to be drawn from the portion of the batch that had been in storage longest, which in the case of piles was established by the practice of drawing from the outer surface rather than from the center or base of the pile. The physical management implications of this practice were reflected in requirements for the layout of grain piles within storage buildings, which specified that piles be formed with access paths between them sufficient to allow the drawing of grain from any face of any pile.

The reassessment procedure for grain approaching the three-year storage threshold was the same as the quality assessment procedure conducted during formal inspections, with the additional requirement that the reassessment document include a statement of the current age of the batch in years and months since original receipt, and a projection of the expected quality trajectory over the following six months. This projection was not expressed in quantitative terms but as one of three options: expected stable; expected gradual decline; or recommend expedited distribution.

The application of quality standards across the empire was subject to regional variations arising from differences in climate, grain varieties cultivated, and established local practices that predated the dynasty's administrative codes. The code acknowledged these variations in a series of regional supplement sections that specified permitted deviations from the general standards for specific regions and grain types.

For the Jiangnan region, which included the present-day provinces of Jiangsu, Zhejiang, and Anhui, the most significant regional supplement addressed the treatment of grain acquired during the spring planting season, which in this region was characterized by high ambient humidity. The supplement permitted a temporary adjustment in the moisture content threshold for the good-quality classification during the period from the second through the fifth months of the lunar calendar, reflecting the practical impossibility of receiving grain at the standard moisture content during a period when environmental conditions made rapid drying after harvest impractical.

For the Shandong and Hebei regions in the north, the significant supplement addressed the treatment of millet and sorghum acquired late in the growing season. In these regions, harvests conducted during the ninth and tenth months sometimes yielded grain that met visual quality standards but showed elevated quantities of immature grains, which were characterized by smaller size and lower density than mature grains of the same variety. The code's general quality standards did not explicitly address the immature grain issue, and the regional supplement provided that facilities in these regions could classify a proportion of up to eight percent of immature grain as consistent with the good-quality designation for millet and sorghum, provided that the specific proportion was noted in the quality assessment record.

For facilities in the Sichuan region, the relevant supplement addressed the treatment of a variety of glutinous rice that was grown locally but was not among the varieties described in the general code. This supplement specified quality assessment criteria applicable to the glutinous variety, which differed in appearance, odor characteristics, and appropriate moisture content from the non-glutinous varieties described in the main code. The regional supplement for Sichuan glutinous rice was among the most detailed in the collection, running to eleven sections, and had been developed through a process of correspondence between the Ministry of Revenue and the Sichuan provincial administration over a period of approximately fifteen years.

The existence of regional supplements created an additional layer of documentation requirements for facilities in the affected regions, since quality assessments had to note whether the general code or a regional supplement was being applied. Inspections sometimes found that facility staff had applied regional supplement criteria to grain varieties or seasons not covered by the supplement, or had applied general code criteria in situations where a regional supplement was applicable. Such misapplications were subject to correction in subsequent inspection records.

The quality assessment and documentation system described in the preceding sections of this account represented a substantial administrative undertaking. For any given batch of grain passing through the Ming dynasty storage system, the associated documentation might include a receipt record, one or more quality assessment records, entries in the inventory register, any applicable reclassification notations, and ultimately a distribution or disposal record. Over the course of a year, a medium-sized facility processing several hundred shi of grain in and out of storage might generate several hundred individual document entries across its various registers, each of which was subject to review by multiple officials at successive levels of the administrative hierarchy.

The degree to which these documentation requirements were consistently met varied across facilities and administrative periods. Formal inspections regularly identified gaps in documentation, and the record of corrective actions ordered during inspections provides a partial account of the types of irregularities most commonly encountered. Among the most frequent findings were delayed entries in the inventory register, quality assessments recorded without the required sample notation, and batch entries that had not been updated following a distribution from the batch.

These irregularities were generally treated as administrative lapses rather than misconduct, provided they were isolated occurrences without a pattern suggesting deliberate falsification. Corrective entries were ordered, and in some cases a notation was made in the official's personnel file regarding the finding, depending on the severity and frequency of the lapses identified. Repeated lapses at the same facility were grounds for escalated oversight and, in cases where the lapses were found to be associated with actual discrepancies in grain quantities, for referral to the relevant judicial authority.

As already mentioned in the first section of this account, the quality classification system for grain in the Ming dynasty recognized five grades, a structure that had been in continuous use since the Yongle reign period. This classification structure, with its associated documentation requirements, continued to be applied through the remainder of the dynasty's administrative history, though the specific threshold values and procedural details were modified on several occasions by subsequent revisions to the inspection code.
