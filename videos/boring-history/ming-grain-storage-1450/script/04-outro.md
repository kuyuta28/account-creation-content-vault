### CHUNK audio-04-01 (~320 words)
<!-- img: IMG-04-01 -->

Records from the inspection cycle of the first year of the Jingtai reign survive in archives both within China and outside it. The largest and most complete holdings are maintained by the First Historical Archives of China in Beijing, which holds comprehensive documentation from several of the principal grain-producing provinces. Provincial archives and research libraries in China hold supplementary materials that in many cases provide additional detail for specific regions or facilities. Holdings outside China exist at a small number of research libraries in other countries, and in most instances consist of documents that have entered those collections through the historical dispersal of administrative records that affected many Chinese archives over the course of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries.

The survival rates of different document types within these holdings are uneven and follow a pattern that reflects the hierarchical importance assigned to different documents within the administrative system. Annual provincial summary reports, which were submitted to and retained by the Ministry of Revenue in Beijing, have a higher survival rate than facility-level inspection reports, which were produced at more numerous and more dispersed locations. Facility-level inspection reports, in turn, have a higher survival rate than distribution and receipt records, which were created in large quantities and were considered lower in administrative importance. Monthly progress reports, which were the documents most densely produced within the system, have the lowest survival rate of any category examined in this account.

This pattern of differential survival means that the higher-level synthesis documents, which represent the aggregate conclusions of the inspection system, are more accessible to researchers than the underlying transaction-level records from which those conclusions were derived. The practical consequence is that it is generally easier to determine what conclusions the Ming administration drew from its grain inspection system than to independently verify those conclusions by reference to the original data on which they were based.

The total quantity of grain held in Ming dynasty government storage facilities in the year fourteen hundred and fifty is not derivable from any single document. An estimate of the order of magnitude of the total would require aggregating provincial summary figures that are themselves estimates, applying adjustments for known gaps in surviving documentation, and accounting for the fact that recorded quantities reflected conditions at the time of inspection rather than a single uniform date. Scholars who have worked with this documentation estimate the relevant order of magnitude to be several million shi across all major facilities combined, though the specific figure depends significantly on the assumptions applied and the sources consulted.

The inspection records from this period have been examined by scholars working in several fields of Chinese history, including administrative history, economic history, and the history of food systems and agricultural management. They have been cited and discussed in works published in Chinese, Japanese, English, and other European languages, and they form part of the primary source base for accounts of the functioning of the Ming bureaucratic state.

The forty-third revision of the inspection protocol, the version described throughout this account, was replaced by the forty-fourth revision in the third year of the Tianshun reign, approximately nine years after the date of the records under examination. The forty-fourth revision modified the pest assessment procedures in several respects and adjusted certain threshold values. It did not alter the fundamental framework of hierarchical oversight, standardized documentation, and graduated accountability that the forty-third revision had inherited from earlier versions and transmitted to later ones.

That is the account of the Ming dynasty grain storage inspection system as it operated in the year fourteen hundred and fifty. The records remain.
