### CHUNK audio-01-01

Responsibility for grain storage in the Ming dynasty was organized across several levels of government. At the top sat the Ministry of Revenue, known in Chinese as the Hubu, which maintained overall jurisdiction over grain storage policy throughout the empire. The Ministry was divided into fourteen sections, of which the section responsible for granary affairs was called the Cangshu Qingli Si, translated as the Office for the Management of Granary Affairs.

Below the Ministry of Revenue, responsibility for grain storage fell to provincial administrations, each of which maintained its own granary supervisory office. These offices operated under the general direction of the provincial administration commissioner, who was in turn responsible to the Ministry of Revenue in Beijing. The provincial offices were staffed by a complement of officials whose numbers varied by province, ranging from as few as three officials in smaller inland provinces to as many as eleven in the larger and more agriculturally productive provinces of the south.

At the prefectural level, granary inspection was the responsibility of an official designated as the Granary Inspector, a position that was typically filled by a junior official of the seventh rank or lower. The Granary Inspector was responsible for conducting the formal inspections prescribed by the administrative code, for reviewing the monthly reports submitted by individual granary managers, and for compiling the annual summary report that was forwarded to the provincial office at the conclusion of each inspection cycle.

At the county level, the immediate supervision of granary facilities fell to the County Magistrate, known as the Zhixian, who was assisted in granary matters by one or two designated clerical staff. The County Magistrate was personally responsible for certifying the accuracy of granary records and for affixing his seal to inspection reports before they were forwarded to the prefectural office. In cases where the County Magistrate was absent from his post due to illness, official travel, or other reasons specified in administrative regulations, the responsibility for certification was assumed by the Deputy Magistrate, provided that the absence had been formally recorded in the administrative log.

The granary facility itself was managed by a Granary Director, an official of relatively low rank whose primary responsibilities included the day-to-day supervision of storage operations, the maintenance of the facility inventory records, and the coordination of grain movements in and out of storage. Below the Granary Director were a number of clerical staff and laborers whose precise numbers were specified in staffing tables issued by the Ministry of Revenue and revised periodically in accordance with the quantity of grain stored at each facility.

Granary facilities in the Ming dynasty were subject to a formal inspection cycle governed by provisions in the administrative code, which specified the timing, procedures, and documentation requirements for each type of inspection. In the forty-third revision of the inspection protocol, issued in the first year of the Jingtai reign period, the cycle was described as consisting of three principal inspections per year: the spring inspection, the autumn inspection, and the year-end inspection.

The spring inspection was to be conducted between the fifteenth day of the second month and the last day of the third month of the lunar calendar. Its primary purpose was to assess the condition of grain that had been in storage through the winter months and to verify the accuracy of inventory records following any distributions that had been made during the winter period. The spring inspection was also required to include an assessment of the physical condition of storage buildings following any damage that might have occurred during the cold season.

The autumn inspection was to be conducted between the first day of the eighth month and the fifteenth day of the ninth month. It was timed to coincide with the period following the main harvest season, during which newly acquired grain would be entering storage. The autumn inspection therefore included both an assessment of existing stocks and a verification of the quantity and quality of incoming grain before it was formally entered into the inventory records.

<!-- img: IMG-01-04 -->

The year-end inspection was to be conducted during the eleventh and twelfth months of the lunar calendar. It served as the principal basis for the annual summary report that was required to be submitted to the provincial office by no later than the twenty-fifth day of the twelfth month. The year-end inspection was the most comprehensive of the three, requiring a complete physical count of all stored grain, an assessment of the condition of all storage buildings, a review of all transactions recorded during the year, and a reconciliation of any discrepancies between recorded and actual quantities.

In addition to these three principal inspections, the administrative code also provided for supplementary inspections to be conducted in response to specific circumstances. These supplementary inspections were to be initiated within three days of the occurrence of any event that might affect the condition or quantity of stored grain. The events listed in the code as requiring a supplementary inspection included flooding of any storage building or the surrounding grounds, fire in or near any storage building, discovery of evidence of rodent infestation, structural damage to any wall or roof, and any unexplained discrepancy in inventory records identified during routine monthly reporting. The record of supplementary inspections conducted in the first year of Jingtai indicates that forty-seven such inspections were initiated across the empire during that year, of which the largest number, eleven, were attributed to flooding.

### CHUNK audio-01-05

Each granary inspection in the Ming dynasty produced a standardized report form whose design and required contents were specified in the administrative code. The form had developed over several decades from the early years of the dynasty, and by the time of the forty-third revision of the inspection protocol, it had reached a format that remained substantially unchanged for the remainder of the dynasty's administration.

The standard inspection report form consisted of seven sections. The first section contained identifying information including the name and location of the granary facility, the administrative jurisdiction to which it belonged, the name and rank of the inspecting official, and the date of inspection expressed in the reign-year, month, and day format prescribed by the Board of Rites for official documents.

The second section contained a summary of the quantity of grain in storage at the time of inspection, expressed in units of shi and sheng. The shi was the standard unit of grain measurement used in Ming administrative records, equivalent to approximately one hundred and seven liters, though the precise conversion to modern volumetric units is subject to some variation depending on the commodity being measured and the regional standards applied. The sheng was a sub-unit equal to one-tenth of a shi, and was used for recording quantities below one full shi. The third decimal unit, the ge, representing one-tenth of a sheng, was used only in cases where the discrepancy between recorded and measured quantities was small enough to require that level of precision.


---

### CHUNK audio-01-06
<!-- img: IMG-01-06 -->

The third section of the report form contained a classification of stored grain by quality level. The Ming granary system recognized five quality classifications, designated as superior, good, middling, inferior, and damaged. The criteria for assignment to each classification were described in a separate technical appendix to the administrative code, which specified measurable characteristics such as moisture content, color, odor, and the presence or absence of surface mold. Grain in the superior and good classifications was considered suitable for distribution without processing. Grain in the middling classification was considered suitable for distribution following inspection and selection. Grain in the inferior classification was considered marginally suitable and required review by the Granary Inspector before distribution could be authorized. Grain in the damaged classification was not suitable for distribution as food and was subject to separate disposal procedures. Each classification carried a different administrative response.

The fourth, fifth, sixth, and seventh sections of the standard report form covered physical condition of storage buildings, a record of all transactions since the previous inspection, a record of any discrepancies identified, and the certification signatures and official seals of the involved officials, respectively. Each section was to be completed in a specified order, and the form was not considered complete until all seven sections had been filled in and certified. Incomplete forms were to be returned to the originating office for completion before being forwarded to the next administrative level.

Granary buildings varied considerably by location and date of construction. The administrative code specified minimum requirements for dimensions and construction materials that applied to all new construction and major repairs conducted under official auspices. These specifications had been issued in the form of a technical supplement to the primary administrative code and were referenced in inspection report forms through a notation system that indicated whether each building in a given facility met, exceeded, or failed to meet the applicable standards.

The standard granary building specified in the technical supplement was a rectangular structure with interior dimensions of no less than eight zhang in length and four zhang in width, with a minimum interior height of two zhang and five chi at the ridge line, and a minimum height of one zhang and eight chi at the eaves. The zhang was equivalent to approximately three point two meters in modern measurement, making the minimum required interior length approximately twenty-five point six meters and the minimum required width approximately twelve point eight meters. These dimensions were understood as minimum requirements, and larger structures were permitted provided they met all other specifications.

The floor of a standard granary building was required to be elevated above the surrounding ground level by no less than three chi, achieved through a raised platform of compacted earth faced with fired brick. The purpose of this elevation was the prevention of moisture ingress from ground-level flooding and the provision of ventilation beneath the floor surface. Inspection reports were required to note whether the floor elevation of each building met or exceeded the minimum requirement, and any building found to have settled below the minimum threshold as a result of subsidence or erosion was to be flagged for remediation in the following construction season.


---

The walls of standard granary buildings were required to be constructed of fired brick to a minimum thickness of one chi and five cun, which translates to approximately forty-eight centimeters. Walls constructed of rammed earth were permitted in regions where fired brick was not available at reasonable cost, provided that the rammed earth walls met a minimum thickness of two chi. The choice of wall material was to be noted in inspection records, and buildings with rammed earth walls were subject to a supplementary assessment of structural integrity during each inspection.

The roof of a standard granary building was required to consist of fired clay tiles arranged in overlapping rows over a wooden frame of specified dimensions. The roof was to extend beyond the wall line by no less than five cun on all sides to divert rainwater away from the wall base. Any building found to have damaged or missing roof tiles at the time of inspection was to have the deficiency noted, along with an estimate of the quantity of replacement tiles required. The estimate was to be expressed in units of pian, meaning individual tiles, and was to be provided by the granary's maintenance staff based on a physical count of damaged areas.

Grain quantities were measured using equipment whose specifications were prescribed by the Ministry of Revenue. The principal measuring instrument was the wooden measuring box, or liangdou, which came in two standard sizes corresponding to the shi and the sheng respectively. Each granary facility was required to maintain a set of measuring boxes that had been certified by the prefectural office, and recertification was required at intervals of three years or following any inspection finding that suggested inaccuracy.

The certification process for measuring boxes involved comparison against a set of reference standards maintained at the prefectural office. These reference standards were themselves calibrated against standards held at the provincial capital, which were in turn calibrated against the national standards maintained at the Ministry of Revenue in Beijing. The calibration chain thus extended over three administrative levels, and any discrepancy identified at one level was required to be reported upward through the chain until the point of origin of the discrepancy could be established.

The procedure for measuring grain quantities during an inspection involved filling each measuring box to the level of its upper rim, without heaping, and then transferring the contents to a collection vessel. The collection vessel was a large wooden trough of standardized dimensions, and the contents of multiple measuring boxes were accumulated in the trough until the total quantity being measured had been transferred. The number of box-loadings required to achieve any given total was recorded by a tally keeper, who used a system of notched wooden sticks to maintain an accurate count.

<!-- img: IMG-01-10 -->

In cases where grain was stored in large piles on the floor of a storage building rather than in sacks or other containers, the measurement procedure was more complex. The standard approach for pile measurement, described in the technical appendix to the inspection code, involved first determining the approximate shape of the pile and then applying a volumetric calculation formula appropriate to that shape. The most common pile shape in Ming granary facilities was described in the code as the truncated pyramid form, for which the applicable formula involved measuring the length and width of the base, the length and width of the top surface, and the height, and then applying a calculation that the code rendered as a verbal description rather than a mathematical notation.

The accuracy of pile measurement was acknowledged in the code to be lower than the accuracy of box measurement, and a tolerance of two percent was permitted for pile-measured quantities, meaning that a discrepancy between recorded and measured quantities of up to two percent did not require formal notation in the inspection report. Discrepancies exceeding two percent were required to be noted and explained, with the explanation to be certified by both the inspecting official and the granary director.

Beyond the formal inspection cycle, each granary facility was required to submit monthly reports to the prefectural granary office. These were less comprehensive than formal inspection reports and did not require the physical presence of a prefectural-level official. They were prepared by the granary director and reviewed and certified by the county magistrate before being forwarded to the prefectural office.

The monthly report form was shorter than the formal inspection report, containing three sections. The first section recorded any transactions that had occurred during the month, meaning any quantities of grain received into storage or distributed from storage. Receipts were to be recorded with reference to the source of the grain, whether from tax collection, transfer from another granary, or purchase, and the date on which each receipt had been entered into storage. Distributions were to be recorded with reference to the authorized purpose, whether for famine relief, military provisioning, official salaries paid in grain, or other specified categories, and with reference to the authorization document by which the distribution had been approved.

The second section of the monthly report recorded the balance of grain in storage at the end of the reporting month, expressed as a total quantity in shi and sheng, along with a breakdown by grain type if more than one type was stored at the facility. The grain types recognized in Ming granary records included milled rice, unhusked rice, millet, wheat, and sorghum, with milled rice being the predominant commodity in southern facilities and millet and wheat being more common in northern facilities. Facilities storing multiple grain types were required to maintain separate inventory records for each type and to report the quantities of each type separately in all official documents.

The third section of the monthly report contained a notation of any events during the month that might affect the condition of stored grain, including weather events, evidence of pest activity, and any structural concerns with storage buildings. This section also included a statement by the granary director certifying that the facility had been visited personally by the director on at least three occasions during the reported month and that conditions at the time of each visit had been consistent with the general description provided in the report.

Monthly reports were due at the prefectural office by the fifth day of the following month. Late submission was subject to a penalty of twenty copper cash per day of delay, as specified in the administrative code. The county magistrate's office maintained a register of submission dates for monthly reports from all granary facilities within the county, and this register was itself subject to review during formal inspections.

The staffing of granary facilities in the Ming dynasty was governed by tables of organization issued by the Ministry of Revenue and revised periodically in response to changes in the quantity of grain stored at individual facilities. The standard staffing table for a medium-sized facility holding between five hundred and fifteen hundred shi of grain specified a complement of one granary director, two senior clerks, four junior clerks, and a variable number of laborers depending on the volume of transactions at the facility.

The granary director, designated as the Cangguan, was an official appointed through the standard civil service process, typically holding a rank of nine-a or nine-b, which represented the lowest tier of the formal official hierarchy. Unlike higher-ranking officials who were subject to the law of avoidance, which prohibited officials from serving in their home prefecture, granary directors were permitted to serve in their home county, a provision that was justified on the grounds that local knowledge of grain quality and storage conditions was considered beneficial to the effective management of facilities.

Senior clerks were responsible for maintaining the facility's official registers, which consisted of a receipt register, a distribution register, an inventory register, and a maintenance register. Each register was a bound volume of specified dimensions, with pages made from a grade of paper that met standards set by the Board of Revenue. Registers were required to use a standardized notation system, including specified abbreviations for recurring terms. Entries were to be made in ink using a specified brush size, and corrections were made by striking through the incorrect entry with a single horizontal line and writing the correct value above, with a notation of the date on which the correction had been made.

Junior clerks performed the day-to-day physical tasks of the facility, including measuring grain, operating the weighing equipment, supervising laborers, and maintaining the physical condition of the storage buildings. They were also responsible for pest control activities, which included setting traps, applying approved pest deterrents to the walls and floors of storage buildings, and maintaining a record of pest activity indicators.

Laborers were employed on a rotational basis from the surrounding communities, with the number employed at any given time depending on the level of activity at the facility. The compensation paid to laborers was set by the county government at rates reviewed annually. Laborers were required to sign a register upon beginning and ending each day's work, and the labor register was one of the documents subject to review during formal inspections. Any discrepancy between the number of laborers recorded in the labor register and the number independently observed by the inspecting official was to be noted in the inspection report.

The movement of grain to and from storage facilities was governed by a set of transportation and delivery procedures specified in the administrative code. Grain arriving at a facility from tax collection or transfer was required to follow a reception procedure that included an initial quality assessment, a measurement of the delivered quantity, and the issuance of a receipt document to the delivering party.

The initial quality assessment upon receipt was conducted by the senior clerk on duty. This assessment involved a visual examination of a sample drawn from the delivery, an assessment of odor, and a physical examination for the presence of foreign matter, including stones, chaff in excess of specified tolerance levels, and evidence of moisture damage. The results of the initial quality assessment were recorded in the receipt register with reference to the applicable quality classification criteria.

If the initial quality assessment found that the delivered grain met the required quality standard for receipt, the delivery was accepted and the measuring procedure commenced. If the assessment found that the grain did not meet requirements, the delivering party was informed of the specific deficiencies identified, and the grain was either returned to the delivering party or held in a designated holding area pending resolution. The holding area was to be a separate space within the facility that was not in direct contact with the main storage areas, and grain held in this area was to be clearly marked as pending review by attaching a labeled tag to the holding vessel or pile.

The measuring procedure for incoming grain followed the same standards described in the section on inspection measurement procedures. Once the quantity had been determined, it was entered in the receipt register along with the date, the source of the delivery, a reference to the authorization document for the receipt, and the quality classification assigned during the initial assessment. A copy of the receipt document was provided to the delivering party and retained by the facility for a period of three years, after which it was to be transferred to the county archives.

Grain being distributed from a facility was also subject to a departure procedure. The authorized quantity to be distributed was specified in a distribution authorization document, which had to be issued and sealed by an official with appropriate authority before any distribution could commence. The granary director verified that the authorization document was valid and that the quantity authorized for distribution was available in the facility. The actual distribution was measured using the same measuring equipment as was used for incoming grain, and the measured quantity was recorded in the distribution register along with the date, the destination of the distribution, the purpose as specified in the authorization document, and the name of the authorized representative of the receiving party.

The structure of the Ming dynasty grain storage inspection system as it existed in fourteen hundred and fifty represented the product of approximately eighty years of administrative development and revision. The hierarchy of oversight, extending from the Ministry of Revenue through provincial and prefectural offices to the county level and ultimately to the individual granary facility, embodied a set of principles that had been articulated in the founding administrative documents of the dynasty and refined through experience.

The documentation requirements associated with this system were substantial. A single granary facility of medium size was expected to maintain, in any given year, a receipt register, a distribution register, an inventory register, a maintenance register, a labor register, and a set of holding-area records, in addition to three formal inspection reports and twelve monthly reports. Each of these documents was subject to review at multiple levels of the administrative hierarchy, and the accuracy of each document was the personal responsibility of specific officials whose names appeared on the certification statements accompanying each record.

The system described above was not unique to the first year of the Jingtai reign. The same structures and procedures had been in use, with modifications, throughout the preceding Xuande and Zhengtong reigns, and would continue in use, again with modifications, through the subsequent reigns of the dynasty. The documentation of the year fourteen hundred and fifty is notable primarily because it falls within a period for which a relatively large number of surviving records are available, as compared to certain earlier and later periods for which archival losses have been more severe.

As mentioned previously, the total number of granary facilities active in the empire in fourteen hundred and fifty is estimated at approximately two hundred and thirty. The administrative machinery described in this section was applied, with local variations, to all of them. The following section of this account addresses the technical procedures by which grain quality was assessed, classified, and recorded within that administrative framework.
